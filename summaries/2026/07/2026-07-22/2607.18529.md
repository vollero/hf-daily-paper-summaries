# EduPanel: A Three-Agent LLM Judge for Teaching Videos -- Reliability, Complementarity, and Human Trust Calibration

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-22.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.18529) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.18529)


## One-sentence takeaway

EduPanel evaluates teaching videos relative to a target learner using three specialized LLM agents and, on a small curated study, matched median-expert agreement while improving—but not replacing—expert judgment.

## Problem and motivation

Teaching-video quality depends on both multimodal evidence—such as diagrams, pacing, and narration–visual alignment—and learner characteristics such as prior knowledge. Existing automatic judges largely target text or assign learner-independent quality scores. The authors address the need for scalable, rubric-based evaluation that assesses a video’s pedagogical fitness for a specified learner and provides evidence experts can inspect rather than an opaque score (Sections 1–2).

## Approach and contributions

- **Three-agent evaluation:** A content-analyst VLM produces a timestamped content map and flags factual or visual issues; a text-only objective scorer evaluates content dimensions from that report; and a persona-simulator VLM watches the video while role-playing the target learner (Section 3.2; Figure 1).
- **Rubric and conditioning:** Each video is evaluated on 1–5 rubric scores using its course specification and a persona describing grade, attention budget, and present or missing prerequisites. The main study retains 10 of an initial 24 metrics (Section 3.1; Table 2).
- **System-level validation:** The authors study human agreement, modality-specific errors, architecture and input ablations, persona sensitivity, and cross-backbone behavior (Sections 4.1–4.3).
- **Human-workflow evaluation:** Twelve experts rate videos before and after seeing EduPanel feedback; planted errors test whether experts can identify unreliable advice (Sections 3.3, 4.4–4.5).
- **Released assets:** The open-source release reportedly includes the pipeline, prompts, rubric, routing, personas, course requirements, per-pass outputs, planted-error annotations, de-identified ratings, and analysis scripts (Section 3.5; Appendix C).

The evidence supports the authors’ claim that EduPanel can be useful as expert decision support within this curated setting. It does not establish population-level human parity, causal benefits from AI assistance, dependable visual reasoning, or general learner conditioning across diverse personas and educational contexts.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** The adjudicated ground truth was produced by one researcher who consulted AI commentary, so it is not independent validation. AI-free consensus and alternative label rules mitigate but do not eliminate reference uncertainty (Section 6).
- **Author-stated:** The fixed blind-then-assisted sequence lacks a no-AI re-rating control; practice, repeated exposure, or scale familiarization could explain part of the observed improvement (Sections 4.4 and 6).
- **Author-stated:** The main benchmark contains only 12 videos and 10 retained dimensions, making dimension-level findings descriptive and limiting generalization (Section 6).
- **Author-stated:** Persona sensitivity covers only school-grade and first-year-university profiles and uses non-expert human comparisons; pacing conditioning is especially weak (Sections 4.3 and 6).
- **Author-stated:** The multi-agent design offers limited aggregate-accuracy gains over a monolithic call, while the observed modality bias appears backbone-specific (Section 6).
- **Analyst inference:** Selecting metrics based on judge variance may favor dimensions on which the system discriminates well. Appendix D notes that experts never rated the 14 excluded dimensions, so this selection concern cannot be fully tested.
- **Analyst inference:** Cluster-bootstrap intervals based on 12 videos may be unstable; Appendix D itself notes that this cluster count is below the reliably calibrated range, although leave-one-video-out checks preserve the headline directions.
- **Analyst inference:** Reproduction from released outputs is stronger than end-to-end reproducibility because the deployed system uses a closed, pinned model endpoint; the supplied text does not establish future access to that snapshot.

## Open research questions

1. **Does EduPanel causally improve expert decisions?** The sequential design cannot separate assistance from repeated-rating effects. A randomized crossover study with AI-assisted, unassisted re-rating, and order-balanced conditions could estimate the causal effect.

2. **How broadly does learner conditioning generalize?** Only two personas were tested, with weak evidence for pacing. A factorial experiment varying prior knowledge, age, language proficiency, attention constraints, and accessibility needs could compare score changes with expert judgments.

3. **Can visual leniency be reduced without losing multimodal information?** Visual dimensions were least accurate, and Gemini’s bias disappeared on GPT but transcript-only performance collapsed. A controlled comparison of frame sampling, native-video ingestion, explicit visual verification, and specialized vision agents could isolate the source of error.

4. **When does agent decomposition outperform a monolithic judge?** The full pipeline improved variance calibration and auditability but not MAE here. Larger benchmarks could vary video difficulty and rubric complexity while measuring accuracy, calibration, cost, and whether intermediate evidence helps humans catch errors.
