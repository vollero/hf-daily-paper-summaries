# ConsiSpace: Learning Geometric Consistency Matters for Video Spatial Reasoning

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-22.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.17599) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.17599)


## One-sentence takeaway

ConsiSpace combines geometry-controlled video memory with cross-view consistency training, improving spatial-reasoning accuracy while reducing long-video memory and inference costs relative to the reported baselines.

## Problem and motivation

Video spatial reasoning requires models to integrate object locations, scene connectivity, directions, and measurements across long sequences and changing viewpoints. The authors argue that existing multimodal language models remain semantics-centric: they weakly model viewpoint geometry, retain redundant frames, and can produce unstable answers from different views of the same scene.

The identified gap is that geometry has typically been used as an input feature or training signal, rather than as a criterion governing the complete evidence lifecycle—when observations enter memory, how redundant observations are fused, and which evidence is retrieved (Introduction; Related Work).

## Approach and contributions

- **Geometry-Consistent Memory (GCM):** A dual memory separates implicit visual/spatial evidence tokens from explicit camera-pose and depth cues. Geometry gates memory writes, constrains fusion neighborhoods, and filters hierarchical query-conditioned retrieval (Sections 3.2–3.3; Figures 1–2).

- **Compact global and local readout:** The model combines topology and metric summary tokens with sparse Top-K frame evidence and aligned geometry cues. The readout ablation supports using both components rather than either alone (Section 3.3; Table 9).

- **Unified Consistency Self-Supervised Reinforcement Learning (UC-SSRL):** After supervised fine-tuning, paired views of the same scene and question receive answer-distribution, metric-estimate, and topological-distribution consistency rewards. Only LoRA parameters are updated, and no additional human annotations are required (Section 3.4; Appendix 0.B.4).

- **Outdoor instruction data:** The authors construct nuScenes-10K, containing 10,000 automatically generated spatial QA pairs after filtering 22,518 candidates; manual inspection of 1,000 pairs reports 91.6% correctness (Section 4.2; Appendix 0.A; Tables 12–13).

The evidence establishes consistent benchmark gains, useful component ablations, and better measured scaling under the reported hardware and frame budgets. It does not establish that consistency alone causes correct geometric reasoning: UC-SSRL starts from an SFT model, and agreement across views can occur even when both predictions are wrong. Nor does next-waypoint prediction demonstrate successful end-to-end embodied navigation.

## Limitations and validity risks

**Author-stated limitations:**

- Predicted pose and depth can fail under motion blur, reflective surfaces, and textureless regions, affecting every GCM operation (Appendix 0.D).
- Memory control relies on fixed thresholds, although the tested local ranges show modest sensitivity (Tables 10–11; Appendix 0.D).
- Extremely long videos may require stronger compression or learned eviction to keep computation bounded (Appendix 0.D).

**Analyst inferences:**

- Cross-view agreement is not a correctness guarantee because the rewards can reinforce consistently incorrect answers. The paper mitigates this by initializing from SFT, but does not report an explicit failure analysis of confident agreement on wrong outputs.
- Evaluation is concentrated on three spatial-QA benchmarks and next-waypoint prediction; transfer to end-to-end control or substantially longer videos remains untested.
- nuScenes-10K contains residual label risk: manual verification found 91.6% correctness, with ambiguity, occlusion, annotation sparsity, and incorrect answers accounting for the remainder (Table 13).
- The supplied text gives reward formulas and broad optimization settings but not enough policy-gradient detail to reconstruct UC-SSRL exactly.
- The abstract’s **12.6-point average improvement over the strongest baselines** is asserted, but the precise averaging procedure is not specified in the supplied text.

## Open research questions

1. **Can consistency training distinguish stable correctness from stable error?**  
   This remains open because the rewards primarily measure agreement. A next experiment could compare consistency-only rewards with geometry- or ground-truth-calibrated rewards and report both cross-view agreement and answer accuracy on matched pairs.

2. **Can fixed memory thresholds be replaced by adaptive policies?**  
   The authors identify threshold-based control as a limitation. A plausible experiment would train a scene-conditioned write/fuse/evict policy and compare accuracy, retained entries, and latency across indoor, outdoor, static, and dynamic videos.

3. **How does GCM behave on genuinely extreme-duration or dynamic scenes?**  
   The longest reported efficiency condition uses 200 frames, while the authors note unresolved long-horizon scaling. Testing thousands of frames with controlled revisits, moving objects, and strict memory budgets would expose accumulation and stale-memory failures.

4. **Do the gains transfer to closed-loop navigation?**  
   Current navigation validation predicts only the next waypoint. A next experiment could deploy ConsiSpace in an embodied simulator and measure navigation success, path efficiency, collision rate, and recovery after viewpoint changes.

## Confidence and missing information

**Confidence: high** for the described method, benchmark results, ablations, and stated limitations because the supplied text includes the main paper and appendices.
