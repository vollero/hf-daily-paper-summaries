# AgentDebugX: An Open-Source Toolkit for Failure Observability, Attribution, and Recovery in LLM Agents

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-22.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.18754) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.18754)


## One-sentence takeaway

AgentDebugX unifies agent-trace capture, root-cause attribution, proposed repair, and gated rerunning, with DeepDebug improving attribution on tested open-weight models and repairing 13 of 73 initially failed GAIA tasks in one rerun.

## Problem and motivation

LLM-agent errors may surface many steps after the decision that caused them, making trace replay insufficient for identifying the responsible agent and step or producing an actionable correction. The authors argue that existing observability, attribution, and self-correction systems address separate pieces of this problem but do not provide a deployable, framework-independent loop from detection through verified recovery (Introduction; Table 1).

## Approach and contributions

- **Closed debugging loop:** AgentDebugX implements Detect, Attribute, Recover, and Rerun over a portable `AgentTrajectory`, retaining original and repaired branches for comparison (Sections 3.1–3.2; Figure 1).
- **Multi-turn attribution:** DeepDebug combines a global trace reading with handoff traversal for multi-agent runs or bisection for single-agent runs, then cross-examines competing root-cause candidates and emits an auditable diagnosis and concrete fix (Section 3.3).
- **Operational toolkit:** The framework provides a Python library, CLI, local web console, runtime adapters/importers, and an installable agentic skill (Sections 3.5; Appendix D).
- **Shared debugging assets:** An opt-in Error Hub stores scrubbed trajectory–diagnosis–repair bundles for incident records, regression fixtures, and potential diagnostic memory (Section 3.5).
- **Extensible taxonomy:** A 19-mode seed taxonomy can be extended through clustered candidate failures subject to human review (Sections 3.2 and 3.4; Appendix A).

## Evidence and main results

| Finding | Evidence / setting | Location in paper |
|---|---|---|
| DeepDebug led all evaluated localizers on five reported qwen3.5-9b attribution metrics. | Who&When, all 184 traces: agent accuracy 56.0% vs 47.8%; exact/±1 step accuracy 28.8%/44.0% vs 22.3%/38.6%; strict exact/±1 joint accuracy 28.8%/32.1% vs 21.7%/23.9% for the strongest reported alternatives. | Section 4, “Failure attribution”; Table 2 |
| The strict joint gain was smaller on the larger tested open-weight model. | Who&When with qwen3.6-27b: 38.0% strict agent-and-step accuracy vs 36.4% for the strongest reported comparison. | Section 4, “Failure attribution” |
| Structure-guided investigation outperformed duplicating the global search in one ablation. | Stratified 42-trace sample using gpt-5.4-mini: strict accuracy 0.310 vs 0.262, a 4.8-point difference; agent accuracy 0.524 vs 0.429 for one reading. | Appendix C, “Localizer design ablation” |
| Gains appeared concentrated on long traces, but the subgroup was small. | Who&When qwen3.5-9b run; only 26 traces exceeded 40 events, and the authors characterize the result as descriptive. | Section 4, “Where the multi-turn agent pays off”; Figure 3 |
| DeepDebug’s retry directive repaired more failed GAIA tasks than the three decoupled baselines. | GAIA validation: one rerun of the same 73 failures from a qwen3.5-9b Open-Deep-Research agent; 13 repairs versus 4–6 for Reflexion, CRITIC, and AutoManual recipes. | Section 4, “End-to-end recovery on GAIA”; Table 3; Appendix C |
| Recovery raised projected overall GAIA accuracy. | Across 165 validation tasks, initial accuracy was 55.8%; one-rerun DeepDebug recovery raised it to 63.6%. Level-2 accuracy increased from 48.8% to 61.6%. | Section 4; Table 3 |
| Multi-turn diagnosis incurred moderate additional token use in a small sample. | Stratified 25-trace Who&When sample: one global pass averaged 8.1K tokens; DeepDebug averaged 12.8K, or 1.6×. DeepDebug used approximately five calls. | Section 4, “Cost-aware selection” |

The evidence supports the authors’ claim that DeepDebug can improve strict localization on the two tested open-weight backbones and that its complete diagnosis-to-retry recipe can recover more failures than the evaluated decoupled recipes under the stated GAIA protocol. It does **not** isolate whether recovery gains come specifically from better attribution, the authored correction, or their combination; nor does it establish universal gains across models, since hosted-model results were model-dependent.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** Evaluation does not measure developer debugging time or console usability (Appendix E).
- **Author-stated:** DeepDebug’s extra calls are not uniformly beneficial; a single global reading was stronger on some hosted backbones (Section 4; Appendix E).
- **Author-stated:** GAIA evaluates one policy model, a fixed subset of 73 initial failures, and complete retry recipes rather than attribution in isolation; it is not a blind single-shot comparison (Appendices C and E).
- **Author-stated:** Error Hub retrieval and taxonomy induction are implemented but unevaluated, and taxonomy changes require human acceptance (Appendix E).
- **Author-stated:** Pattern-based scrubbing cannot guarantee removal of arbitrary sensitive material, while recovery requires a human or policy gate (Section 3.5; Ethical Considerations; Appendix E).
- **Analyst inference:** The long-trace finding is uncertain because it rests on only 26 traces and is explicitly presented as descriptive (Section 4; Figure 3).
- **Analyst inference:** Comparisons against Reflexion, CRITIC, and AutoManual do not control for access to the same diagnostic information: those baselines receive a generic summary rather than DeepDebug’s localization, evidence, and fix (Section 4; Appendix C).
- **Analyst inference:** Although artifacts and regeneration scripts are said to ship with the code, the supplied text does not report independent reproduction or repeated-run variability.

## Open research questions

1. **Which DeepDebug component causes the recovery improvement?** The GAIA experiment evaluates localization and authored repair jointly. A factorial experiment could independently vary gold versus predicted localization and generic versus DeepDebug-authored fixes.

2. **When should multi-turn diagnosis be invoked?** Benefits vary across backbones and appear concentrated on longer traces. A next experiment could train and evaluate a routing rule based on trace length, candidate disagreement, confidence, expected token cost, and model family.

3. **Does historical debugging memory improve diagnosis safely?** Error Hub retrieval is implemented but unevaluated. A controlled study could compare empty memory, reviewed relevant cases, irrelevant cases, and contaminated cases on held-out failures.

4. **Does the toolkit improve human debugging outcomes?** Developer time and console usability are not evaluated. A user study could measure diagnosis accuracy, repair success, completion time, and inappropriate trust with and without AgentDebugX.

## Confidence and missing information

**Confidence: medium.** The supplied text describes the methods, protocols, principal comparisons, and limitations in substantial detail. However, the rendered numeric rows of Tables 2 and 3 and Figure 3’s subgroup values are absent; full per-baseline GAIA results, confidence intervals or variance estimates, latency and monetary cost, detailed hosted-model scores, failure-mode breakdowns, and independent reproducibility evidence are unavailable.
