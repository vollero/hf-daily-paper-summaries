# Two-Level Meta-Rubrics for Evaluating Open-Ended Generation: GAMUT, a Benchmark for Factual Completeness

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-22.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.19322) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19322)


## One-sentence takeaway

GAMUT evaluates factual completeness by authoring structured, evidence-grounded meta-rubrics and mechanically compiling them into binary checks, revealing substantial omissions even in the strongest evaluated multimodal model.

## Problem and motivation

Long-form factuality evaluation primarily measures precision—whether generated claims are correct—while giving less attention to recall or completeness—whether an answer includes the information it should. The authors argue that flat lists of independent fact checks cannot faithfully represent open-ended sets, required coverage levels, ordered processes, relationships, or differences in importance (Sections 1–2).

This matters because an answer can contain no obvious falsehoods yet remain seriously incomplete. Rich structured criteria are difficult for automatic judges to apply consistently, however, creating a tension between expressive annotation and reliable grading (Sections 1 and 3).

## Approach and contributions

- **Two-level evaluation framework:** A per-question structured meta-rubric records five content types—Simple Knowledge, Strict List, Flexible List, Process, and Relationship—plus three importance tiers and optional meta-insights. Deterministic rules compile this representation into independently gradable checks (Sections 3.1–3.2).

- **Completeness-sensitive scoring:** Judges assign `meets`, `partially meets`, `missing`, or `contradicts`. Scores use partial-credit weight \(\lambda=0.5\), contradiction penalty \(\mu=-2\), and tier weights 0.6/0.3/0.1 for Answer-Critical, Valuable, and Context content (Section 3.3).

- **GAMUT benchmark:** The released asset contains 1,813 open-ended questions across 10 everyday domains, grounded in real wearable imagery and paired with rubrics built through LLM-assisted research and expert review (Sections 4 and 7).

- **Text-only variant and model study:** The authors also release self-contained textual questions and evaluate 14 proprietary and open-weight models, including a three-judge robustness comparison (Sections 5.1–5.2).

These results establish that the benchmark separates the tested systems, exposes extensive missing content, and yields broadly stable rankings under three selected judges. They do not independently establish that the rubric definitions exhaust all valid answer content, that GAMUT scores align with end-user judgments, or that judge robustness generalizes beyond the three judges and this benchmark.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated scope:** GAMUT targets “everyday deep research,” not academic report generation, and its source images and domain distribution reflect wearable-assistant use cases (Sections 2, 4.1, and Figure 2).

- **Incomplete evidence citation:** Despite the paper’s description of evidence-backed rubrics, only 97% of meta-rubric items and 94% of compiled checks have citations (Figure 3). The supplied text does not characterize the uncited remainder.

- **Analyst inference—construction-model dependence:** Gemini 3.1 Pro generates initial questions and rubrics, participates in refinement, serves as the main judge, and is also an evaluated model (Sections 4–5). Cross-judge results reduce concern about scoring self-favoritism, but no ablation measures how its role in dataset construction affects question or rubric selection.

- **Analyst inference—human validation breadth:** Rubric revision relies on a small in-house team because a general annotation pool reportedly could not improve the drafts (Section 4.2). The paper provides training and 10% auditing details but no reported inter-annotator agreement for final rubrics or comparison against independently authored rubrics (Appendix C).

- **Analyst inference—score-design sensitivity:** The paper fixes \(\lambda=0.5\), \(\mu=-2\), and tier weights 0.6/0.3/0.1, but the supplied text reports no sensitivity analysis for these choices (Section 3.3).

- **Analyst inference—text-only interpretation:** The multimodal-to-text gains vary from +7.8 to +23.9 points, so describing visual identification as a roughly constant tax is approximate rather than literal (Table 3).

- **Analyst inference—external validity:** Judge stability is tested with three judges on one benchmark. No human-versus-judge agreement result or evaluation outside GAMUT is reported in the supplied text.

## Open research questions

1. **How sensitive are model rankings to scoring parameters?**  
   This remains open because no sensitivity study is reported for tier weights, partial credit, or contradiction penalties. A next experiment could sweep these parameters and report rank correlations and confidence intervals.

2. **Do compiled LLM judgments agree with independent human completeness judgments?**  
   The paper reports cross-LLM robustness but no direct human–judge agreement on scored answers. A blinded study could have multiple experts grade a stratified answer sample in both structured and compiled formats.

3. **Does model-assisted construction systematically favor particular answer styles or model families?**  
   Gemini 3.1 Pro plays several construction and evaluation roles, while construction-bias ablations are absent. A plausible experiment would independently rebuild matched rubric subsets with different model families and human-only authors, then compare coverage and rankings.

4. **Does the framework transfer beyond everyday deep research?**  
   General applicability is claimed, but the empirical study centers on this benchmark. The framework could be tested on academic synthesis, technical procedures, or other open-ended generation tasks using domain-expert rubrics.

## Confidence and missing information

**Confidence: high** for the framework, dataset composition, scoring rules, and reported results because the supplied text includes the full main paper, tables, figures, prompts, and annotation description.

Material missing information includes human–LLM grading agreement, statistical uncertainty or significance tests, scoring-weight sensitivity, construction-model ablations, detailed characteristics of uncited rubric checks, final-rubric inter-annotator agreement, computational cost, and independent evidence that the rubrics exhaust the acceptable factual content for each question.
