# ReferTrack: Referring Then Tracking for Embodied Visual Tracking

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-24.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.20061) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.20061)


## One-sentence takeaway

ReferTrack improves single-camera embodied person tracking by explicitly selecting the language-specified pedestrian from indexed image-space bounding boxes before predicting waypoints, achieving the strongest reported single-view results on all three EVT-Bench splits.

## Problem and motivation

Embodied visual tracking requires a mobile robot to identify a person described in natural language and continuously follow them using onboard vision while avoiding collisions and maintaining a suitable distance. The authors argue that existing VLA policies often represent target-identification reasoning through abstract spatial tokens that are difficult to supervise and insufficiently grounded in visible detections, particularly in crowded or ambiguous scenes (§1).

ReferTrack addresses this gap by converting identification into a directly supervised choice among detected pedestrian bounding boxes and conditioning trajectory planning on that choice.

## Approach and contributions

- **Referring-then-tracking policy:** A single Refer-CoT token selects an indexed pedestrian bounding box—or `NO_EXIST`—before an action token is decoded into tracking waypoints (§3.1–3.2; Fig. 2).

- **Target-conditioned temporal memory:** Selected boxes enter a sliding FIFO queue and are embedded into historical visual tokens as temporal-viewpoint-bbox indicator (TVBI) tokens, preserving target geometry and motion cues across frames (§3.2, Eq. 2).

- **Refer-QA co-training:** The authors synthesize 1.3M indexed-box referring examples from SYNTH-PEDES and jointly train them with 1.3M EVT navigation samples at a 1:1 ratio (§3.3; Appendix A.2–A.3).

- **Single-view evaluation and deployment:** A 4B-parameter, SFT-only model is evaluated on EVT-Bench using one forward RGB camera and qualitatively deployed on Unitree Go2 and G1 robots (§4.1–4.4).

The quantitative evidence establishes strong simulator performance under the stated EVT-Bench single-view protocol and supports the authors’ claim that explicit target selection is particularly useful under distraction and ambiguity. The oracle and component ablations also support identification as an important DT bottleneck.

It does not establish statistically significant improvements, broad real-world reliability, or that referring is intrinsically better than additional cameras: the multi-camera numbers are external references rather than controlled comparisons, and the physical-robot evidence is qualitative.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-indicated bottleneck:** The oracle-box ablation shows that target identification remains substantially below perfect-identification performance on DT (§4.3).

- **Detector dependence:** The candidate set comes from YOLO11+ByteTrack, and scenes with more than \(K\) pedestrians retain candidates by box area (§3.2, §4.1). Missed detections, tracking errors, or exclusion from top-\(K\) can therefore prevent correct selection.

- **Analyst inference — narrow quantitative environment:** All reported numerical task results come from EVT-Bench in Habitat 3.0; the real-world evaluation provides examples rather than success rates, episode counts, or controlled comparisons (§4.1, §4.4).

- **Analyst inference — incomplete ablation coverage:** Ablations are reported only on the DT split, so the separate effects of Refer-CoT, TVBI, and Refer-QA on STT and AT are not established (§4.3).

- **Analyst inference — Refer-QA effect not isolated:** The supplied ablation discussion does not report a model trained without Refer-QA, leaving its independent contribution unclear.

- **Analyst inference — comparison uncertainty:** Baseline results are taken from their respective publications, and multi-camera results are explicitly treated as external references rather than ranked, controlled comparisons (§4.1; Table 1).

- **Analyst inference — training-selection bias:** Expert navigation data retain successful, collision-free rollouts and store failures separately, which may narrow the demonstrated behavior distribution (Appendix A.1).

## Open research questions

1. **How much does Refer-QA co-training contribute independently?** No without-Refer-QA result is supplied. A next experiment could hold navigation data and optimization fixed while varying Refer-QA inclusion and mixture ratio across all EVT-Bench splits.

2. **How robust is ReferTrack to detection and catalog failures?** The method assumes the target appears among detector-produced top-\(K\) candidates. A next experiment could systematically vary missed detections, box noise, identity switches, crowd size, and \(K\).

3. **Does the approach remain reliable in extended physical deployments?** The paper reports qualitative robot rollouts but no real-world aggregate metrics. A next experiment could run repeated trials on both platforms and report identification accuracy, tracking success, collision rate, latency, and target-loss recovery.

4. **Does explicit referring outperform extra camera coverage under controlled conditions?** Current multi-camera comparisons use externally reported systems. A next experiment could train and test the same backbone and data recipe with one versus multiple cameras while holding all other factors fixed.
