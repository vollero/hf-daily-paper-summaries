# Streaming Multi-Agent Autoregressive Diffusion Model with World State Registers

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-24.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.21594) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.21594)


## One-sentence takeaway

WorldWeaver adds persistent, supervised cross-agent state tokens to a streaming video diffusion model, improving logical consistency and visual quality in synchronized two-agent Minecraft rollouts over frame-history-based baselines.

## Problem and motivation

Multi-agent world models must generate compatible observations for agents viewing and changing the same partially observed world. Existing autoregressive video diffusion models mainly condition on a local history of generated frames, forcing them to repeatedly reconstruct shared state and making unobserved changes, global geometry, and cross-view consistency difficult to preserve. The authors address this gap by representing the evolving shared world explicitly within the generation process (§1).

## Approach and contributions

- **World state registers:** A bank of learnable tokens shared across agents and committed after each generated frame. Each register update combines the previous state with recent observations, then conditions the next frame (§3.3; Figure 2).
- **Explicit state grounding:** Training-only heads supervise registers using agent position, velocity, and orientation; overhead-view DINOv2 features; and generated scene descriptions (§3.3; Appendix B.1).
- **Joint frame-and-state rollout:** A three-stage curriculum progresses from bidirectional multi-agent training to causal register training and then self-forcing on generated frames and registers, exposing both frame and state drift during training (§3.1–§3.4).
- **Role-specific architecture:** A Mixture-of-Transformers backbone gives register and visual tokens separate parameter branches while retaining joint attention (§4.4; Appendix B.2).
- **Semi-supervised extension:** State-labeled clips supervise both registers and video generation, while unlabeled clips contribute only the diffusion objective (§4.5).

The evidence supports improved benchmark performance in the tested two-agent Minecraft setting and indicates that explicit state supervision, MoT separation under semantic supervision, and additional unlabeled videos contribute to the reported gains. It does not establish generalization to real-world video, other simulators, more than two agents, or arbitrary rollout lengths. Exact aggregate VLM/FID values, uncertainty estimates, sample counts, and most individual table cells are absent from the supplied rendering.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** The principal gains depend on simulator state supervision that is substantially harder to obtain in real environments (§5).
- **Author-stated:** Real-world state can contain much richer low-level 3D detail and high-level agent relationships than the tested supervision represents (§5).
- **Author-stated:** Scene captions focus on the two agents’ local motion and controller inputs and omit broader terrain or events outside their immediate vicinity (Appendix A.4).
- **Analyst inference:** External validity is narrow because all reported experiments use synchronized two-agent Minecraft data from one simulator pipeline (§4.1; Appendix A.1).
- **Analyst inference:** Caption targets are generated by Qwen2.5-VL-72B-Instruct rather than human annotation, but the paper reports no caption-quality audit in the supplied text (Appendix A.4).
- **Analyst inference:** WorldScore is unbounded and FID-scale-sensitive; the authors accordingly treat it as auxiliary, yet several ablation summaries emphasize it (§4.1, Tables 2–4).
- **Analyst inference:** The supplied text gives no statistical uncertainty, repeated-run analysis, or significance testing, limiting confidence in the robustness of reported differences.
- **Analyst inference:** MoT increases parameter count despite unchanged per-token FLOPs apart from routing overhead; memory use, latency, and end-to-end throughput are not reported (Appendix B.2).

## Open research questions

1. **Do registers remain useful beyond two agents and longer rollouts?** The evaluation only establishes results for two-player Minecraft. A next experiment could scale agent count and rollout duration while measuring consistency and register drift by horizon.

2. **Can registers be grounded without privileged simulator labels?** The semi-supervised study still relies on 1K labeled clips. A next experiment could compare learned pseudo-labels, multiview reconstruction, or self-supervised state objectives at several label budgets.

3. **Which state signal improves which behavior?** The paper reports single-signal WorldScore summaries but the supplied text lacks full per-category results. A next experiment could report category-level VLM/FID changes and controlled removal of each signal from the combined model.

4. **Does the method transfer outside Minecraft?** No cross-domain results are provided. A next experiment could train or adapt the same register mechanism on a different interactive simulator or synchronized real multiview recordings.
