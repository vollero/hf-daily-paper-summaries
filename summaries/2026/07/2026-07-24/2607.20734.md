# LLMs Get Lost in Evolving User Intent

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-24.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.20734) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.20734)


## One-sentence takeaway

The authors show that strong single-turn LLM performance often degrades when user requirements are progressively revealed, revised, or redirected, and introduce a framework for measuring this failure using existing verifiable benchmarks.

## Problem and motivation

Collaborative agents must maintain an accurate representation of what users currently want as requirements emerge and change across turns. Most evaluations instead present a fully specified task in one turn, making this capability invisible.

The authors argue that existing multi-turn benchmarks are difficult to scale, often rely on LLM judges, cover relatively short interactions, and offer limited control over user behavior—frequently testing only incremental disclosure rather than revisions or task changes (Introduction; Section 2). The paper therefore asks whether static benchmarks can become controlled, long-horizon interaction tests while retaining their original automatic verifiers.

## Approach and contributions

- **Structured evolving intent:** An intent state contains a function, its arguments, the revealed subset, and a target answer. Three controlled transitions model argument reveal, argument revision, and function switch (Section 3; Figure 2).

- **Retrospective benchmark transformation:** The original task is placed as the final-turn anchor. Counterfactual argument values and related predecessor functions are synthesized backward to create earlier turns, so the final action remains scorable by the source benchmark’s verifier (Sections 4.1–4.2; Figure 3).

- **Controlled conversation simulation:** A scheduler enforces transition consistency, after which rule-based or validated LLM-based renderers express only the current intent update (Section 4.3; Appendix C).

- **Cross-domain evaluation and analysis:** The framework is applied to math, text-to-SQL, agentic search, and software engineering, with analyses of transition type, count, composition, memory interventions, source difficulty, model capacity, reasoning, and turn count (Section 5; Appendix F).

- **Preliminary training use:** The authors demonstrate that the transformed conversations can supply outcome-verifiable reinforcement-learning examples (Appendix F.5).

These results support the authors’ claim that controlled intent changes—not merely longer conversations—expose a weakness hidden by single-turn evaluation. They do not establish performance with real users, unrestricted conversations, or every possible intent dynamic. They also do not cleanly isolate intent tracking from all execution and context-interference errors: oracle recap improves but does not close the gap, while the direct tracking probe uses a separate judge (Section 5.3).

## Limitations and validity risks

**Author-stated limitations:**

- User persona, communication style, typos, and grammatical variation are not modeled, so utterances may be stylistically uniform.
- Each user turn is assumed to contain one transition; simultaneous revisions, switches, or multiple requests are excluded.
- Exact verifier-based reward exists only at the final turn, not throughout the trajectory (“Limitations and Future Directions”).
- Evaluation sets are filtered for extractability and then sampled at only 200 GSM8K, 100 BIRD-SQL, 100 BrowseComp+, and 50 SWE-Bench Verified instances because multi-turn inference is costly (Appendix B.2).

**Analyst inferences:**

- Synthetic-component validity remains partly dependent on GPT 5.1: it generates the main pipeline components and acts as judge or reference solver during several rejection-sampling checks (Section 5; Appendix D). This may introduce correlated construction and validation errors.
- The framework evaluates conversations deliberately constructed to converge back to an existing task. Its conclusions may not transfer to conversations whose final intent has no pre-existing single-turn equivalent.
- The extreme SWE-Bench outcomes are sensitive to scaffold and budget behavior: most models receive 100 tool calls per turn, while two receive 200, and several zero scores are associated with timeouts (Sections 5 and 5.1).
- The direct intent-tracking analysis is narrow—GPT 5.1 on GSM8K—and relies on a separate judge rather than the native verifier (Table 3).
- No human study of conversation realism is reported in the supplied text. BrowseComp+ uses validated LLM naturalization, whereas the main simulator otherwise relies largely on rule-based rendering (Appendix C.2).

## Open research questions

1. **Do the same failures occur in genuine human-agent conversations?** The evaluation uses synthetic trajectories and omits user-style diversity. A next experiment could collect matched human conversations for the same final tasks and compare transition-specific degradation.

2. **Can explicit, learned intent state outperform prompt and oracle recaps?** Oracle recap helps but remains below single-turn performance. A next experiment could train a state tracker that updates structured functions and arguments after every turn, then ablate tracking errors versus action-selection errors.

3. **How should intermediate actions be verified?** Only the final turn has exact benchmark-derived supervision. A next experiment could add transition-level verifiers for a domain such as SQL, where intermediate intent states can potentially be converted into executable queries.

4. **How well does targeted training generalize beyond GSM8K?** The preliminary GRPO result covers Qwen3-4B and transformed math examples only. A next experiment could train across several transition compositions and test held-out domains, longer trajectories, and unseen transition orders.
