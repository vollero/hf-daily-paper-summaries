# Sample-Efficient Learning from Agent Experience

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-24.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.21051) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.21051)


## One-sentence takeaway

Experience Distillation trains an agent to reproduce experience-conditioned decisions at recorded history points, retaining substantial in-context-learning gains without further environment interaction and outperforming direct SFT and the evaluated RL baselines in sample efficiency.

## Problem and motivation

Agents can learn from trial-and-error histories in context, but the benefit disappears when that context is removed; conventional context distillation would require new teacher–environment rollouts, undermining sample efficiency. The authors define **Experience Distillation** as consolidating experience-induced behavior into model weights without additional environment interaction, motivated by settings where experiments, expert feedback, or long agent rollouts are costly (Abstract; Sections 1 and 3).

## Approach and contributions

- **One-step Experience Distillation:** At each recorded history point, an experience-conditioned teacher samples only its next decision. A student without the experience context learns that decision, requiring neither new environment responses nor a learned world model (Sections 3.1–3.2, Equations 1–2).
- **Practical long-history pipeline:** Experience is preprocessed, the teacher receives a prompt eliciting more thorough reasoning, and successive branches are packed into dense sequences. Only teacher-generated decisions receive loss (Section 3.3, Equations 3–4).
- **Empirical characterization:** The paper compares Experience Distillation with direct SFT, PPO/GRPO, model-based rollouts, reverse-KL on-policy distillation, and full-distribution distillation; it also evaluates OOD transfer, continual learning, data scaling, and packing efficiency (Sections 4.2–4.10).
- **Two-domain evaluation:** Experiments cover 749 curated software-engineering tasks and six long-horizon text-adventure games, with additional evaluation on 494 OOD software-engineering tasks (Sections 4.1 and 4.6).

## Evidence and main results

| Finding | Evidence / setting | Location in paper |
|---|---|---|
| Experience Distillation retained much more ICL gain than SFT. | On 749 SWE tasks: 51.4% pass@1 and 64.8% \(G_{\mathrm{ICL}}\), versus SFT at 8.0% and 3.8%; zeroshot was 5.3% and ICL 76.4%. On six TaleSuite tasks: score 43.8 and 93.4% \(G_{\mathrm{ICL}}\), versus SFT at 17.8 and −2.6%; zeroshot was 18.5 and ICL 45.6. | Section 4.2, Table 1 |
| The pipeline used fewer environment samples and achieved higher performance than the evaluated RL baselines. | SWE: 51.4% pass@1 versus PPO’s 17.7%, with 9.6× fewer complete rollouts. TaleSuite: 43.8 versus GRPO’s 29.9, with 57.2× fewer rollouts. | Section 4.3, Figure 1 |
| Removing world-model participation improved the evaluated distillation targets. | Across five TaleSuite tasks, model-free one-step distillation averaged 83.8% task-level \(G_{\mathrm{ICL}}\), versus 57.3% for a two-decision model-based branch rollout; Detective improved from 19.2% for full rollout to 68.1% for branch rollout and 86.7% for one-step distillation. | Sections 4.4, Table 2 |

The evidence establishes strong performance and environment-sample efficiency under the paper’s two agent domains and protocols. It does not establish superiority to all RL or distillation methods: only particular PPO, GRPO, reverse-KL, and model-based configurations were evaluated, and RL’s best checkpoint was charged the full run budget (Section 4.1). OOD gains demonstrate transfer within software engineering, not transfer to unrelated domains.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated cost limitation:** Teacher generation remains expensive; the largest SWE run generates data equal to 2.79× the 61.7M-token experience corpus (Section 4.10, Figure 3).
- **Approximate objective:** Branch packing is not an exact implementation of Equation 2 because later teacher decisions condition on earlier teacher-generated decisions (Section 3.3).
- **World-model evidence is limited:** Table 2 covers five TaleSuite tasks, and full rollout is reported only for Detective. The qualitative errors illustrate failure modes but do not estimate their frequency (Section 4.4; Appendix A.5).
- **Analyst inference — narrow external validity:** Evaluation is confined to curated SWE and text-adventure games; the claimed relevance to laboratory experiments or expert feedback remains motivational rather than tested.
- **Analyst inference — baseline sensitivity:** PPO and GRPO results come from specific in-house-model runs and budgets. The text does not provide a broad sweep across RL algorithms, hyperparameters, or equalized wall-clock/compute costs.
- **Analyst inference — unclear reproducibility:** Model identities, preprocessing implementation, fixed reasoning prompt, and release status are not supplied in the retrieved text. The use of distinct in-house base models further limits independent replication (Sections 3.3 and 4.1).
- **Selected qualitative cases:** The eight SWE studies were chosen for outcome contrast and do not estimate prevalence; individual teacher excerpts cannot establish a unique causal transfer mechanism (Appendix C.11).

## Open research questions

1. **Does Experience Distillation work when interactions are genuinely expensive or non-textual?** The experiments use software and games rather than the motivating laboratory or expert-review settings. A next experiment could test the method on a fixed-budget embodied or human-feedback task and measure gains per real interaction.

2. **Can teacher-generation cost be reduced without losing retained ICL gain?** Figure 3 shows continued gains as generated tokens increase. A next experiment could compare branch selection, uncertainty-based sampling, target compression, and fewer teacher samples at matched environment and inference budgets.

3. **How robust is the method across models and experience quality?** The paper uses distinct unspecified in-house models and curated successful SWE histories. A factorial experiment could vary model family, model scale, noisy versus successful histories, and preprocessing while reporting absolute performance and \(G_{\mathrm{ICL}}\).

4. **Which parts of experience are actually consolidated?** Selected case studies show correspondence but not causal attribution. A next experiment could remove or perturb individual trials, feedback events, rejected hypotheses, or teacher reasoning and measure task-level changes.
