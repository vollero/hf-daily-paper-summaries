# TableVerse: A Large-scale Tabletop Dataset with Real-world Grounded Layouts for Generalizable Manipulation

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-24.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.21017) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.21017)


## One-sentence takeaway

TableVerse converts single-view internet images into physically stabilized tabletop simulations and generated pick-and-place trajectories, producing 100,000 scenes while reducing pre-relaxation scene collisions from 79.0% to 0.0% on a 100-scene test set.

## Problem and motivation

Generalizable manipulation policies need large, physically usable scene and trajectory data that resembles real tabletop environments. The authors argue that existing procedural and text-conditioned generators tend to produce sparse, orderly layouts, while single-view reconstruction methods can introduce incorrect scales and mesh intersections—problems that are especially severe in dense clutter, stacking, and containers with nested objects (§1; §2.1–2.2).

The paper addresses the gap between abundant unstructured real-world images and simulation-ready scenes with metric scale, recoverable object topology, stable contacts, and executable manipulation demonstrations.

## Approach and contributions

- **Automated Real2Sim pipeline:** Starting from one unstructured image, TableVerse detects and segments objects, reconstructs individual meshes, derives metric point clouds, aligns gravity, and estimates object dimensions and 6-DoF poses through rotation search and ICP (§3.1; Figure 2).

- **Composite-object reconstruction:** Containers and their contents are reconstructed separately, then assembled through free-fall simulation so nested items remain independently manipulable (§3.1; Figure 3).

- **Layout-Consistent Collision Rectification:** LCCR groups likely stacked or contained objects, radially separates lateral intersections while preserving azimuthal directions, lifts vertically intersecting objects, and uses MuJoCo to settle the resulting scene (§3.2; Figure 4).

- **Dataset and annotation pipeline:** TableVerse-100K contains 100,000 instruction-annotated environments, nearly 1 million object instances, more than 35,000 semantic categories, simulation-ready MJCF files, and over 1,700 tabletop textures (§3.3; Figures 1, 5, and 6).

- **Trajectory synthesis:** MLLM-generated pick-and-place tasks are converted into demonstrations using GraspGen candidates, prioritized top-down grasps, relation-constrained placement sampling, and cuRobo joint-space planning (§3.4; Appendix D).

These results support the authors’ claim that LCCR eliminates detected pre-relaxation mesh intersections on their curated reconstruction benchmark and that TableVerse is preferred by their MLLM evaluator over the three tested reconstruction systems. They do not establish real-robot transfer, manipulation-policy improvement, trajectory execution success, or generalization beyond the 100 selected tabletop images. The reported 0.0% collision rate also measures volumetric intersection, not every aspect of mechanical stability or physical realism.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** Small or low-resolution objects inside containers may be reconstructed by SAM3D as entirely different objects (§5).

- **Author-stated:** Reconstructing every object independently with SAM3D is time-consuming; no runtime or compute measurements are supplied (§5).

- **Analyst inference:** Evaluation is narrow relative to the claimed dataset scale: reconstruction quality and LCCR are evaluated on only 100 curated internet scenes (§4.2–4.3).

- **Analyst inference:** Important quality results depend on an MLLM judge, but the supplied text provides no human-rating agreement, repeated-judge variance, or alternative perceptual evaluation (§4.1; Appendix C.2).

- **Analyst inference:** The baseline protocol is not fully symmetric. Baselines receive TableVerse masks, cannot represent nested objects, and are evaluated with nested contents omitted; SceneMaker additionally receives point-cloud upsampling. These choices are explained, but their effect on rankings is not ablated (§4.1; Appendix C.1).

- **Analyst inference:** The component ablation establishes the collision benefit of LCCR, but does not quantify how much radial and vertical rectification changes the original layout; therefore “layout-preserving” is not directly validated with a displacement or topology metric (§4.3).

- **Analyst inference:** No success rate, failure taxonomy, or physical-validity measurement is reported for the generated pick-and-place trajectories, so their stated quality and executability remain empirically unverified in the supplied text (§3.4).

- **Analyst inference:** No downstream policy-training experiment or real-robot study demonstrates that the dataset improves generalizable manipulation, despite that being its motivating use (§4).

## Open research questions

1. **Does TableVerse-100K improve manipulation-policy generalization?** This remains open because no downstream policy is trained or evaluated. A next experiment could train the same policy with and without TableVerse data and compare success on held-out simulated layouts and real tabletop scenes.

2. **How faithfully does LCCR preserve the observed layout?** Zero collision does not measure displacement, topology retention, or relation preservation. A useful experiment would compare pre- and post-LCCR object positions, orientations, pairwise spatial relations, and contact graphs against manually reconstructed references.

3. **How reliable are the generated demonstrations?** The paper supplies no trajectory success statistics. A next experiment could execute a stratified sample across clutter levels and relation types, reporting planning success, collision rate, grasp success, placement-relation accuracy, and failure causes.

4. **How robust is reconstruction under occlusion and low resolution?** The authors identify small nested objects as a failure case but provide no stratified analysis. An experiment could bin objects by visible pixel area and occlusion level, then measure category, geometry, pose, and scale accuracy.

## Confidence and missing information

**Confidence: medium.** The supplied text describes the pipeline and reports several explicit experimental values, but the rendered numeric bodies of Tables 1 and 2 are absent; only values repeated in the surrounding prose can be verified.

Material missing information includes full per-baseline metric results, dataset licensing and exact release contents, source-image licensing and provenance, train/validation/test splits, dataset acceptance rates, compute and runtime costs, numeric mechanical-stability criteria, trajectory counts and success rates, human validation of MLLM annotations and GPT-Score, downstream policy results, and real-robot evaluation.
