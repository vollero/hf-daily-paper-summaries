# Spectral Rewiring for Exploration, Purification, and Model Merging

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-17.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.03065) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.03065)


## One-sentence takeaway

The authors propose a training-free spectral projection of dense language-model RL updates that preserves compact reasoning gains while improving exploration and reducing interference in mixed-domain training and expert merging.

## Problem and motivation

Full-parameter reinforcement-learning post-training can narrow useful solution diversity, causing Pass@k saturation, and can create parameter-space conflicts when combining mathematics, coding, and instruction-following capabilities. The authors argue that existing training and merging methods offer limited control over which parts of a dense update are reasoning-effective versus interfering residuals (Introduction).

Their motivating hypothesis is that outcome-reward RL often reorganizes capabilities already present in the base model, so its useful update may be recoverable in the base model’s singular-vector coordinates rather than requiring arbitrary movement through the full parameter space (§2.2).

## Approach and contributions

- **Subspace-Aligned Rewiring (SAR):** For each selected weight matrix, SAR obtains a low-rank RL update and projects it into the base model’s left and right SVD subspaces, yielding \(\Delta W^*=UMV^\top\), where \(M=U^\top\Delta W_kV\) is the compact “rewiring matrix” (§2.2, Algorithm 1).

- **Spectral interpretation:** The authors interpret diagonal elements of \(M\) as rescaling existing components and off-diagonal elements as reconnecting pretrained input and output directions. This is a proposed mechanistic interpretation, not direct evidence that individual singular directions correspond to identifiable skills (§2.3, Equation 6).

- **Post-hoc extraction and exploration:** SAR compresses completed RL updates without retraining, approximately preserves their measured math performance, and improves large-\(k\) exploration (§3.1–§3.2).

- **Purification and merging:** The same projection is applied to mixed-domain RL updates and to math-expert deltas before merging with code experts, with reported improvements in cross-domain performance (§4.1–§4.2).

- **Controls and boundary analysis:** The paper compares SAR with equal-rank no-projection, random-projection, diagonal-only, and off-diagonal-only variants, and discusses cases where RL goes beyond eliciting knowledge already represented by the reference model (Appendices §10–§12).

The evidence supports SAR as an effective post-hoc edit across several model families, scales, and domains, with equal-rank controls indicating that projection—not merely low rank—contributes to some gains. It does not prove that singular vectors literally encode atomic skills, that off-manifold update components are universally harmful, or that SAR will work when RL learns genuinely new knowledge. Statistical uncertainty, repeated-run variability, and significance tests are not reported in the supplied text.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated boundary:** SAR is expected to work best when the reference model already contains the relevant ability or knowledge and RL mainly reorganizes it (Discussion; Appendix §10).

- **Author-stated boundary:** After more than 4,000 JustRL steps, projection recovers roughly 43% on AIME 2024 but remains below the final RL model’s roughly 52%, suggesting incomplete recovery under extensive optimization (Appendix §10).

- **Author-stated boundary:** Direct code RL from a base model is less projection-compatible when code formatting, execution protocols, or tool interfaces are not already established; projection improves after code SFT supplies that knowledge (Appendix §10).

- **Author-stated boundary:** Dense critic rewards or strong PPO-style optimization may move parameters beyond the reference spectral manifold (Appendix §10).

- **Analyst inference:** The “atomic skills” and relational-rewiring account is primarily an interpretation of the algebra. The supplied experiments show performance effects but do not causally identify semantic skills represented by particular singular vectors.

- **Analyst inference:** Rank selection uses a sweep to find the smallest rank preserving full-RL performance, which introduces evaluation-set selection risk unless ranks are chosen on separate validation data; such separation is not established (§3.1).

- **Analyst inference:** AIME contains only 30 problems, so differences of one covered problem and small Pass@k changes may be unstable. The text provides no confidence intervals or repeated-run analysis.

- **Analyst inference:** The agentic-coding evidence relies on an undescribed in-house model, limiting reproducibility and attribution of the reported gains (§3.3).

- **Analyst inference:** Merging evaluates two scale-specific math/code expert pairs. Broader expert combinations, unrelated base models, and more than two domains are not tested in the supplied text (§4.2).

- **Analyst inference:** FP32 SVD and FP16 storage are specified, but computational cost, memory requirements, wall-clock time, and comparison with deployment-oriented compression methods are not quantified (Appendix §9).

## Open research questions

1. **Can projection compatibility be predicted before full evaluation?** The paper identifies elicitation versus rewriting as the key boundary but supplies no predictive diagnostic. A next experiment could correlate the fraction of update energy retained by SAR with held-out performance recovery across RL checkpoints and domains.

2. **Does SAR generalize beyond sparse outcome-reward RL?** Dense-reward and critic-shaped training are discussed as potential failure cases but not systematically benchmarked. A controlled comparison could hold the base model and task fixed while varying reward density and optimization duration.

3. **Are the claimed spectral “skills” semantically identifiable?** The mechanistic account is algebraically motivated rather than directly validated. Intervention experiments could modify selected diagonal and off-diagonal entries and test preregistered effects on specific compositional behaviors.

4. **Can rank be selected without benchmark-driven sweeps?** Reported ranks are chosen to preserve measured performance. A next experiment could select rank using only update geometry or a separate validation set, then evaluate once on held-out benchmarks.
