# Partition, Prompt, Aggregate: Statistical Self-Consistency in Language Models

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-17.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.15277) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15277)


## One-sentence takeaway

LLM probability estimates frequently violate aggregation and ordering identities, while explicitly reconstructing population estimates from finer-grained subgroup predictions often improves alignment with survey data.

## Problem and motivation

Many uses of in-context learning treat an LLM’s output under a given prompt as an estimate of a conditional distribution. Such estimates should obey the law of total probability: a direct population estimate should equal the prior-weighted combination of estimates over any valid population partition. The authors identify a gap in existing evaluation: distributional alignment can measure agreement with reference data, but it does not test whether estimates across different prompt granularities form a coherent probabilistic system (Sections 1–2).

This matters for persona prompting, synthetic surveys, personalization, and forecasting because statistically equivalent formulations may otherwise produce incompatible answers, including when no ground-truth labels are available (Sections 5.3 and 7).

## Approach and contributions

1. **Partition-based evaluation.** Binary conditioning trees recursively divide a base population or scenario space using complementary binary attributes. The model estimates outcomes and subgroup priors at each node; estimates at every level are aggregated using the law of total probability and compared with the direct root estimate (Sections 3.1–3.4; Figure 1).

2. **Reference-free consistency criteria.** The paper introduces:

   - **Split consistency:** agreement between a parent estimate and the prior-weighted estimates of its two children.
   - **Order consistency:** invariance when equivalent conditioning constraints are verbalized in different orders.

   It also proves bounds connecting local checks to deeper aggregation and arbitrary constraint orderings (Definitions 1–5; Propositions 2, 6, and 7).

3. **The “macro fallacy.”** In ACS experiments, aggregates reconstructed from more specific subgroup estimates are generally better aligned with survey statistics than direct population-level estimates, although gains are non-monotonic with depth (Section 4; Figures 3–4).

4. **Error decomposition and intervention.** Oracle-prior experiments separate conditional-estimation errors from subgroup-prior errors, while “micro-to-macro” prompting asks a model to consider subpopulations within one prompt and partially recovers the explicit-aggregation benefit (Sections 4.2–4.4; Appendix A; Figures 5, 7–9).

The evidence establishes substantial within-model disagreement across statistically equivalent elicitation procedures and shows that explicit subgroup aggregation can improve ACS alignment. It does not show that consistency implies accuracy, that micro-to-macro prompting is universally beneficial, or that the synthetic forecasting estimates correspond to real outcome probabilities.

## Limitations and validity risks

**Author-stated limitations:**

- The macro-fallacy alignment analysis relies primarily on ACS data and therefore covers a narrow application domain (Section 7).
- Results depend on probability-verbalization prompts, post-hoc normalization, and selected demographic splits (Section 7).
- Self-consistency is necessary but insufficient for faithful conditional inference: consistently wrong estimates remain possible (Sections 5.3 and 7).
- Synthetic tasks demonstrate applicability without labels but do not establish real-world forecasting or decision quality (Section 7).
- Richer conditioning structures, broader domains, and alternative elicitation methods remain unevaluated (Section 7).

**Analyst inferences:**

- The order-consistency extension beyond two attributes depends on the unverified assumption that pairwise swap discrepancies do not depend on surrounding prompt context (Assumption 1; Proposition 6).
- WVS “low” versus “high” income is left for the model to interpret. Although treated as complementary, differing interpretations could weaken comparability across prompts or models (Appendix E.9).
- The ACS split attributes were selected partly by qualitative judgment for distinctive income distributions, so the observed aggregation gains may not generalize to arbitrary or uninformative partitions (Appendix E.1).
- Most reported consistency scores use a single tolerance, \(\varepsilon=0.02\); sensitivity to alternative tolerances is not established in the supplied text.
- Figures 3 and 6 support qualitative trends but do not provide all underlying numerical values, limiting precise effect-size comparison.

## Open research questions

1. **Which partitions reliably improve aggregate estimates?** Gains eventually decline with depth as priors become harder to estimate. A next experiment could compare random, balanced, outcome-informed, and model-selected trees under a fixed query budget.

2. **Can consistency be improved without sacrificing alignment?** The constraints are directionless, so enforcing agreement could move accurate subgroup estimates toward a worse aggregate. A next experiment could compare symmetric consistency training with an approach anchored to validated fine-grained estimates.

3. **Does pairwise order consistency predict higher-order invariance?** Proposition 6 requires context-independent pairwise discrepancies, but the paper does not empirically validate this assumption. A next experiment could exhaustively permute three- or four-attribute prompts and compare observed errors with the theoretical bound.

4. **How robust are the findings to elicitation choices?** The paper studies two persona formulations but acknowledges dependence on verbalization and normalization. A next experiment could compare verbalized probabilities, token-probability extraction, repeated sampling, and calibrated scoring across identical trees.
