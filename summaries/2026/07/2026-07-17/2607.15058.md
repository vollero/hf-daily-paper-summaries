# SUFLECA: Scaling Up Feature Learning for CAD-to-image Alignment

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-17.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.15058) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15058)


## One-sentence takeaway

SUFLECA combines large-scale NOC-supervised feature learning with geometry-consistent correspondence filtering to improve zero-shot 9D CAD alignment accuracy while avoiding iterative pose refinement.

## Problem and motivation

The paper addresses estimating a CAD object’s rotation, translation, and anisotropic scale from one RGB image, given camera intrinsics and a retrieved candidate CAD model. This supports applications such as robotic perception, manipulation, and augmented reality.

The authors identify two gaps in prior zero-shot systems: foundation-model correspondences are driven mainly by appearance and degrade under occlusion and real-to-synthetic domain shift, while conventional nearest-neighbor matching is computationally costly, permits geometrically inconsistent many-to-one matches, and often requires iterative refinement (Abstract; Sections I and II-C).

## Approach and contributions

- **Scaled geometry-aware feature learning:** A frozen perception encoder and trainable Dense Prediction Transformer are weakly supervised with binned Normalized Object Coordinate prediction. Training uses 674K real and synthetic images from 12 datasets, averaging 3.38 annotated objects per frame. The NOC head is removed at inference, leaving compact normalized descriptors (Section III-C; Figures 2–3; Table I).

- **Real–synthetic data unification:** Where CAD annotations permit, real scenes receive synthetic counterparts with similar poses and occlusion patterns but randomized textures, backgrounds, and occasional object substitutions. Derived NOC annotations are filtered through consistency checks (Section III-C).

- **Geometry-consistent correspondence estimation:** The method selects among six pre-rendered CAD views, applies mutual top-\(k\) matching and greedy one-to-one assignment, robustly estimates anisotropic scale, and filters matches through pairwise geometric consensus before RANSAC-based Procrustes registration (Section III-D; Equations 3–8).

- **Residual-based alignment scoring:** A log-determinant information score derived from registration residuals is combined with the retrieval score for ranking alignments during non-maximum suppression (Section III-D; Equations 9–10).

The ScanNet25k and DiffCAD comparisons support improved alignment accuracy under their respective shared protocols, while the component ablations support contributions from mutual matching, geometric consensus, and fit-based scoring. They do not isolate the effect of scaling data volume from changes in dataset diversity or real–synthetic pairing, nor do they establish end-to-end zero-shot performance because retrieval, segmentation, and depth are supplied by upstream systems. ## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** Performance is constrained by CAD retrieval; fully zero-shot retrieval from occluded real observations remains a bottleneck (Sections IV-F and V).
- **Author-stated:** Training and evaluation focus primarily on indoor scenes and common categories, limiting evidence for outdoor and uncommon objects (Section V).
- **Author-stated:** The anisotropic scale estimate can degrade when correspondences do not sufficiently cover all spatial axes; the method therefore shrinks it toward an isotropic estimate (Section III-D).
- **Analyst inference:** “Zero-shot” does not describe the complete system. Alignment depends on externally supplied detection, segmentation, depth, and retrieval, and ScanNet25k uses a depth estimator fine-tuned on its training split (Sections III-B and IV-C).
- **Analyst inference:** The CO3D protocol uses estimated metric scaling, synthetic rectangular occluders, generated CAD pools, and no ground-truth poses. This makes it useful as a stress test but limits direct claims about absolute real-world pose accuracy (Section IV-C).
- **Analyst inference:** The ablations compare correspondence components and inclusion of ScanNet data, but the supplied text does not report controlled experiments separately varying training-set size, category breadth, real/synthetic balance, or paired-scene construction (Section IV-E).
- **Analyst inference:** All hyperparameters were empirically selected, and experiments used one RTX 5090 workstation; no sensitivity analysis or multi-hardware timing study is reported (Section IV-A).
- **Analyst inference:** ZeroCAD is not open source, so its efficiency comparison uses a runtime-only proxy described as a lower bound rather than the original implementation (Section IV-G).

## Open research questions

1. **How well does SUFLECA extend beyond indoor and common-object imagery?** This remains open because those are explicit scope limitations. A next experiment could evaluate unchanged models on outdoor, industrial, and long-tail object benchmarks, stratified by whether categories occurred during weak supervision.

2. **Which aspect of training-data scaling drives the gain?** Dataset size, real/synthetic balance, occlusion diversity, category breadth, and paired rendering change together. A factorial ablation could hold the number of training images fixed while independently varying these factors.

3. **Can an end-to-end zero-shot pipeline retain the reported alignment gains?** Current ScanNet25k evaluation inherits retrieved CADs, masks, boxes, and depth from upstream components. A next experiment could jointly report retrieval recall and final alignment accuracy using only open-set upstream models, with oracle-to-predicted component substitutions.

4. **How robust is geometric consensus to depth errors and poorly observed axes?** The scale procedure explicitly relies on lifted 3D points and acknowledges insufficient axis coverage. Controlled perturbations of depth scale, depth noise, occlusion, viewpoint count, and anisotropy could measure failure thresholds.
