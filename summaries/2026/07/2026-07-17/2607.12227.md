# Rethinking the Evaluation of Harness Evolution for Agents

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-17.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.12227) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12227)


## One-sentence takeaway

Under matched feedback and inference budgets on Terminal-Bench 2.1, automatic harness evolution trails simpler test-time scaling methods and transfers only marginally to held-out tasks.

## Problem and motivation

Automatic harness evolution iteratively changes an LLM agent’s prompts, tools, memory, middleware, and control logic using task feedback. Existing evaluations often optimize and evaluate the harness on the same public benchmark, potentially conflating reusable harness improvements with repeated task-level search and benchmark overfitting. The authors argue that evaluation should include simple search baselines under comparable budgets and use disjoint optimization and evaluation tasks (§1, §3).

## Approach and contributions

- **Unified evaluation protocol:** Formalizes parallel sampling, sequential refinement, harness evolution, and a new task-specific “harness scaling” baseline according to their compute budget, observed feedback, and whether they update trajectories or harnesses (§3; Figure 2).
- **Budget-controlled comparison:** Evaluates all four approaches with a common initial harness and \(K=5\), matching harness-evolution rollouts with \(m=1\) (§4.1).
- **Feedback-sensitive evaluation:** Tests both self-evaluation without unit tests and refinement or oracle selection with unit-test outcomes (§4.2–§4.3).
- **Transfer evaluation:** Separates harness search, validation-based selection, and final testing using a 45/10/34 task split (§4.4).
- **Qualitative analysis:** Examines harness edits and argues that many encode task-specific fixes rather than transferable strategies (§5.1; Appendix B).

The results support the authors’ claim that, in this benchmark and configuration, the tested harness-evolution implementation does not consistently beat simpler uses of equivalent inference effort. The held-out experiment also shows little transfer for the tested models and split. It does not establish that harness evolution is generally ineffective across other algorithms, benchmarks, budgets, models, or harness-sensitive environments. Despite language such as “no significant gains,” the supplied text reports no statistical tests or uncertainty intervals.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated scope concern:** Terminal-Bench may have limited remaining headroom and may be relatively insensitive to harness design because a shell tool and basic prompt already solve many tasks (§5.2).
- **Author-stated capability concern:** Current models may be insufficiently capable of revising an entire harness; stronger priors or models could change the outcome (§5.2).
- **Author-stated mechanism risk:** Persistent task-specific fixes can cause context bloat, while failures requiring deep domain reasoning or changes outside the harness remain unaffected (§5.1).
- **Analyst inference:** The study instantiates automatic evolution with AHE and disables its explore agent. This isolates feedback-driven evolution but limits conclusions about other algorithms and full AHE (§3.4; Appendix A.3).
- **Analyst inference:** Results are averaged over only two independent runs, with no confidence intervals, variance estimates, or significance tests supplied (§4.1).
- **Analyst inference:** Generalization is evaluated using one 45/10/34 split from the same benchmark rather than multiple splits or an external benchmark (§4.4).
- **Analyst inference:** A fixed \(K=5\) budget does not reveal whether relative performance changes along broader compute-scaling curves (§4.1).
- **Analyst inference:** Infrastructure failures count as incorrect, but per-method failure rates are not reported, leaving their effect on comparisons unclear (Appendix A.5).

## Open research questions

1. **When does harness evolution outperform trajectory-level search?** The paper studies one terminal benchmark that may be weakly harness-sensitive. A next experiment could compare the methods across benchmarks stratified by dependence on specialized tools, memory, middleware, and workflows.

2. **Do conclusions change with compute scale?** Only \(K=5\) is reported. A controlled sweep over multiple budgets could measure pass@1, cost, and transfer as scaling curves rather than at one operating point.

3. **Which harness edits generalize?** The qualitative analysis suggests task-specific memorization, but does not quantify edit categories. An ablation could transfer prompt, tool, middleware, and memory edits separately to multiple held-out splits.

4. **How robust is held-out transfer across algorithms and splits?** The reported transfer test uses AHE and one partition. Repeated cross-validation plus additional harness-evolution algorithms and external test suites could distinguish algorithm-specific failure from a broader limitation.
