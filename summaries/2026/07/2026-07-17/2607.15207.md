# BadWAM: When World-Action Models Dream Right but Act Wrong

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-17.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.15207) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15207)


## One-sentence takeaway
BadWAM shows that small black-box visual perturbations can desynchronize world-action models, causing robot task failures while sometimes leaving predicted futures comparatively close to clean rollouts.

## Problem and motivation
The paper addresses adversarial robustness of world-action models (WAMs), which couple robot action generation with future world prediction. The authors argue that this coupling is often treated as a safety or interpretability signal, but they identify a gap: a plausible imagined future does not guarantee that the executed action remains aligned with it. This matters for embodied control because a monitor that checks only future predictions may miss attacks that shift actions while preserving imagination.

## Approach and contributions
1. The authors define **World-Action Drift Attacks**, where bounded visual perturbations induce harmful action changes while potentially preserving predicted futures.
2. They introduce **BadWAM**, a black-box query-based framework with two attack variants: action-only attacks for disruption and imagination-preserving attacks for stealth.
3. They evaluate attacks in closed-loop manipulation on LIBERO and RoboTwin across three WAM-style controllers, reporting task success, action shifts, future drift, transfer, ablations, and simple defenses.

## Evidence and main results

| Finding | Evidence / setting | Location in paper |
|---|---|---|
| Action-only BadWAM sharply reduces LIBERO success for the action-only WAM | Clean 96.5% to attacked 43.1%, a 53.4% drop, with epsilon=0.06 and 8 optimization iterations per replan | Table 1 caption; Section 5.2 |
| Joint and IDM WAMs also degrade under attacks | Joint WAM: action-only attack to 61.5%, imagination-preserving to 63.0%; IDM WAM: 66.1% and 67.0% | Section 5.2 |
| Some tasks become nearly unusable under attack | Action-only WAM has 42.5% of tasks in the 0-25% success bin; joint/IDM have 17.5-25.0% in that bin depending on objective | Figure 5; Section 5.2 |
| BadWAM is stronger than random perturbations | Random noise leaves Joint/IDM success at 71.0% and 75.2%; BadWAM lowers them to 61.5%/63.0% and 66.1%/68.1% | Figure 6; Section 5.2 |
| Spatial and long-horizon LIBERO suites are especially vulnerable | Action-only WAM: Spatial 96.5% to 16.0%, Goal 97.0% to 40.0%, Long-horizon 92.5% to 23.5%; Object remains 93.0% | Figure 8; Section 5.3 |
| Imagination preservation reduces future drift under similar attack resources | Predicted-future distance decreases from 14.01 to 13.04; lower on 39/40 LIBERO tasks, while attacked success rates are close | Figure 12; Section 5.4 |
| Perturbation budget strongly controls attack strength | With epsilon=0.01, success is 95.8% Joint and 98.3% IDM; with epsilon=0.06, 56.7% and 51.7%; with epsilon=0.20, both 0.0% | Figure 14; Section 5.5 |
| Simple detectors have low recall at practical false-positive rate | Augmentation-consistency detector detects 13.4% of attacked joint-WAM replans and 21.4% of attacked IDM-WAM replans at 5% FPR | Figure 15; Section 5.7 |

The evidence supports the authors’ claim that WAM action outputs can be attacked under bounded visual perturbations and that future predictions may not fully reveal the attack. It does not establish robustness under adaptive defenses, physical-world perturbations, or all WAM architectures, and several ablations use a smaller LIBERO subset rather than the full sweep.

## Limitations and validity risks
The authors state that they do not study training-time poisoning, model extraction, reward hacking, prompt injection, physical environment modification, or direct robot-command tampering. They also state that defense results are non-adaptive and should not be read as complete defenses.

Author-stated scope limits include subset evaluation for expensive diagnostics: Appendix A.1 says transfer, defenses, and ablations use a balanced LIBERO subset and that full reruns are planned. Runtime numbers are also described as prototype costs because the implementation saves extensive statistics.

Analyst inference: reproducibility depends on details not fully visible in the supplied text, because several tables are referenced as images or captions without all numeric entries reproduced. The paper also evaluates simulated benchmark protocols in the supplied text; evidence for real-robot physical attack validity is not established.

## Open research questions
1. Can adaptive preprocessing-aware defenses stop BadWAM without reducing clean control? This remains open because Section 5.7 tests only non-adaptive defenses. A next experiment would optimize BadWAM through JPEG, blur, or ensemble transformations.
2. Does action-imagination synchronization monitoring outperform future-plausibility monitoring? The paper argues for synchronization checks but does not present a full defense. A next experiment would train or design monitors using both action and future consistency and evaluate low-FPR recall.
3. How well do these attacks transfer to physical robots? The supplied text evaluates benchmark closed-loop protocols but does not establish physical-world deployment. A next experiment would test camera-space perturbations or patches on real manipulation tasks.
4. Which architectural choices reduce world-action drift? The paper compares three WAM variants but does not isolate architecture-level causes. A next experiment would ablate future-conditioning strength, action heads, and latent sharing while measuring decoupling score.
