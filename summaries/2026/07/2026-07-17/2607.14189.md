# MultiRef-Compass: Towards Comprehensive Evaluation of Multi-Reference-to-Audio-Video Generation

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-17.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.14189) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14189)


## One-sentence takeaway
MultiRef-Compass is a 350-sample benchmark and hybrid evaluation protocol for diagnosing whether audio-video generation systems can preserve, bind, and compose multiple visual/audio references while following multimodal instructions.

## Problem and motivation
The paper addresses evaluation of multi-reference-to-audio-video (MR2AV) generation, where models generate synchronized audio-video content from multiple references plus text instructions. The authors argue that existing text-to-audio-video, single-reference, and audio-video alignment benchmarks do not adequately test cross-reference understanding, multi-entity binding, or natural integration of multiple referenced entities. This matters because practical content creation workflows increasingly use multiple images, objects, scenes, voices, or videos as conditioning inputs, but current models may split identities, swap roles, copy-paste references unnaturally, or misassign sounds.

## Approach and contributions
1. **Benchmark dataset:** MultiRef-Compass contains 350 curated samples: 300 across three main boards and 50 in a challenge board. The boards cover multi-view subject preservation, heterogeneous reference composition, multi-entity binding, and audio-video reference conditioning. Evidence: Abstract; Dataset Statistics; Appendix B.3.

2. **Controlled construction pipeline:** The benchmark uses reusable subject, object, scene, video, and audio asset packs, metadata-guided composition, structured prompt generation, and human review. Evidence: Figure 2; Data Construction; Appendix B.1-B.4.

3. **Evaluation framework:** The authors define four dimensions: Basic Quality, Reference Consistency, Audio-Visual Consistency, and Instruction Following, with 14 sub-metrics overall. The main boards use 13 metrics; Timbre Similarity is evaluated on Board 4. Evidence: Abstract; Metric Dimensions; Main Results.

4. **Hybrid judging protocol:** Automatic metrics are combined with MLLM-as-a-Judge scoring and a rejudging stage intended to improve consistency across model outputs. Evidence: Hybrid Judge Architecture; Rejudging; Appendix D.3-D.4.

5. **Model analysis:** Eight MR2AV systems are evaluated, revealing substantial room for improvement and complementary strengths across models. Evidence: Experimental Settings; Main Results; Further Analysis.

The evidence supports the authors' claim that MultiRef-Compass can expose different failure modes across reference preservation, binding, audio-visual alignment, and instruction following. It does not establish that the benchmark covers all MR2AV use cases, that MLLM judging is unbiased, or that model rankings will remain stable as proprietary APIs change.

## Limitations and validity risks
The authors state that MultiRef-Compass is a controlled diagnostic benchmark and does not cover every creative domain, cultural style, or reference modality. The main evaluation standardizes samples to three reference images, leaving larger reference sets outside scope. Automatic tools are limited for cartoon subjects and faces with large pose or expression changes. The SLS metric is sensitive to stable frontal face visibility and may favor models that generate less dynamic speaking faces. MLLM judging adds cost and may inherit model-specific biases. Proprietary model APIs may change over time, affecting reproducibility.

## Open research questions
1. How well does MR2AV evaluation scale beyond three reference-image slots?  
It remains open because the paper standardizes the main evaluation to three reference images. A next experiment would test models on systematically larger reference sets while holding task type constant.

2. Can lip-sync evaluation become robust to profile faces, occlusion, and dynamic head motion?  
It remains open because the authors report SLS sensitivity to stable frontal faces and uneven retained sample counts. A next experiment would compare current SLS against a synchronization metric designed for multi-speaker dynamic scenes.

3. How much do MLLM judge choices affect scores and rankings?  
It remains open because the paper uses Gemini 3.1 Pro as the judge and notes possible model-specific bias. A next experiment would rerun the MLLM metrics with multiple independent judge models and compare ranking stability.

4. Do the benchmark findings generalize to broader cultural styles and creative domains?  
It remains open because the authors explicitly limit coverage. A next experiment would add domain- or culture-specific asset packs and measure whether failure patterns change.
