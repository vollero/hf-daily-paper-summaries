# SEED: Self-Evolving On-Policy Distillation for Agentic Reinforcement Learning

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-17.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.14777) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14777)


## One-sentence takeaway
SEED improves long-horizon LLM agents by turning completed on-policy trajectories into self-generated hindsight skills and distilling their token-level behavioral effect into the policy during RL.

## Problem and motivation
The paper addresses sparse, delayed, trajectory-level supervision in agentic RL for LLMs. In long-horizon tasks, a final success/failure reward does not identify which intermediate actions, observations, or tool calls were useful or harmful. The authors identify a gap between outcome-level RL signals and token-level policy learning, and argue that completed trajectories contain hindsight information that should evolve with the current policy rather than remain static or be used only as inference-time memory.

## Approach and contributions
1. **Self-evolving hindsight loop:** SEED uses the current policy both to collect trajectories and to analyze completed trajectories into natural-language skills, so the actor and analyzer update together. Evidence: Method §3, Figure 2.

2. **Policy-synchronized on-policy distillation:** SEED re-scores the same sampled action tokens under ordinary and skill-augmented contexts, converts the skill-induced log-probability shift into a confidence-gated OPD loss, and jointly trains it with GRPO. Evidence: §3.3, Eq. 1, joint objective.

3. **Training-only skill use:** Skills guide training but are removed at inference, so deployment uses only the ordinary policy and interaction history. Evidence: §3 and §3.3 inference statement.

4. **Empirical evaluation across agentic domains:** Experiments cover ALFWorld, WebShop, Search-based QA, and a multimodal extension to Sokoban and EZPoints. Evidence: §4.1, Appendix C.3.

The evidence supports the authors’ claim that SEED improves performance and sample efficiency over the reproduced baselines in the reported settings. It does not establish monotonic improvement guarantees; Appendix A explicitly says the theoretical statements do not imply monotonic return improvement and depend on generated skills being behaviorally informative.

## Limitations and validity risks
The authors state that self-evolving supervision can inherit model errors and may plateau below oracle-supervised training; because actor and analyzer share parameters, their blind spots can also be shared. They also note that confidence gating and on-policy alignment do not guarantee semantic correctness. Evidence: Appendix E.

Training cost is a stated practical constraint: SEED adds trajectory analysis and paired scoring under ordinary and skill-augmented contexts, with cost growing with interaction length and multimodal context. Evidence: Appendix E.

Analyst inference: the SFT stage depends on GLM-5.2 as an external analyzer, so reproducibility and behavior may depend on that external model and prompt, although Figure 10 is referenced rather than reproduced in the supplied text. Evidence: §4.1, Appendix B.4.

Analyst inference: comparisons appear broad but are mostly against reproduced baselines under the authors’ implementation; independent replication is not available in the supplied text.

## Open research questions
1. **How robust is SEED when generated hindsight skills are wrong?**  
   This remains open because Appendix E identifies inherited model errors as a risk. A next experiment would inject controlled noisy or adversarial skill annotations and measure performance degradation.

2. **Can verifier-grounded skills reduce self-reinforcing errors?**  
   The paper suggests anchoring skills to verifiable state changes but does not run that variant. A next experiment would compare SEED with and without verifier-checked skill filtering.

3. **How does SEED scale to much longer workflows?**  
   Appendix E names longer-horizon benchmarks as future tests. A next experiment would evaluate on DeepPlanning, Long-Horizon-Terminal-Bench, or OdysseyArena-like settings with rare terminal success.

4. **What is the compute-performance tradeoff of selective analysis?**  
   The authors note trajectory analysis and paired scoring add training cost. A next experiment would analyze only uncertain or novel trajectories and compare cost, sample efficiency, and final performance.

## Confidence and missing information
Confidence: **high** for the paper’s stated method and reported results; **medium** for broader claims about robustness and generality.

Missing information: exact analyzer prompt content is referenced but not included in the supplied text; full code/model release details are not provided; variance/error bars and statistical significance are not reported; compute cost beyond 8 Nvidia A800 80G GPUs is limited; independent replication evidence is absent.
