# AsySplat: Efficient Asymmetric 3D Gaussian Splatting for Long-Sequence Scene Modeling

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-17.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.10995) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.10995)


## One-sentence takeaway

AsySplat separates coarse, compute-heavy geometry reasoning from fine, lightweight appearance modeling, matching or exceeding LongLRM with 98M rather than 142M parameters while reducing training and inference overhead.

## Problem and motivation

Generalizable 3D Gaussian Splatting avoids per-scene optimization, but high-resolution, long-sequence novel-view synthesis creates many fine-grained tokens whose cross-view interactions are expensive. The authors argue that prior stacked architectures waste computation by treating geometry and appearance identically, even though precise geometry is not always necessary for good rendering and appearance can be predicted more easily once depth is available (Sections 1 and 3.1).

The target setting is primarily 32 posed input views at 540×960 resolution. The goal is better parameter and compute efficiency without sacrificing rendering quality (Sections 2 and 4.2).

## Approach and contributions

- **Asymmetric two-branch architecture:** The geometry branch applies extensive inter-frame reasoning to coarse tokens, while the appearance branch preserves detail using fine tokens but restricts processing to within each frame. Approximately 90% of parameters are assigned to geometry (Sections 3.2 and C; Figure 2).

- **Bilateral communication and fusion:** Cross-attention passes fine detail toward geometry and geometric context toward appearance; both outputs are then merged to predict Gaussian attributes (Section 3.2, Equations 3–4).

- **Sparse multi-view attention:** Tokens are locally refined with convolutions, partitioned on a uniform grid, and processed as shorter attention sequences. This permits additional attention layers while reducing training time relative to full attention (Section 3.3; Figure 3).

- **Efficiency-oriented Gaussian prediction:** With patch sizes \(p_c=16\) and \(p_f=8\), the architecture emits fewer Gaussians than LongLRM, reducing post-optimization cost (Sections 3.2 and 4.2; Table 4).

These results establish substantial efficiency gains against LongLRM and strong rendering performance across the reported posed-view datasets. They do not establish superiority over all generalizable 3DGS methods in equivalent long-sequence settings: the principal detailed comparison is LongLRM, while pose-free methods have a materially different input assumption. The “800×” result compares feed-forward prediction with 30k-iteration per-scene optimization and therefore should be interpreted as a system-level runtime contrast, not equal computational procedures.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated limitation:** Visual fidelity can trail optimization-based approaches, particularly in LPIPS, because the feed-forward model lacks error-driven densification for recovering fine detail (Supplement D; also Section 4.3).

- **Author-stated scope boundary:** AsySplat assumes camera poses, so direct comparison with pose-free approaches such as AnySplat is considered mismatched by the authors (Supplement B).

- **Author-stated measurement caveats:** Reported TFLOPs exclude splatting computation. Inference time removes the single longest DL3DV-140 observation as an outlier. Some low-resolution training-overhead measurements average only the first 20 iterations from random initialization because LongLRM checkpoints were unavailable (Supplement A).

- **Analyst inference:** Evidence for the claim that precise geometry is unnecessary is task-specific: Table 6c shows equal PSNR despite weaker depth metrics, but this does not demonstrate suitability for geometry-sensitive uses such as accurate surfaces or meshes.

- **Analyst inference:** Main ablations use 256×256 models trained for only 10k iterations because of resource constraints, whereas headline evaluations use 540×960. Their quantitative effects may not transfer exactly to the full training regime (Section 4.4).

- **Analyst inference:** The paper provides no variance, confidence intervals, or repeated-run statistics, leaving sensitivity to training randomness unclear.

## Open research questions

1. **Can error-driven densification close the LPIPS gap without forfeiting efficiency?** The authors identify absent densification as a source of weaker fine-detail recovery. A next experiment could add a bounded, learned densification stage and measure LPIPS, Gaussian count, memory, and runtime against the current model.

2. **How far does asymmetric scaling extend beyond 32 views?** The main evaluation centers on 32-view sequences, with only a limited 64-view comparison against a pose-free model. A controlled sweep over 16, 32, 64, and more posed views against LongLRM could test quality and compute scaling.

3. **Can the approach work without known poses?** Pose dependence prevents equivalent comparison with pose-free systems. A plausible experiment would add a pose-estimation front end and separately report pose error, reconstruction quality, memory, and latency.

4. **What geometry precision is required for non-rendering tasks?** The evidence supports NVS despite coarser depth, not accurate surface recovery. Evaluating depth, point-cloud, and mesh metrics on geometry-focused benchmarks would clarify the boundary.
