# KeyFrame-Compass: Towards Comprehensive Evaluation of Keyframe-Conditioned Video Generation

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-17.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.14202) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14202)


## One-sentence takeaway
KeyFrame-Compass is a 386-case benchmark and automated evaluation framework for testing whether video generators can reproduce ordered keyframe sequences at intended times while maintaining general video quality.

## Problem and motivation
The paper addresses evaluation of keyframe-conditioned video generation, where a model receives ordered reference images plus a prompt and must synthesize a coherent video that realizes those visual anchors. The authors argue existing video benchmarks emphasize overall quality, prompt following, or single-image conditioning, but do not diagnose whether a complete keyframe sequence is reproduced with correct appearance, order, timing, persistence, and transitions (Sec. 1; Sec. 2.2; Table 1). This matters because multi-keyframe workflows are increasingly used for creator control, and failures can arise from distinct capabilities rather than one aggregate alignment score (Sec. 1).

## Approach and contributions
1. **Benchmark asset:** KeyFrame-Compass contains 386 curated cases spanning three application domains, two video structures, two prompt granularities, two conditioning formats, and four keyframe counts (Abstract; Sec. 3.1; Table 2).
2. **Keyframe response evaluation:** The authors define six metrics: Hit Rate, Keyframe Similarity, Keyframe Order Consistency, Keyframe Position Accuracy, Persistence Around Keyframe, and Response Uniqueness (Sec. 3.3; Fig. 2; Fig. 4).
3. **General quality evaluation:** The framework combines MLLM checklist judgments with specialized perception models for visual quality, spatiotemporal coherence, instruction adherence, and audio-visual coordination (Sec. 3.3).
4. **Empirical analysis:** The paper evaluates nine representative systems and reports trade-offs between keyframe fidelity and natural video synthesis, degradation under denser constraints, and storyboard-grid failures in several open-source models (Sec. 4.1-4.3; Tables 3-5).

The evidence supports the authors’ main claim that different systems trade off visual keyframe preservation, temporal organization, and general video quality. It does not establish broad generality beyond the evaluated models, benchmark domains, prompt templates, and automated-judge setup.

## Limitations and validity risks
The authors state several model-side limitations revealed by the benchmark: dense keyframe constraints reduce visual instruction adherence, transitions can be abrupt or implausible, storyboard-grid inputs are poorly understood by many open-source models, and one-take continuity remains difficult (Sec. 4.3; Sec. 5).

Analyst inference: the benchmark’s conclusions depend heavily on automated MLLM judging and perception-model thresholds. The paper provides calibration, repeated-run stability, and human-alignment evidence (Appendix B-D), but human validation covers 60 segment-specific cases and five models, not the full benchmark. Long-video evaluation is also narrow: only Kling-3.0-Omni and Seedance 2.0 are evaluated in agent-mode workflows (Sec. 4.1; Table 4b).

## Open research questions
1. **How can models preserve keyframes while generating natural transitions?** The benchmark shows LTX-2.3 can reproduce keyframes but struggles with dynamics; a next experiment could train or evaluate transition-specific objectives between adjacent keyframes (Sec. 4.3; Fig. 6a-b).
2. **Can open-source models learn robust storyboard-grid parsing?** Several open-source systems fail to decompose grids into temporal sequences; a next experiment could compare grid-specific instruction tuning against multi-image conditioning on the same cases (Sec. 4.3; Fig. 6c).
3. **What evaluation design best balances semantic compliance and visual grounding?** Gemini-Omni-Flash follows instructions while restaging visual content; a next experiment could vary prompts to explicitly weight exact visual anchoring versus semantic restaging (Sec. 4.3; App. A.1).
4. **How reliable are automated judges across broader domains and failure modes?** The paper reports stability and human alignment, but on limited validation subsets; a next experiment could expand human comparisons to all domains, prompt modes, and long-video settings (Sec. 4.4; Appendix C-D).
