# RynnBrain 1.1: Towards More Capable and Generalizable Embodied Foundation Model

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-21.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.17977) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.17977)


## One-sentence takeaway

RynnBrain 1.1 combines embodied multimodal pretraining, native 3D and contact grounding, and a masked cross-embodiment VLA action space, with reported gains from model scaling and joint robot training.

## Problem and motivation

Robots need visual-language models that understand 3D structure, reason across time and viewpoints, ground instructions spatially, and transfer that understanding into executable actions. The authors identify two gaps in RynnBrain 1.0: its outputs were not sufficiently aligned with manipulation, and its value as an initialization for downstream vision-language-action (VLA) policies was not established (Section 1).

## Approach and contributions

- **Scaled embodied model family:** Decoder-only vision-language models at 2B, 9B, and 122B-A10B scales, initialized from matched Qwen3.5 variants and trained with a unified autoregressive interface for text, points, boxes, and trajectories (Sections 2.1 and 3.1).
- **Manipulation-oriented grounding:** Contact-point prediction represents a grasp using a 2D center and in-plane gripper angle; native language-conditioned 3D grounding predicts nine camera-frame box parameters for the 2B and 9B models (Sections 3.1–3.2).
- **Cross-embodiment VLA:** A flow-matching policy predicts action chunks in an 81-dimensional, body-part-structured space, masking dimensions unavailable on each embodiment (Sections 4.1–4.2; Figure 3).
- **Real-robot study:** The authors compare RynnBrain and Qwen initializations under matched post-training and test separate versus joint multi-task, multi-embodiment training (Sections 4.5 and 5.6).

The evidence supports strong benchmark performance, useful embodied initialization under one controlled Qwen comparison, and promising joint training on the evaluated tasks. It does not establish statistical significance, causal attribution to individual pretraining additions, unseen-embodiment transfer, or general manipulation reliability beyond the reported platforms and trials.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** Contact-point prediction lacks a standardized functional-validity metric and is evaluated only qualitatively (Section 5.5).
- **Author-stated:** A contact point and planar angle are not a complete grasp; depth, approach direction, aperture, collisions, and kinematics remain the downstream policy’s responsibility (Section 3.2).
- **Author-stated:** WildDet3D’s lifted annotations retain residual noise, while FoundationPose has only 928 object instances despite precise synthetic geometry (Section 3.2).
- **Analyst inference:** No ablation isolates the effects of contact supervision, 3D supervision, updated data, or architectural components; improvements over RynnBrain 1.0 therefore cannot be assigned to a particular change.
- **Analyst inference:** Joint-training evidence covers three quantitative tasks on two platforms, so the claim of cross-embodiment benefit remains preliminary. Unitree G1 appears in additional evaluations but not Table 6’s joint-training comparison.
- **Analyst inference:** Different VLA baselines use method-specific chunk lengths, weakening strict comparisons with GR00T N1.7 and π0.5; the Qwen comparison is better controlled (Section 5.6.1).
- **Analyst inference:** The supplied text does not report confidence intervals, seeds, significance tests, evaluation-set sizes for the static benchmarks, or detailed success criteria.
- **Analyst inference:** Several evaluation benchmarks share names or sources with training-mixture components; the text does not explain split separation or contamination controls.

## Open research questions

1. **Which new supervision produces the downstream VLA gains?** The paper changes several data and capability components simultaneously. A factorial ablation of contact, 3D, localization, and planning supervision under fixed compute could measure each contribution.
2. **Does joint training transfer to a held-out embodiment?** All reported platforms contribute to the described training/deployment setup. Train on two embodiments and fine-tune or evaluate on a third held-out robot, comparing masked unified actions against separate action heads.
3. **How well do contact predictions correlate with execution success?** Figure 7 is qualitative and the representation omits several grasp variables. Execute multiple predicted contacts per object and compare contact error, functional equivalence, and physical grasp success.
4. **Are scaling gains separable from data-mixture differences?** The text says model variants have corresponding mixtures, while only 2B and 9B receive native 3D data. Train matched scales on an identical token-level mixture and compute budget schedule.

## Confidence and missing information

**Confidence: medium.** The supplied text contains extensive method descriptions and numerical results, but omits model artifact details and licensing, parameter-count conventions beyond the names, pretraining token counts and mixture weights, compute cost, benchmark metric definitions and test-set sizes, contamination controls, quantitative contact evaluation, statistical uncertainty, demonstration counts, and complete per-task results for the additional Unitree G1 and other qualitative robot tasks.
