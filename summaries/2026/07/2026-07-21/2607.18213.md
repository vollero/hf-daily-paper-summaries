# SWE-Pruner Pro: The Coder LLM Already Knows What to Prune

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-21.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.18213) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.18213)


## One-sentence takeaway

SWE-Pruner Pro extracts line-level pruning decisions from a coding agent’s frozen hidden states, reducing end-to-end token use by as much as 39.4% while generally preserving quality, though benefits vary by backbone and workload.

## Problem and motivation

Multi-turn coding agents accumulate large, repetitive tool outputs that increase cost and can degrade long-context performance. Existing compressors either apply task-agnostic signals such as perplexity or require a separate pruning model and an explicit goal-hint query each turn. The authors argue that both reconstruct relevance externally even though the agent’s own representations may already encode which lines matter (§1–2).

A frozen-state probe supports this premise: on held-out trajectories, a logistic regression over Qwen3-Coder-Next’s last-layer line representations achieves AUC 0.83 and best F1 0.63, versus a reported majority-class F1 upper bound of 0.46 (§2, Figure 2).

## Approach and contributions

- **Internal pruning signal:** The paper shows that keep/prune labels are partly separable in the frozen agent backbone’s last-layer representations (§2, Figure 2).

- **In-prefill pruning head:** SWE-Pruner Pro applies a small nonlinear classifier to token representations already computed while prefilling a tool response. Token predictions are combined by majority vote into line-level decisions; pruned output replaces the raw response in subsequent turns (§3.1–3.2, Figure 3, Equations 2–3).

- **Length-aware behavior:** A learned embedding of the response’s line-count bucket shifts hidden states so pruning can be more conservative for short outputs (§3.2, Equation 1; Appendix B).

- **Per-sample balanced focal loss:** Keep and prune losses are balanced within each sample, intended to prevent unusual keep ratios from being overwhelmed by the corpus-wide class distribution (§3.3, Equations 4–6).

- **Systems integration and evaluation:** Separate heads are trained from cached features for two frozen open-weight backbones, with an in-engine implementation that avoids transferring full hidden-state tensors. Evaluation covers four multi-turn benchmarks and six prior pruning approaches where applicable (§4; Appendix E).

The results establish that internal representations can support useful pruning and that SWE-Pruner Pro offers a favorable quality–token trade-off across the reported read-only benchmark cells. They do not establish universal quality preservation: SWE-Bench outcomes differ by backbone, and pruning can increase API calls or even total input tokens. Statistical uncertainty, repeated-run variance, and significance tests are not reported in the supplied text.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** The approach requires access to internal hidden states, so evaluation is limited to open-weight models; each new backbone requires head retraining (Limitations).

- **Author-stated:** Agent-task evaluation is Python-centric. Oolong provides a natural-language check, but broader programming-language coverage remains future work (Limitations; Appendix A, Table 5).

- **Author-stated:** Deployment should be validated per backbone, especially in safety-critical settings, because aggressive pruning may cause failures not captured by the benchmarks (Ethical Considerations).

- **Analyst inference:** Labels and some quality evaluations depend on model judgments—Claude Sonnet 4.6 supplies training labels and GPT-5.4-mini judges SWE-QA and the ablation skeletons—creating possible judge bias or correlated preferences (§3.3, §4, Appendix A).

- **Analyst inference:** The training data is heavily concentrated in Python and CLI/shell content—approximately 83% combined—which may limit claims of language-agnostic behavior (Appendix A, Table 5).

- **Analyst inference:** The latency evidence is narrow: 16 replayed MiMo-V2-Flash trajectories do not establish end-to-end latency across both backbones or all benchmarks (§5.3). Calling the added inference cost “negligible” in the conclusion is stronger than the reported 15.0% aggregate and 34.8% p95 overhead directly supports.

- **Analyst inference:** The probe and head targets come from the same LLM-labeling protocol, so separability establishes recovery of those annotations rather than an independent ground truth of relevance (§2; Appendix A).

- **Analyst inference:** No confidence intervals, multi-seed rollout results, or statistical significance tests are supplied, leaving uncertainty around modest changes such as ±0.1 judge points or a few SWE-Bench resolves.

## Open research questions

1. **Does the method generalize to substantially different languages and tools?**  
   The benchmarks are Python-centric and the corpus is dominated by Python and CLI data. A next experiment could train and evaluate per-language heads on held-out Java, C++, JavaScript, and non-shell tool outputs.

2. **Can one pruning head transfer across backbones?**  
   The current method requires per-backbone resizing and retraining. A next experiment could compare a shared projected representation space, direct head transfer, and lightweight calibration across multiple model families.

3. **What is the true end-to-end latency and cost trade-off?**  
   The reported latency replay is small, while API calls sometimes increase. A next experiment could measure wall time, energy, and monetary cost over complete benchmark trajectories under matched serving load.

4. **How robust is pruning when mistakes have high downstream cost?**  
   Aggregate task metrics may hide rare irreversible removals. A next experiment could stratify results by response length, tool type, pruning rate, and error severity, including safety-critical or dependency-sensitive tasks.
