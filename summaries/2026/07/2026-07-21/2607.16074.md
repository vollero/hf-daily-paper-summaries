# JoyNexus: Service-Oriented Multi-Tenant Post-Training for VLA Models

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-21.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.16074) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16074)


## One-sentence takeaway

JoyNexus unifies multi-tenant VLA fine-tuning, reinforcement learning, and evaluation over shared resident model services, reducing matched-workload aggregate GPU time by 28.3% while preserving tenant-specific training state.

## Problem and motivation

VLA post-training must accommodate heterogeneous robot embodiments, simulators, data schemas, and objectives across SFT, RL, and evaluation. Existing dedicated-resource and batch-submission services leave users responsible for complex infrastructure and can waste accelerators during bursty workloads, simulator interaction, data loading, or synchronization. The authors identify a gap in service-oriented post-training systems: prior frameworks primarily target language models or text agents rather than the environment sessions, action schemas, rollout records, evaluation protocols, and policy synchronization required by VLAs (Sections 1 and 2.1).

## Approach and contributions

- **Unified service architecture:** JoyNexus separates a Training Model Service, Inference Model Service, and Environment Service, coordinated by a Master Service and independent training and inference queues (Sections 4.1–4.2; Figures 3–5).
- **Multi-tenant state separation:** Compatible tenants share memory-resident base models while retaining private action modules, optimizer states, checkpoints, rollout records, and policy versions (Sections 4.1.2 and 4.2).
- **Composable workflows:** High-level SFT, RL, rollout, and evaluation operations compile into lower-level APIs, allowing custom algorithms without requiring tenants to manage placement and distributed execution (Section 4.1.2; Figure 4).
- **Group batching:** Compatible heterogeneous requests are canonicalized for one shared frozen-backbone forward pass, then split for tenant-specific decoding or optimization (Section 4.3; Figure 8).
- **Systems evaluation:** The paper evaluates matched multi-tenant workloads, shared-service utilization, group-batched forward efficiency, and per-tenant training behavior (Section 5).

The evidence supports improved aggregate resource efficiency for one matched mixed-workload deployment and shows that shared-forward batching can retain similar training-curve behavior in a controlled experiment. It does not establish end-to-end group-batched training speedup, identical learned policies, task-success improvements, general performance isolation, or security isolation. The authors also acknowledge that shared execution may increase an individual tenant’s wall-clock time (Section 5.1).

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated scope:** Group-batching speedups cover only the shareable forward stage, excluding tenant-private backward passes and optimizer updates (Section 5.2).
- **Author-stated isolation boundary:** The loss experiment tests separation of model and optimizer state, not security or performance isolation (Section 5.2).
- **Author-stated operational gaps:** Runtime routes and resource assignments remain largely fixed; adaptive routing, pricing, service guarantees, safe extension points, sandboxing, and attack detection are future work (Section 6).
- **Author-stated schema risk:** Canonicalization is valid only when masks, action dimensions, camera slots, and state conventions preserve tenant semantics (Section 6).
- **Analyst inference:** The principal efficiency result comes from one 8-GPU topology and a trace-matched replay baseline, so generalization to other hardware, arrival patterns, model sizes, or tenant mixtures is untested.
- **Analyst inference:** Similar loss curves alone do not demonstrate equivalent final policy quality or simulator success; no downstream task-performance comparison is reported.
- **Analyst inference:** Absolute utilization figures and numerical values from Figures 10–11 are missing from the supplied text, limiting independent quantitative assessment.
- **Analyst inference:** No ablation isolates the gains from cross-tenant RL overlap, SFT gap filling, queue policies, and shared model residency.

## Open research questions

1. **How does JoyNexus affect per-tenant latency and fairness under contention?** The paper notes that sharing can lengthen individual workloads but reports aggregate efficiency rather than latency distributions. A next experiment could vary priorities and arrival rates while measuring median and tail completion time per tenant.

2. **Does group batching preserve final policy quality?** Closely tracking training losses do not establish equivalent robot-task success. A controlled run could train grouped and serial policies to completion and compare evaluation success across LIBERO, CALVIN, and ManiSkill seeds.

3. **How much does each scheduling mechanism contribute?** The mixed experiment combines asynchronous rollouts, RL prioritization, SFT prefetching, and shared services. An ablation could enable these mechanisms incrementally and report GPU time, utilization, and staleness.

4. **Can compatibility checks safely support more heterogeneous schemas?** The current method requires a compatible frozen prefix and semantics-preserving canonicalization. A next experiment could systematically vary cameras, action dimensions, state layouts, and padding rules while checking outputs and downstream performance.
