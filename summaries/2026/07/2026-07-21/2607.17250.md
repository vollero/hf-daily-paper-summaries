# EvolvingWorld: An Open-Schema Framework for Co-Evolving Role-Play Agents and World Model in Interactive Literary World

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-21.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.17250) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.17250)


## One-sentence takeaway

EvolvingWorld couples persistently updated, open-schema character profiles with global and location/entity world states, and the authors report substantially better long-horizon literary simulation than fixed-schema BookWorld and role-play-only training.

## Problem and motivation

The paper addresses long-horizon simulation of literary worlds, where character beliefs, motivations, relationships, locations, objects, and broader conditions must evolve coherently across scenes. The authors argue that existing systems emphasize static persona imitation, isolated scenes, or manually defined worlds and update only limited predefined fields. This prevents interactions from producing persistent, mutually consistent changes in both characters and their environment (§1; Table 1).

The identified gap is an open-schema mechanism that discovers book-specific state dimensions and couples character evolution to global, location, and entity-level world evolution (§1, §3.1).

## Approach and contributions

- **Coupled simulation architecture:** A Character Agent generates multi-character interactions, motivations, and persistent profiles, while an LLM-based World Model plans scenes and maintains global and location/entity states (§3.1–§3.2).
- **Open-schema state representation:** Profile and world-state fields are inferred for each literary work rather than restricted to universal predefined slots (§3.1; Figure 4).
- **Multi-timescale character evolution:** A hidden tracker accumulates weak evidence before it is strong enough to modify persistent profile fields (§3.1.1).
- **Seven supervised tasks:** `scene_cast`, `location_scenario`, `motivation_update`, `next_character`, `interaction_gen`, `world_update`, and `character_update form a repeated scene-level transition (§3.2).
- **Dataset and benchmark:** The authors derive 138,596 training samples and 222 test snapshots from 57 chronologically narrated public-domain books, and propose trajectory-level evaluation over 10 dimensions and 20 metrics (§3.3–§3.4; Tables 6, 8, and 10).

These results support the authors’ claim that explicit state evolution and task-specific supervision improve performance on their benchmark, including matched-framework and component ablations. They do not establish superiority for unrestricted interactive fiction, modern narratives, non-chronological stories, or real users. Most scores come from LLM judges, and cross-judge tables show that absolute values can change substantially even when leading-model rankings are broadly similar.

## Limitations and validity risks

**Author-stated limitations:**

- The World Model maintains one objective state shared by all characters, excluding subjective perceptions and imperfect memories (Limitations).
- Context limits force it to track only selected important entities rather than the entire environment (Limitations).
- The benchmark contains public-domain classic books and does not cover modern novels, games, or user-created worlds (Limitations).
- Source texts and LLM-generated annotations may contain or amplify harmful stereotypes and biases (Risks).
- The resources are restricted to scientific, non-commercial use (Data Use Restrictions).

**Analyst inferences supported by the text:**

- The benchmark depends heavily on synthetic extraction, refinement, scene enhancement, and state annotation by Gemini-2.5-Pro, creating possible extraction-model bias (§3.3; Appendix C).
- Main evaluation depends on Claude-4.6-Sonnet as an LLM judge. Tables 18–20 demonstrate sensitivity of absolute scores to judge choice, so small score differences should be interpreted cautiously.
- The BookWorld comparison uses EvolvingWorld’s test set and only shared metrics; this supports an in-benchmark framework comparison, not comprehensive superiority on BookWorld’s own evaluation setting (§4.3; Tables 4–5).
- Chronological narration and access to later scenes are construction assumptions; applicability to nonlinear or genuinely open-ended worlds remains untested (§3.3; Appendix C.1).
- Although human evaluation is described, its agreement statistics and sample size lie in omitted material and cannot be assessed here.

## Open research questions

1. **Can characters maintain distinct perceived worlds and fallible memories?** The current framework assumes a shared objective state. A next experiment could add per-character belief states and test behavior under conflicting observations.

2. **Does the method generalize beyond public-domain chronological novels?** Modern fiction, games, user-authored worlds, and nonlinear narratives are absent. A next experiment could construct a licensed cross-domain test set without look-ahead chronological supervision.

3. **How robust are conclusions to annotation and judge models?** Extraction relies on one named LLM, while absolute evaluation scores vary across judges. A next experiment could independently reconstruct subsets with multiple extraction models and use blinded human adjudication.

4. **Can richer world representations remain efficient over longer trajectories?** Context limits currently restrict entity coverage. A next experiment could compare the flat in-context representation with retrieved hierarchical state storage over trajectories longer than 20 scenes.
