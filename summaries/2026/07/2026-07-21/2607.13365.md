# DiffGI: Differentiable Geometry Images for High-Fidelity Thin-Shell 3D Generation

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-21.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.13365) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13365)


## One-sentence takeaway

DiffGI combines a continuous 2D TSDF geometry image with differentiable Marching Squares, enabling compact, UV-compatible generation of thin-shell and open-boundary meshes with substantially improved boundary reconstruction.

## Problem and motivation

Implicit volumetric 3D representations commonly favor watertight topology, which can introduce thickness, merge front and back surfaces, and lose open boundaries in garments and frame-like objects. They also do not naturally provide the UV coordinates needed for simulation and material editing.

Geometry images preserve surfaces and UV parameterization, but prior variants use binary occupancy masks whose boundaries become stair-stepped or disappear after downsampling; their mesh-recovery heuristics are also typically outside the differentiable training pipeline. The authors seek a surface-centric representation that retains subpixel boundaries, supports end-to-end geometric supervision, and avoids the cubic cost of 3D grids (Introduction; Sections 2.2–2.3).

## Approach and contributions

- **Continuous geometry representation:** DiffGI replaces binary chart occupancy with a 2D TSDF paired with a three-channel position map. It computes the TSDF on a packed \(1024\times1024\) UV grid, truncates distances at 15 pixels, and downsamples the resulting representation to \(256\times256\times4\) (Section 3.1).

- **Differentiable surface extraction:** Differentiable Marching Squares locates TSDF zero crossings by analytical linear interpolation and samples the position map to obtain 3D vertices. This permits surface-rendering losses to propagate into the TSDF and position predictions (Section 3.2; Equation 1).

- **Geometry-aware compression:** DiffGI-VAE compresses the representation to \(32\times32\times4\), jointly optimizing position, TSDF, KL, and differentiably rendered normal-map losses (Section 3.3; Equations 2–3).

- **Conditional generation:** Models trained in the compact latent space support label-conditioned objects, single-image-conditioned geometry, and occupancy-conditioned garment generation using DiT or a lightweight U-Net, depending on the task (Section 3.4).

## Evidence and main results

| Finding | Evidence / setting | Location in paper |
|---|---|---|
| TSDF sharply improves compressed reconstruction over occupancy | On GarmageSet without normal loss, switching occupancy to TSDF reduces CD from \(1.503\times10^{-3}\) to \(0.595\times10^{-3}\) and JSD from \(4.539\times10^{-3}\) to \(2.169\times10^{-3}\). | Section 4.3; Table 2 |
| Normal supervision adds complementary gains | In the TSDF setting, normal loss raises NC from 0.921 to 0.961 and lowers CD further to \(0.461\times10^{-3}\). | Section 4.3; Table 2; Supplement S7 |
| Pretraining affects convergence more than final fidelity | On GarmageSet with identical architecture and DMS extraction, random versus SD1.5 initialization gives CD 0.47 versus 0.46 and NC 0.96 for both. | Section 4.3; Table S1; Figure S2 |
| Image-conditioned garment generation leads the reported geometric metrics | On GarmageSet, DiffGI reports CD \(1.35\times10^{-2}\), F1 0.48, HD \(8.42\times10^{-2}\), and BCD \(2.91\times10^{-2}\), using about 23K vertices. | Section 4.5.2; Table 5 |
| Label-conditioned distribution quality improves over Omages | With 512 generated samples per ABO class, P-FID reductions are reported as 50% for Chair and 49% for Lamp; Lamp P-KID is 0.07 versus 0.26. | Section 4.5.1; Table 4 |
| Compact latents reduce inference requirements | Image-conditioned generation takes approximately 1.2 seconds on an RTX 4070 using 3.22 GB VRAM and approximately 8.5 seconds on a MacBook M4 CPU; cited comparators include TRELLIS at up to 16.28 GB and Omages at 52 seconds per sample under their recommended settings. | Section 4.4; Table 3 |
| TSDF’s raw-resolution advantage is concentrated at low resolution | Without VAE compression, TSDF has lower HD at resolutions 64 and 128, while TSDF and occupancy approach similar error beyond 256; the advantage is stronger on GarmageSet than ABO. | Supplement S1; Figure S1 |
| UV augmentation enables higher-capacity training | On the four-class ABO setting, augmentation expands 3,800 meshes to 500K layout variants; DiT-Base is reported unstable without it and convergent with it. | Supplement S3; Figures S3–S4 |

The evidence supports the authors’ central claim that continuous boundary encoding is particularly useful under low-resolution compression, while differentiable normal supervision provides an additional benefit. It also demonstrates competitive conditional generation in the tested garment and furniture settings.

It does not establish universal superiority over general-purpose 3D generators: the authors explicitly note differing training scopes in Table 5. Several headline comparisons cannot be independently inspected from the supplied text because the numerical contents of Tables 1–5 are embedded as images, leaving only values repeated in the surrounding prose.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** Linear TSDF interpolation can round extremely sharp mechanical edges (Section 5).
- **Author-stated:** The framework generates geometry but not RGB textures or PBR material attributes (Section 5).
- **Author-stated:** Charts are reconstructed independently, which can create visible cross-chart seams and complicate physical simulation; these discontinuities are not captured by the reported reconstruction and generation metrics (Section 5; Figure 6).
- **Author-stated:** Fixed handling of ambiguous Marching Squares saddle cases may incorrectly split narrow bridge-like structures, although the authors report that such cases are rare at \(256\times256\) with chart padding (Section 3.2; Supplement S4).
- **Analyst inference:** Quantitative evaluation is limited to ABO and GarmageSet; transfer to real WARDROBE photographs is qualitative, so real-image generalization is not numerically established (Sections 4.1 and 4.5.2; Figure S6).
- **Analyst inference:** Baselines use different native extractors and, in the image-conditioned comparison, different training regimes. The paper acknowledges these differences, but they constrain causal and universal comparisons (Sections 4.2 and 4.5.2; Supplement S6).
- **Analyst inference:** The ablation isolates TSDF and normal loss, but the supplied text gives no direct ablation of DMS against another differentiable extractor while holding the representation fixed.
- **Analyst inference:** A project page is identified, but the supplied text does not explicitly confirm availability of code, weights, preprocessing tools, or full training configurations.

## Open research questions

1. **Can cross-chart continuity be enforced without losing independent chart flexibility?** Seams remain outside the current metrics and threaten simulation validity. A next experiment could add paired boundary constraints and report seam distance plus cloth-simulation stability.

2. **Can feature-preserving extraction retain extreme sharp edges?** Linear interpolation produces localized rounding. A controlled comparison against differentiable dual-contouring-style extraction on mechanical shapes could measure edge-angle error and HD.

3. **How robust is image conditioning on real photographs?** WARDROBE evidence is qualitative and zero-shot. A held-out real-image benchmark with paired or independently scanned geometry could quantify CD, silhouette agreement, boundary accuracy, and failure rates.

4. **How much of the generation improvement comes from the representation versus augmentation and backbone choices?** The reconstruction ablation covers representation and normal loss, while the generator also changes architecture and greatly expands UV layouts. A factorial experiment holding backbone, augmentation count, and latent representation fixed in turn would separate their effects.
