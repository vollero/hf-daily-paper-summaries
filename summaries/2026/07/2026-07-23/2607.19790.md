# Trace: A Taxonomy-Guided Environment for Multidomain Visual Reasoning

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-23.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.19790) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19790)


## One-sentence takeaway

Trace combines a program-centered taxonomy with an executable visual generator–renderer–verifier pipeline, and RLVR on 64,000 generated instances improves two Qwen2.5-VL models across a 24-benchmark external evaluation suite.

## Problem and motivation

Visual RL with verifiable rewards needs training data that are visually broad, exactly scored, controllable, and reproducible. The authors argue that existing dataset mixtures inherit inconsistent task definitions and reward formats, while procedural environments usually cover only one scene grammar or a few related objectives. Consequently, breadth, controlled variation, and exact verification rarely coexist in one coherent environment (Sections 1–2).

## Approach and contributions

- **Program-centered taxonomy:** Trace separates a visual **scene grammar**, an executable **task program**, an answer schema, and a reward contract. Bounded query arguments and generation parameters vary instances without artificially creating new tasks (Section 3; Figures 2–3).
- **Executable environment:** A shared semantic state produces the image, prompt, typed answer, verifier state, and replayable trace. The environment contains 1,000 tasks, 277 scene grammars, and 11 visual domains (Section 4).
- **Controlled variation and validation:** Semantic controls can change answers, while render-only controls must preserve answer-relevant relations. Retained instances require a unique answer, state-consistent prompt and image, deterministic replay, and scene-specific visibility checks (Sections 4.3–4.4; Appendix A.3).
- **Transfer study:** The authors apply GRPO-based RLVR to Qwen2.5-VL-3B-Instruct and 7B-Instruct using the same 64,000 instances, then evaluate both on held-out Trace data and 24 external benchmarks (Section 5).

These results establish improvement over each model’s base checkpoint within Trace and across the selected external suite. They do not isolate which domains, operation families, rendering controls, or sampling decisions caused the gains. Nor do checkpoint comparisons establish superiority of Trace’s data or method independently of unmatched training recipes and compute.

## Limitations and validity risks

**Author-stated limitations:**

- The taxonomy is human-designed, task boundaries require judgment, and 1,000 tasks do not exhaust visual reasoning (Section 7).
- No mixture ablations attribute transfer gains to domains, operation families, sampling choices, or rendering controls (Section 7).
- Only one training run was performed at each scale; reported decoding-seed variability is not independent training variability (Section 7).
- Comparisons with other RLVR checkpoints are descriptive because their data, optimization, and compute differ (Sections 5.3 and 7).
- The external suite cannot establish transfer to every natural-image distribution, and some benchmarks use model-based graders (Section 7; Appendix B.2).
- Procedural renderers may contain synthetic regularities or occasional ambiguities; structural validation cannot guarantee equal legibility or semantic clarity for every instance (Sections 4.4 and 7).
- The operation families do not provide a cross-domain measure of difficulty (Section 7).

**Analyst inference:** Because both scales use one fixed mixture, a single shuffled training pass, and common hyperparameters, the evidence does not reveal whether results are robust to dataset resampling, longer training, or scale-specific optimization. This follows from Sections 5.1–5.2 and is not an author-tested conclusion.

## Open research questions

1. **Which parts of Trace drive external transfer?** This remains open because the paper trains only on the complete mixture. A next experiment could remove or reweight individual domains, operation families, and rendering controls while holding instance count and compute constant.

2. **Does adaptive sampling outperform uniform task sampling?** Trace samples tasks uniformly, but task difficulty varies and is not modeled across domains. A controlled comparison could test uniform, difficulty-aware, and learning-progress-based curricula under equal rollout budgets.

3. **How stable are the gains across independent training runs?** One run per scale cannot measure optimization variance. Repeating each configuration with several training seeds would separate training instability from decoding and evaluation-item uncertainty.

4. **How much transfer reaches natural-image settings rather than benchmark-adjacent synthetic structure?** The suite is broad but not exhaustive, and procedural images may contain distinctive regularities. A next experiment could evaluate matched reasoning programs across procedural, photorealistic, and natural-image inputs.
