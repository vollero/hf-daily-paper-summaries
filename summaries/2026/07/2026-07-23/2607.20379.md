# Train the Model, Not the Reader: Decodability Supervision for Verifiable Activation Explanations

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-23.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.20379) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.20379)


## One-sentence takeaway

Reconstruction quality does not verify individual activation-explanation claims, while RECAP makes designated internal content independently probe-decodable by supervising the target model with external targets.

## Problem and motivation

Natural-language autoencoders treat successful reconstruction of an activation from a textual explanation as evidence of faithfulness. The authors identify a structural gap: reconstruction rewards information sufficient to recover the activation but does not penalize individual false claims when changing them leaves reconstruction unchanged (§§1–2.1).

They demonstrate two failure modes: a released Qwen-2.5-7B system reconstructs mainly from gist while showing little measurable dependence on specific claims (§3.1), and synthetic co-trained verbalizer/reconstructor pairs develop false, reconstruction-relevant private codes (§3.2). This matters for interpretability and oversight because a high reconstruction score can appear self-verifying without establishing claim-level truth.

## Approach and contributions

- **Claim-level audits:** The grounded-vs-true cross flips one claim at a time and measures the resulting reconstruction drop; the evaluator swap tests whether dependencies survive evaluation by an independently trained reconstructor (§§2.1, 3.2).

- **Failure analysis:** Experiments distinguish missing activation content, verbalizer under-reading, co-adapted codes, representation collapse under self-reconstruction training, and frozen-probe staleness under representation drift (§§3–4).

- **RECAP:** Readable Encodings via Co-trained Auxiliary Predictors adds linear heads and externally defined prediction targets to the target model’s language-modeling objective. All reported decodability is measured using independently fitted fresh probes rather than the training heads (§5.1, Eq. 3).

- **Monitoring evaluation:** Fresh probes check verbalizer claims against designated activation content, including under a report-space adversary that edits explanations to maximize reconstruction score (§6).

| Decodability only partially propagates to prose at scale | On Pythia-160M, stated-word truth was 0.44 on RECAP versus 0.06 on control; across three target-model seeds it was 0.44–0.46 versus 0.00–0.07. A stronger reconstructor yielded 0.57 versus 0.15. | §5.4; Appendix J, Table 13 |
| RECAP improves claim monitoring | Fresh presence probes ranked true above false claims at AUC 0.965 versus 0.823 control over 672 held-out claims, with 95% CIs \([0.956,0.973]\) and \([0.759,0.879]\). False-claim flagging achieved 0.93 recall at 0.87 precision. | §6 |
| Probe monitoring survives reconstruction-score gaming | A score-optimal lie reduced \(\tilde r\) by 0.022 versus 0.173 for a random lie, suppressing about 87% of the penalty. RECAP probe AUC remained 0.952 with 0.86 recall; control fell to AUC 0.508 and 0.01 recall. | §6; Appendix F, Table 9 |
| RECAP’s effect requires continuing supervision | Most surplus decodability disappeared within 250 head-free fine-tuning steps and approached control by 2,000; applying the head loss every tenth step retained most decodability in a single run. | Appendix C, Table 3 |

Collectively, the evidence supports the authors’ claims that reconstruction score is not a claim-level truth certificate and that designated-content supervision can make selected internal information independently checkable. It does not establish open-ended explanation faithfulness, frontier-scale effectiveness, behavioral use of the decoded content, or protection outside designated targets and report-space attacks.

## Limitations and validity risks

**Author-stated limitations:**

- Evidence covers two synthetic domains, one released Qwen-2.5-7B NLA, and continued pretraining of Pythia-160M; many scale experiments use single seeds, and larger-scale RECAP is untested (§8).
- Audits parse closed-vocabulary or templated claims. Open-ended faithfulness is not measured (§8).
- RECAP requires access to target-model training, cannot retrofit a frozen model, and depends on targets whose loss requires genuine discrimination; a naive rare-target head failed this test (§§5.3, 8).
- Guarantees apply only to designated content. The never-designated vocabulary band showed no monitoring advantage (§6; Appendix F, Table 10).
- Decodability is not verbalizability: free-form generation conveys only part of the probe-readable content (§§5.4, 8).
- Decodability does not show causal use. Patching the tap changed behavior by only approximately 0.003, and computation could bypass a monitored subspace (§8).
- The adversarial study covers report-space edits, not activation-level or probe-aware target-model training attacks (Appendix F).
- Decodability erodes after removing auxiliary supervision; the persistence and intermittent-maintenance studies are single-seed and the intermittent run’s LM tax was not separately measured (Appendix C).

**Analyst inference:** The strong sandbox result may overstate real-world prose faithfulness because its exact, closed-vocabulary structure removes ambiguity and generation difficulty; the paper’s own Pythia results show a substantial decodability–verbalizability gap. The evidence nevertheless directly supports probe-based verification of designated predicates, which is narrower than general explanation correctness.

## Open research questions

1. **Does RECAP work at frontier scale and through realistic post-training?** Only Pythia-160M and short head-free fine-tuning are tested. A next experiment could apply balanced RECAP targets during pretraining and instruction tuning of several larger models, measuring probe AUC, LM tax, persistence, and prose truth.

2. **Can probe-decodable content be rendered faithfully in open-ended language?** At scale, probe precision exceeded generated precision despite multiple reader-side variations (§5.4). A next experiment could compare constrained extraction, structured decoding, and free-form generation on the same designated targets and recall levels.

3. **Can RECAP ensure that monitored content is causally used?** The paper finds that readability need not imply behavioral influence (§8). A next experiment could combine RECAP with causal interventions or routing constraints and test whether changing the designated representation predictably changes outputs.

4. **How robust is monitoring to activation-level adaptation?** The evaluated adversary edits reports but does not train the target model to evade probes. A next experiment could adversarially fine-tune the model against held-out monitors while rotating targets and independent probe families, then measure both task performance and detection.
