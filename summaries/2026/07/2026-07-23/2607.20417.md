# ATSplat: Compact Feed-forward 3D Gaussian Splatting with Adaptive Token Expansion

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-23.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.20417) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.20417)


## One-sentence takeaway

ATSplat reconstructs compact 3D Gaussian scenes in one forward pass by expanding uncertainty-selected 3D anchor tokens, reportedly matching or exceeding prior rendering quality with over 5.7× fewer Gaussians.

## Problem and motivation

Feed-forward 3D Gaussian Splatting avoids costly per-scene optimization, but dominant pixel-aligned methods predict Gaussians from input pixels and camera rays. Their primitive count and placement therefore scale with image resolution and view count rather than reconstruction difficulty, creating redundant Gaussians in simple regions while denying difficult regions extra capacity (Sections 1 and 3).

The authors seek to restore three useful properties of optimization-based 3DGS—sparse initialization, free 3D placement, and scene-adaptive capacity allocation—within a single-pass model.

## Approach and contributions

- **Sparse scene-conditioned anchors:** Coarse DINO patch features, patch-level depth estimates, camera rays, and local kNN aggregation produce a sparse 3D token scaffold (Section 4).
- **Grid-independent Gaussian placement:** Each refined anchor generates \(K\) local Gaussians with learned 3D offsets, rather than constraining their centers to input rays (Equations 4–5).
- **Adaptive Token Expansion:** At successive decoder blocks, a learned uncertainty head selects a top fraction of anchors and expands each into multiple child tokens. These tokens receive further image cross-attention, allocating both representation and computation to difficult regions (Section 5; Figure 2).
- **Error-aligned uncertainty training:** Rasterized token uncertainty is supervised against stopped-gradient D-SSIM rendering-error maps from intermediate Gaussian predictions (Equation 8).
- **Compact empirical result:** The authors report state-of-the-art or comparable rendering quality across RealEstate10K and DL3DV while using substantially fewer primitives, including a claimed reduction exceeding 5.7× (Section 7).

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** ATSplat expands tokens but does not prune tokens that later become redundant (Section 8).
- **Author-stated:** Scaling to larger scenes, more views, and higher resolutions needs further architectural work (Section 8).
- **Author-stated:** The method currently assumes posed inputs; unposed, in-the-wild reconstruction remains future work (Section 8).
- **Author-stated scope effect:** ATSplat falls behind pixel-aligned baselines in the large-overlap RealEstate10K regime, although it performs best at small overlap (Appendix B.1).
- **Analyst inference:** Main experiments use fixed expansion ratios, so spatial allocation is adaptive but the total budget is largely determined by input configuration. Scene-dependent budgets are demonstrated only through thresholding in an appendix experiment (Appendix A.3; Appendix C.1).
- **Analyst inference:** Hardware differs across training and some runtime measurements; DepthSplat’s high-resolution runtime uses an A6000 because of memory limits. Direct efficiency comparisons therefore require careful hardware normalization (Section 7.1).
- **Analyst inference:** Some baseline values are reported from prior work, while others use public checkpoints; code or weights are unavailable for certain settings. This creates possible implementation and protocol asymmetry (Section 7.1; Tables 1–3 captions).
- **Analyst inference:** Exact quantitative tables are missing from the supplied retrieval, preventing verification of claimed metric margins and ablation magnitudes.

## Open research questions

1. **Can expansion and pruning be learned jointly?** The current decoder only adds tokens, so redundancy may persist. A next experiment could add uncertainty- or opacity-based pruning and measure quality, decoder cost, Gaussian count, and budget stability.

2. **How robust is uncertainty-guided allocation under distribution shift?** Cross-dataset transfer is reported, but exact metrics and calibration behavior are unavailable. A next experiment could measure uncertainty–error correlation and token-selection precision on unseen datasets and capture conditions.

3. **Can one model support unposed and variably sized inputs?** Known poses remain a core prerequisite. A next experiment could jointly estimate cameras and anchors, then compare reconstruction quality and failure rates against the posed version.

4. **What is the quality–compute frontier at larger scale?** The authors identify larger scenes, more views, and higher resolutions as open. Controlled scaling experiments could vary view count, resolution, scene extent, \(K\), and expansion thresholds while reporting memory, latency, Gaussian count, and quality.
