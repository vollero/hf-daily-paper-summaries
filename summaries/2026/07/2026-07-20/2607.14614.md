# Beyond Entropy: Correctness-Aware Advantage Shaping via Contrastive Policy Optimization

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-20.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.14614) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14614)


## One-sentence takeaway

CPO shapes RLVR advantages with token-level probability shifts induced by reference-answer conditioning, outperforming GRPO and entropy-based shaping on evaluated reasoning tasks while requiring references and an additional forward pass.

## Problem and motivation

GRPO’s binary trajectory rewards cannot assign credit to individual reasoning tokens and produce zero advantage when every sampled response in a group receives the same reward. Entropy-based shaping adds token-level signals but measures uncertainty rather than correctness, so it cannot distinguish productive exploration from confusion. The authors seek a correctness-aware token signal that improves credit assignment without a separate critic or stronger teacher.

## Approach and contributions

- **Contrastive disagreement:** CPO compares each sampled token’s log-probability under the ordinary policy with its probability under the same policy conditioned on a reference answer. The resulting log-ratio is used as a proxy for whether that token raises or lowers the probability of a correct completion (§2.1, Eqs. 1–7).
- **Correctness-aware advantage shaping:** The token log-ratio is added to GRPO’s trajectory advantage, with clipping that prevents it from reversing a nonzero trajectory-level advantage. For zero-advantage groups, the disagreement supplies the entire token-level signal (§2.2, Eqs. 8–9).
- **Theoretical interpretation:** For an ideal policy conditioned on correctness, the disagreement equals log token correctness probability up to a prefix-dependent constant. The practical reference-conditioned policy is assumed to approximate this ideal distribution (Appendix C, Corollary C.6, Assumption C.7, Theorem C.8).
- **Unification with distillation:** The authors interpret on-policy distillation’s reverse-KL signal as a CPO instance whose correctness-informed posterior comes from an external teacher; reference- and critic-conditioned policies are other possible posterior designs (§2.1.2, §3.3).
- **Empirical analysis:** Experiments compare CPO with standard and entropy-intervened RLVR, study posterior and advantage designs, and analyze how correct versus incorrect responses affect exploration and exploitation (§§3.2–3.5).

The evidence supports the authors’ claim that negative disagreement is informative about error-prone token choices and that CPO improves aggregate sampled-answer performance in the tested setting. Ablations also support the usefulness of token-level shaping, clipping, current-question references, and combining correct with incorrect responses.

It does not establish that disagreement is a calibrated token-correctness probability: the theory proves that property for an ideal correctness-conditioned distribution, while the practical reference-conditioned model is only a proxy. The supplied text also omits the numerical contents of Tables 1–3 and 8–10, preventing verification of per-benchmark scores, all baseline gaps, or the meaning of reported percentage improvements.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** CPO requires an extra reference-guided forward pass, increasing training time by approximately 20% over GRPO (Appendix A).
- **Author-stated:** The current implementation requires reference answers, restricting direct use to tasks where suitable correctness information exists. Critic feedback and process annotations are proposed but not tested replacements (Appendix A).
- **Author-stated:** Statistical tests are limited by computation: only CPO, GRPO, and DAPO receive three-run reporting; other baselines apparently do not (§3.2, Appendix F.1).
- **Analyst inference:** The central practical guarantee depends on Assumption C.7—that prompting the same model with a reference approximates conditioning on eventual correctness. Figure 2 supports negative-disagreement ranking in one model/dataset setup, but positive disagreement shows little improvement over baseline (§2.1.2; Appendix C).
- **Analyst inference:** Training is confined to 7.5k MATH problems and two Qwen backbones, so evidence for other model families, open-ended tasks, programming, or subjective rewards is absent (§3.1).
- **Analyst inference:** Pass@16 emphasizes whether any of 16 samples succeeds; the prose reports some greedy and Pass@K analyses, but complete Pass@1 and efficiency-adjusted results are unavailable (§§3.1, 3.5).
- **Analyst inference:** Reproducibility is incomplete from the retrieved text because table cells and exact prompt bodies are missing, despite their captions and hyperparameter descriptions being present.

## Open research questions

1. **Can CPO work without gold references?** This remains open because critic- and process-guided posteriors are proposed but not evaluated. A next experiment could compare reference-, critic-, and process-supervised posteriors under matched compute on tasks lacking unique answers.

2. **How well calibrated is disagreement across models and domains?** Validation uses Qwen2.5-Math-7B on MATH500 and is strongest for negative disagreement. A next experiment could measure rank correlation and calibration against continuation-based correctness estimates across both backbones and every evaluation domain.

3. **Are the gains retained under equal compute or latency budgets?** CPO costs about 20% more training time. A next experiment could compare CPO and GRPO using matched GPU-hours, rollout counts, and total forward-pass budgets.

4. **Does CPO improve single-sample reliability as strongly as Pass@16?** The paper says Pass@K gaps exceed greedy-accuracy gaps, but the supplied text lacks full values. A next experiment could report Pass@1, greedy accuracy, Pass@16, and compute-normalized success with confidence intervals.
