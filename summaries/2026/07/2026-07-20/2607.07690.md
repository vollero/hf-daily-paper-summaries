# Agon: Competitive Cross-Model RL with Implicit Rival Grading of Reasoning

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-20.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.07690) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07690)


## One-sentence takeaway

Agon jointly trains two rotating draft-and-challenge language-model policies so that opponent-conditioned, difficulty-weighted rewards implicitly supervise reasoning, raising Qwen3-0.6B pass@1 from 30% with GRPO to 61% on a 300-problem DeepMath-hard evaluation, albeit with two-pass inference and only one training run per method.

## Problem and motivation

Outcome-based RL such as GRPO grades only final-answer correctness, leaving the reasoning trace unscored. The authors argue that on difficult problems this encourages longer exploratory traces without commensurate accuracy gains; in their runs, GRPO increased mean trace length from 6.1k to 8.1k tokens for a 7-percentage-point accuracy gain (Section 3, Table 3).

Direct process supervision would require step-level labels or a learned process reward model. Agon instead uses a distinct peer policy as an implicit grader: each model reads the other’s worked solution and is rewarded for correctly solving cases its rival missed. The intended gap is therefore process-sensitive training without process labels or a separate reward model.

## Approach and contributions

- **Competitive draft-and-challenge RL:** One policy drafts \(N\) solutions; the challenger reads each draft’s post-reasoning summary, with the final answer and raw private trace removed, and produces paired solutions. Roles rotate every optimizer step (Sections 4.3–4.4; Algorithm 1).

- **Conversion-based competitive reward:** Challenger correctness receives extra weight when the paired draft is wrong:
  \[
  R(b_i)=2c(b_i)+c(b_i)(1-c(a_i))+0.5\phi(b_i).
  \]
  Unlike a subtractive win margin, this changes the action-dependent correctness weight across opponent contexts (Section 4.4, Equation 3).

- **Efficient two-policy implementation:** The reported implementation uses two rank-16 LoRA adapters over one frozen base. The second adapter adds approximately 10M parameters, reported as about 2% of the base, plus optimizer state (Section 4.2).

- **Factorized evaluation design:** The study separates information exchange from competitive reward through cooperative and adversarial variants, with GRPO, self-refinement, untrained Mixture-of-Agents, and two-pass GRPO controls. The authors acknowledge that the competition-without-exchange cell is gradient-inert and does not fully isolate competition (Sections 4.4 and 5; Table 1).

The evidence supports a large single-run improvement on the specified DeepMath-hard split and supports the adversarial-versus-cooperative comparison within the authors’ design. It does not establish training-run stability, equal end-to-end inference cost, causation by genuinely complementary blind spots, or broad effectiveness beyond cleanly verifiable math and code tasks. Confidence intervals cover evaluation-set sampling only, not variation across training seeds.

## Limitations and validity risks

**Author-stated limitations:**

- Agon requires reference problems and a verifier; only math and a smaller code study with clean programmatic rewards are evaluated (Section 6).
- Comparable strength and behavioral divergence are assumed, but divergence is maintained heuristically and complementarity is not directly measured (Sections 4.1–4.2 and 6).
- Inference requires two sequential generation passes. Only final-stage trace lengths are reported, and opponent-summary prefill compute is not equalized (Sections 4.5, 5.5, and 6).
- Every trained result comes from one training run and one sampled rollout per problem, leaving run-to-run variance unknown (Section 6).
- The better cascade direction is chosen post hoc on the held-out set, creating selection risk (Sections 4.5 and 6).
- Zero-variance GRPO groups are dropped, potentially reducing signal on uniformly easy or hard problems (Sections 3 and 6).
- Text-only communication restricts channel bandwidth (Section 6).

**Analyst inferences grounded in the text:**

- The 61% versus 30% headline comparison is not inference-budget matched; the fairer controls are the two-pass self-cascade and untrained MoA.
- The reported “competitive” gain may instead reflect difficulty-weighted reward shaping under heterogeneous challenger contexts. The authors explicitly state their experiments do not distinguish these interpretations (Section 4.4).
- Competition without visible exchange is not cleanly ablated: the tested shared-opponent reward normalizes away, while the needed per-rollout hidden-draft condition remains untested (Sections 4.4 and 5.4).
- The peer-versus-self comparison changes adapter capacity, draft source, and prompting simultaneously, so it cannot attribute gains specifically to different blind spots (Section 4.2).
- Exact scaling, transfer, and code results cannot be independently summarized from the supplied text because several table bodies contain captions but no numerical rows.

## Open research questions

1. **Is the gain truly competitive, or primarily context-difficulty reweighting?**  
   This remains open because Section 4.4 presents both interpretations. A next experiment could compare conversion rewards against explicit draft-difficulty weights while holding opponent contexts fixed.

2. **How much behavioral complementarity is necessary?**  
   The paper neither measures error-profile divergence nor ablates strength gaps. A controlled study could vary initialization distance, adapter overlap, or model-family pairing and correlate disagreement patterns with cascade gains.

3. **Does Agon remain beneficial under full compute and latency parity?**  
   Rollout counts and length caps are matched, but prefill cost, drafting length, and sequential latency are not. A next experiment could equalize total generated and prefetched tokens plus wall-clock inference latency.

4. **Does the method work with noisy or non-programmatic feedback?**  
   Evaluation is confined to clean math and unit-test verification. A plausible experiment would introduce controlled verifier noise or apply Agon to a task with partial-credit judgments and measure robustness.
