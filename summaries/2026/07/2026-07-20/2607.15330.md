# Xiaomi-Robotics-1: Scaling Vision-Language-Action Models with over 100K Hours of Real-World Trajectories

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-20.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.15330) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15330)


## One-sentence takeaway

The authors present a two-stage vision-language-action model whose large-scale UMI pre-training improves real-robot generalization and data-efficient adaptation, while achieving leading reported results on four simulation benchmarks.

## Problem and motivation

Generalist robot policies need large, diverse trajectory corpora, but conventional robot teleoperation is costly, hardware-bound, and often concentrated on narrow tasks and environments. The authors identify two additional scaling gaps: language-labeling trajectories becomes prohibitive at large scale, and behavior learned from handheld UMI grippers must be aligned with robot embodiments and natural imperative instructions (Sections 1 and 2.2).

## Approach and contributions

- **Large-scale pre-training:** Train on more than 100,000 hours of real-world UMI manipulation trajectories spanning household, commercial, industrial, office, and outdoor settings (Section 2.2.1; Figure 3).

- **Scalable language annotation:** Split trajectories into fixed-length clips and use Qwen3.5-27B to describe gripper and object state transitions; the producer–consumer pipeline reportedly labels the corpus in roughly two weeks (Section 2.2.1).

- **Cross-embodiment post-training:** Use about 10,000 hours of UMI and robot trajectories to transfer UMI behavior to multiple robot embodiments and convert descriptive state-transition conditioning into imperative task following (Section 2.2.2; Figure 4).

- **VLA architecture:** Couple a Qwen3-VL backbone with a flow-matching diffusion transformer; auxiliary Choice Policy action prediction shapes VLM representations, while action-token representations are hidden from the DiT to discourage shortcut copying (Section 2.1; Figure 2).

- **Scaling and adaptation study:** Evaluate how pre-training data and model size affect held-out action error, out-of-the-box real-robot success, low-data downstream adaptation, and four simulation benchmarks (Section 3).

The evidence supports the authors’ claims of monotonic improvements across the tested data and model scales, strong low-data fine-tuning, and leading results among the baselines listed for four simulation benchmarks. It does not establish a general scaling law beyond the tested range, isolate every architectural contribution, or prove that the full 100,000-hour corpus—not other training choices—causes the downstream gains. The abstract reports 57.6% on RoboCasa365, whereas Table 3 and the main text report 57.4%; the tabulated result is therefore internally inconsistent with the abstract.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated scope issue:** RoboDojo evaluation omits observation history, and the model consequently trails the memory-aware Hy-Embodied-0.5-VLA on the memory dimension (Section 3.4; Table 5).

- **Author-stated resource constraint:** Scaling experiments use only about 20,000 hours because of compute limits, despite the claimed pre-training corpus exceeding 100,000 hours (Section 3.1).

- **Analyst inference — limited real-robot novelty:** The out-of-the-box tasks were present in post-training; only environments and object instances were unseen. This tests environmental and instance generalization, not unseen-task generalization (Section 3.2).

- **Analyst inference — small trial count:** Downstream real-robot comparisons use 10 trials per task, and no uncertainty intervals or significance tests are provided in the supplied text (Section 3.3).

- **Analyst inference — incomplete causal attribution:** No reported ablations separately quantify the auto-labeling pipeline, Choice Policy supervision, masking action-related VLM tokens from DiT attention, vision-language co-training, or cross-embodiment mixture.

- **Analyst inference — reproducibility limitations:** Code and checkpoints are promised but not yet reported as released; dataset release, training compute, detailed optimization schedules, caption-quality audits, and real-robot evaluation variance are not established in the supplied text.

- **Analyst inference — inconsistent headline metric:** RoboCasa365 is reported as 57.6% in the abstract but 57.4% in Table 3 and Section 3.4.

## Open research questions

1. **Does scaling continue beyond the tested 20,000-hour range?** The controlled scaling study does not use the full corpus. Train otherwise identical models on progressively larger subsets through 100,000 hours and report held-out error and real-robot success with uncertainty.

2. **How much does automatic state-transition labeling contribute?** Caption quality and its causal value are not isolated. Compare automatic captions against manual labels, imperative labels, unlabeled trajectories, and captions from alternative annotators at fixed data and compute.

3. **Which architectural and post-training components drive transfer?** The paper does not provide component-level ablations. Remove Choice Policy supervision, action-token masking, vision-language co-training, and embodiment alignment individually and evaluate the same real-robot suite.

4. **Can the model generalize out-of-the-box to genuinely unseen tasks?** Section 3.2 changes environments and objects but retains task categories seen during post-training. Evaluate zero-shot execution of held-out task compositions and new task categories on real robots.
