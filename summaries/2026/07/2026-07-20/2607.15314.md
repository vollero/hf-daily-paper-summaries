# Cura 1T: Specialized Model for Agentic Healthcare

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-20.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.15314) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15314)


## One-sentence takeaway

Cura 1T specializes Kimi-K2.6 for clinical communication, multimodal medical reasoning, diagnosis, and EHR tool use through a human-gated loop that converts benchmark failures into targeted post-training mixtures, improving all five reported healthcare evaluations while retaining competitive out-of-domain performance.

## Problem and motivation

Healthcare models must jointly support patient-facing communication, expert reasoning over text and images, interactive diagnosis, and protocol-constrained EHR actions. These capabilities have distinct failure modes—such as rubric omissions, missing knowledge, premature diagnosis, and malformed tool calls—and improving one can degrade another. The authors argue that existing work largely treats these capabilities separately and that the central specialization bottleneck is scarce, fragmented supervision and careful data-mixture construction rather than merely selecting training algorithms or hyperparameters (Sections 1 and 3.2).

## Approach and contributions

- **Human-gated self-evolution:** A training agent plans capability-specific rounds, trains candidate LoRA adapters, evaluates graded trajectories, diagnoses failures, and proposes revised data mixtures; humans approve plans and keep, revert, refine, or deploy decisions (Sections 3.1–3.2; Figure 2).
- **Failure-specific data construction:** Refinement actions include reasoning correction, grounded knowledge injection, behavior calibration, workflow-specific examples, retention anchors, deduplication, reweighting, and safety/PII validation (Section 3.2; Table 2).
- **Three-stage post-training:** SFT screens mixtures and hyperparameters, RL optimizes task rewards, and self-distillation fine-tuning consolidates updates using on-policy samples and privileged-context teacher trajectories (Section 3.1; Equation 1).
- **Unified healthcare model:** Cura 1T is a LoRA-specialized Kimi-K2.6 model with native text-and-vision input and a 256K-token context window, targeting patient care, clinical reasoning, and healthcare-agent workflows (Section 3; Appendix A.1).
- **Tool-native evaluation adaptations:** The work defines structured AgentClinic interaction tools and renderer-native FHIR operations for MedAgentBench (Appendix A.2).

These results establish performance under the paper’s stated benchmark harnesses and show that different failure types benefited from different data interventions. They do not establish clinical safety, real-world effectiveness, or that the self-evolution loop is superior to equally resourced manual curation. The paper also does not provide numerical out-of-domain results in the supplied text. The inconsistent MedAgentBench base values in Tables 1 and 3 prevent a single unambiguous base-to-Cura delta without further explanation.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** Compute, data, and other resource constraints bound the training regime; clinical behavior coverage and long-horizon workflows remain incomplete, and full-parameter training was not explored (Section 6, Limitations).
- **Author-stated:** The evaluations measure narrow competencies under fixed harnesses and do not justify unsupervised clinical use or replacement of clinicians (Section 6, Safety and clinical use).
- **Analyst inference:** There is no controlled comparison against manual data-mixture design or an otherwise identical pipeline without the training agent, so the agent’s incremental value is not isolated.
- **Analyst inference:** Component evidence is incomplete: the paper reports successful and reverted rounds, but not a systematic factorial ablation of SFT, RL, SDFT, retention anchors, human review, or each refinement skill.
- **Analyst inference:** Data quantities, mixture proportions, synthesis prompts, teacher identities, RL reward details, capability-specific hyperparameters, and compute costs are absent, limiting reproducibility.
- **Analyst inference:** AgentClinic uses a modified tool-native protocol and MedAgentBench uses a custom renderer-native interface; comparisons are valid only to the extent that all reported models received equivalent implementations and prompting.
- **Analyst inference:** The MedAgentBench base is 0.847 in Table 1 but 0.883 in Table 3, with no explicit reconciliation.
- **Analyst inference:** No deployment-oriented evaluation is reported for calibration, subgroup performance, privacy leakage, adversarial robustness, clinician oversight, or prospective patient outcomes.

## Open research questions

1. **How much value comes from agent-directed refinement rather than expert manual curation?** The paper lacks a matched manual baseline. A next experiment could hold compute, source data, and training stages fixed while comparing agent-planned, expert-planned, and static mixtures.

2. **Which training and refinement components are necessary for cross-task retention?** Reported trajectories suggest retention anchors and knowledge injection matter, but there is no systematic ablation. A factorial study could remove SDFT, RL, retention anchors, or individual refinement actions and measure all healthcare and out-of-domain benchmarks after each change.

3. **Do benchmark gains transfer to realistic longitudinal care?** Current evidence comes from fixed benchmarks and simulated or constrained tool environments. A prospective, clinician-supervised study could evaluate multi-session cases, evolving records, handoffs, and error recovery without permitting autonomous clinical decisions.

4. **Can the method scale beyond LoRA without increasing harmful forgetting?** Full-parameter updates remain unexplored because of resource limits. A matched LoRA-versus-full-parameter experiment could compare healthcare gains, general-capability retention, compute cost, and safety regressions.

## Confidence and missing information

**Confidence: medium.** The supplied text provides detailed benchmark tables, training stages, harness descriptions, and case studies, but omits training-data sizes and mixture proportions, synthesis and filtering prompts, teacher-model identities, RL objectives and reward construction, capability-specific hyperparameters, compute budget, uncertainty estimates, repeated-run variance, contamination checks, exact out-of-domain scores, and a reconciliation of the two MedAgentBench base scores.
