# RESOURCE2SKILL: Distilling Executable Agent Skills from Human-Created Multimodal Resources

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-20.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2606.29538) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.29538)


## One-sentence takeaway

Resource2Skills converts videos, code repositories, articles, and reference artifacts into a hierarchical multimodal skill wiki that, according to the authors, raises software-authoring agents’ average artifact score by 11.9 percentage points over matched no-skill agents across seven domains and four model backends.

## Problem and motivation

Software agents need reusable procedural knowledge: how to sequence tool operations, inspect intermediate results, bind parameters, and recover from failures. Existing skill libraries are mainly hand-authored, learned from agents’ own traces, or mined from text and code, leaving tutorial videos and other multimodal human resources underused.

The authors identify two related obstacles. Raw videos are expensive and redundant as inference-time context, while text summaries discard visually and temporally important information such as operation order, layout, motion, and before–after changes. The paper therefore targets the extraction, organization, retrieval, and execution of compact multimodal skills rather than direct retrieval of raw resources (Introduction).

## Approach and contributions

1. **Resource-to-skill construction.** A vision-capable language model distills tutorial videos, repositories, articles, and reference artifacts into structured skill entries. Deterministic processing and five acceptance checks cover completeness, provenance, deduplication, modality consistency, and structural code executability (Method; Appendix D).

2. **Hierarchical multimodal Skill Wiki.** Each entry contains a taxonomy path, structured text, optional visual evidence, optional executable or adaptable code, metadata, and provenance. Taxonomies are domain-specific, while the browse-and-read interface is shared (Method, “Hierarchical Multimodal Skill Wiki”; Appendix L).

3. **Hierarchy-aware selection and execution.** MetaBrowse uses BM25 over names, tags, applicability text, and taxonomy paths to shortlist 20 entries; an LM then selects up to five entries for composition and execution through an MCP-mediated domain interface (Method; Appendix A).

4. **Unified offline and online acquisition.** The same construction and validation operator builds the offline library and, when coverage is inadequate, creates a separate temporary online pool for gap filling (Method, “Execution and Online Acquisition”; Appendix I).

5. **Multi-domain empirical evaluation.** The framework is tested on seven software-authoring domains, four agent backends, two harness baselines, and ablations of source mix, representation, library scale, wiki organization, selection, and online acquisition (Experiments).

The results establish consistent gains within the tested authoring environments, model backends, interfaces, and evaluation protocol. Matched briefs and controlled ablations strengthen the attribution to skill access, source choice, representation, and selection strategy.

They do not establish generalization beyond software with programmatic interfaces and accessible procedural resources. Nor do they fully isolate Resource2Skills from raw-resource retrieval, prove semantic correctness of artifacts, or demonstrate reliable execution of every selected skill. The judge primarily assesses rendered outputs, and failures receive zero, so the overall metric combines artifact quality with successful artifact production.

## Limitations and validity risks

**Author-stated limitations**

- Generalization is not claimed for domains lacking either programmatic tool interfaces or public procedural resources (Appendix M).
- Online acquisition adds search, distillation, and validation latency and is evaluated on a targeted stress test rather than as part of the default main pipeline (Appendix M).
- Retrieval baselines operate over distilled skills, not raw resources under an equal context budget; that comparison is left open (Appendix M).
- Case studies expose partial parameter grounding, unresolved formula bindings, overly literal composition, placeholder leakage, and reduced variation (Appendix J).
- Structural executability checks only establish that code runs and creates a non-trivial artifact, not that it solves a benchmark request (Appendix D).

**Analyst inferences**

- **Judge validity risk:** Most main scores depend on model judges. The 17-pair judge-reliability sample is small, agreement with humans is moderate rather than conclusive, and the paper reports that utility is the least reliable axis (Appendix F).
- **Targeted online-test risk:** \(T_{\text{novel}}\) is deliberately selected from capability regions known to be absent from the offline pool, so its +21.6-point result supports gap filling under that construction, not performance on naturally occurring unseen requests (Appendices B, I, and M).
- **Incomplete statistical coverage:** Appendix G supplies significance results for 9 cells, while the main comparison contains 28 model–domain cells; uncertainty for the remaining cells is not available in the supplied text.
- **Reproducibility ambiguity:** The paper describes supplementary logs and configurations, but the supplied text does not provide the actual artifacts, prompts, raw tables, resource manifests, or complete per-domain results needed to independently reproduce the claims.
- **No end-to-end semantic correctness metric:** Rendered-artifact rubrics can reward visible quality without fully verifying formulas, interaction, or underlying program behavior; the paper itself notes judge disagreement over apparent functionality (Appendix F).

## Open research questions

1. **How does distilled-skill retrieval compare with raw-resource retrieval at equal cost?**  
   This remains open because all retrieval baselines search the distilled library. A next experiment could match token, latency, and resource budgets across raw video/transcript/code retrieval, flat distilled skills, and the hierarchical wiki.

2. **Can skill composition detect and repair unresolved bindings before rendering?**  
   Appendix J shows broken formulas, missing visual parameters, and overly literal composition. A next experiment could add parameter-schema validation and intermediate artifact checks, then measure execution failures and final scores against the current single-pass composition loop.

3. **How well does the framework transfer to genuinely new domains?**  
   Evaluation covers seven authoring domains that all have public procedural content and programmatic backends. A next experiment could build a wiki for a held-out domain using a fixed construction budget and compare zero-shot taxonomy transfer with a newly designed domain taxonomy.

4. **Can online acquisition be made efficient and evaluated on natural distribution shift?**  
   Its latency is acknowledged but not quantified, and the novel suite is intentionally coverage-targeted. A next experiment could report end-to-end time and cost while triggering acquisition automatically on a temporally or independently sampled request stream.

## Confidence and missing information

**Confidence: high** for the description of the method, reported aggregate findings, and stated evaluation design because the supplied text includes the main paper and extensive appendices.

Material missing information includes the numerical contents of Tables 1–8 beyond values quoted in prose, complete per-domain and per-backend scores, exact distillation and selection prompts, resource counts by source family, acquisition latency and compute cost, all acceptance thresholds, per-skill execution rates, raw-resource retrieval comparisons, and the supplementary artifacts and logs claimed by the authors.
