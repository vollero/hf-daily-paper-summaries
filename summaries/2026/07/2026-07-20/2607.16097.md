# Understanding Reasoning from Pretraining to Post-Training

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-20.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.16097) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16097)


## One-sentence takeaway

In controlled chess experiments, the authors find that pretraining loss predicts post-RL pass@1, pretraining exposure predicts the local rate of RL improvement, and RL’s effect shifts from reinforcing known correct moves on easy puzzles to both discovering correct moves and amplifying errors on harder ones.

## Problem and motivation

RL post-training and pretraining scaling are usually studied separately, leaving unclear how model size, pretraining data, and RL compute jointly determine reasoning performance. Natural-language studies make this difficult because corpora are uncontrolled, actions and intermediate reasoning are hard to verify, and full compute sweeps are expensive. The authors use chess to isolate these interactions and investigate both compute allocation and how RL changes an inherited policy (Abstract; Sections 1–2).

## Approach and contributions

- **Controlled pipeline:** Pretrain 5M–1B parameter language models on tokenized human chess games, SFT them on synthetic tree-structured move traces, and apply GRPO to puzzles with binary, exactly verifiable rewards (Sections 2.1–2.4).
- **Joint scaling model:** Fit post-RL pass@1 locally as a log-linear function of RL FLOPs, with the reference reward predicted by pretraining loss and the slope parameterized by model size and pretraining tokens (Section 3.3; Appendix G).
- **Compute-allocation analysis:** Evaluate 36 pretraining–RL combinations across four model sizes and use the fitted law with a Chinchilla-style loss surface to estimate compute-optimal allocations (Sections 3.2–3.3).
- **Policy-mechanism analysis:** Marginalize over 128 sampled reasoning traces per puzzle and classify move-policy changes as correct-move amplification, tail discovery, wrong-mode amplification, and related categories (Section 4.1; Appendix H).
- **Cross-domain check:** Repeat the scaling analysis using checkpoints of a fixed 1B-class OLMo-2 model trained on 10B–200B math-domain tokens (Section 5; Appendix I).
- **Released assets:** The paper reports public models, datasets, and code (Abstract resource links).

## Evidence and main results

| Finding | Evidence / setting | Location in paper |
|---|---|---|
| Pretraining loss strongly predicts fitted post-RL pass@1 at fixed RL compute. | On the B3–B4 chess benchmark, Spearman \(|\rho|\) rises from 0.93 to 0.99 as reference RL compute increases from \(10^{16}\) to \(10^{20}\) FLOPs. | Section 3.3; Figure 3 |
| More pretraining exposure is associated with faster local RL improvement. | Across chess runs, the slope per decade of RL compute correlates with \(\log_{10}T\) at Pearson \(r=0.84\); a joint model using \(\log T\) and \(\log N\) performs best among tested parameterizations. | Section 3.3; Figures 3 and 12 |
| The joint law predicts held-out runs substantially better when observed pretraining loss is supplied. | Across 36 leave-one-out runs, reward RMSE is 0.0194 using Chinchilla-predicted loss and 0.0102 using observed loss; corresponding MAE is 0.0153 and 0.0078. | Appendix G.3; Table 6 |
| Extrapolation across model sizes is less accurate, especially at the upper observed scale. | Leave-one-model-size-out RMSE is 0.0242 overall versus 0.0194 for run-level LOO; holding out 680M yields RMSE 0.048. | Appendix G.3.1; Tables 7–8 |
| The preferred RL compute fraction rises with total compute in the studied frontier. | Empirically, the 20M model’s frontier moves from 5% to 32% RL compute; the fitted global frontier moves from about 20% at 50M to about 28% at 680M. | Sections 3.2–3.3; Figures 2 and 4 |
| RL helps pass@1 more consistently than pass@16. | RL improves pass@1 across runs, while pass@16 is nearly flat or sometimes slightly worse for larger models; no aggregate effect sizes are supplied in the retrieved text. | Section 3.2; Figure 2 |
| RL is only partly explained by uniform sharpening. | From SFT to RL step 750, the global power coefficient reaches 1.35, but explained sharpening is 0.17; the centered-logit fit has slope 1.13 and \(R^2=0.56\). | Section 4.1; Tables 11–12 |
| Policy changes depend on puzzle difficulty. | Easy states are dominated by amplification of already-preferred correct moves; harder bins increasingly exhibit both tail discovery and wrong-mode amplification. Exact category proportions are not included in the text. | Section 4.1; Figure 5 |
| RL mainly expands search breadth, with limited long-horizon recovery. | Branching and width-to-depth ratio increase while depth stays roughly flat; even at step 1000 for the 50M model, few rollouts recover a complete four-ply target line. | Section 4.2; Figures 22–25 |
| A fixed-architecture math experiment shows the same qualitative scaling pattern. | Across 14 checkpoints spanning 10B–200B pretraining tokens, lower loss predicts higher post-RL pass@1 and the local slope increases approximately linearly with log tokens; numerical fit statistics are not supplied. | Section 5; Figure 6 |

These results support an empirical, local relationship among pretraining quality, data exposure, and early-to-intermediate RL behavior in the tested regimes. They do not establish a universal causal scaling law, an unbounded benefit from RL compute, or quantitatively valid compute shares for frontier language models. The math experiment is corroborative but qualitative and changes several factors relative to chess.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** Chess has an 81-token vocabulary, exact verification, and little entanglement with fluency or world knowledge; its exponents and policy-category proportions should not be treated as language-model predictions (Appendix A).
- **Author-stated:** Puzzles have unique designated solutions and all-or-nothing binary rewards, unlike open-ended or partial-credit language tasks (Appendix A).
- **Author-stated:** Models reach at most 1B parameters, and behavior at larger scales is unknown (Appendix A).
- **Author-stated:** The log-linear RL law is local to the measured, non-saturated compute range and must not be extrapolated as unbounded improvement (Appendix G.8).
- **Author-stated:** The slope model is noisier than the reference-reward model, while practical prediction compounds scaling-law loss error with downstream fit error (Appendix G.8; Table 6).
- **Author-stated:** Results may depend on the chosen tree-based reasoning-trace serialization (Appendix A).
- **Analyst inference:** The math check does not isolate pretraining-token count from checkpoint position along a single training trajectory, so it is weaker evidence than an independent controlled sweep.
- **Analyst inference:** No alternative RL algorithm, reward design, or trace format is compared, limiting claims that the observed mechanisms characterize RL post-training generally.
- **Analyst inference:** Figure-level numerical values for difficulty-specific policy categories, pass@k changes, and math fits are unavailable in the supplied text, preventing precise effect-size assessment.

## Open research questions

1. **Does the allocation law hold for larger natural-language models?** The paper stops at 1B-class models and warns against quantitative transfer. A next experiment could sweep pretraining and RL compute for several larger architectures on the same verifiable math tasks.

2. **Can RL reduce wrong-mode amplification without sacrificing tail discovery?** Both behaviors increase on harder chess puzzles, but the tested GRPO recipe does not separate them. Compare reward shaping, KL schedules, or diversity/coverage objectives using the policy taxonomy in Table 13.

3. **Can training produce deeper rather than merely broader search?** Maximum depth remains roughly flat and long continuations remain difficult. SFT or RL could be run with traces explicitly balanced by solution depth, followed by depth-stratified coverage evaluation.

4. **Are pretraining tokens themselves causal for the steeper RL slope?** Token exposure correlates with slope, but unexplained run-level variation remains. A factorial experiment varying token count, data quality, model size, and final loss independently would better distinguish their effects.
