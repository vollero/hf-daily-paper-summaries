# Behavioral Privacy Leakage in Agentic Negotiation: Formalizing and Mitigating Inference Attacks via Randomized Policies

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-20.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.06815) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06815)


## One-sentence takeaway

The paper formalizes privacy leakage from negotiation trajectories and proposes phase-decaying Gaussian randomization, but its promising synthetic results do not constitute an end-to-end differential-privacy guarantee for the experimentally evaluated private-boundary clipping mechanism.

## Problem and motivation

Negotiation agents may expose private constraints—such as budgets or reservation values—through their offer trajectories, concession rates, timing, and convergence behavior, even when cryptography prevents direct disclosure. The authors call this behavioral privacy leakage.

They identify a gap between cryptographic negotiation defenses, which protect explicit values, and conventional differential privacy, which is generally formulated for static datasets rather than sequential strategic interactions that must preserve feasibility, convergence, and utility. The paper therefore targets inference of private constraints from complete observable negotiation traces under a passive-adversary model (Sections 1 and 3).

## Approach and contributions

1. **Behavioral-DP formulation:** Defines adjacent private constraints by \(|\theta-\theta'|\leq\Delta\) and requires \((\varepsilon,\delta)\)-DP over distributions of observable offer sequences (Sections 3.2 and 4.1).

2. **Randomized negotiation policy:** Adds Gaussian noise whose scale decays with the negotiation phase, then clips offers to a feasible interval through a safety critic (Sections 4.3–4.6).

3. **Conditional theoretical analysis:** Claims differential privacy when clipping uses a public proxy ceiling, almost-sure offer-sequence convergence, and an \(O(\sigma_{\max}^2)\) expected Nash-surplus loss for fixed round count (Section 6). The private-constraint clipping used experimentally is explicitly outside the complete DP proof.

4. **Synthetic evaluation:** Tests constraint-inference attacks, utility, success, and convergence on 3,000 simulated bilateral negotiations, including an adaptive meta-learning adversary (Section 5).

The evidence supports resistance to several trained inference models in one synthetic, single-issue bilateral setting while meeting the paper’s chosen utility thresholds. It does not establish effectiveness on real negotiations, against active probing, or under repeated interactions. Crucially, the formal DP theorem covers public-proxy clipping, whereas the reported experiments use private-\(\theta\) clipping; the empirical accuracy reductions therefore should not be read as validation of the theorem’s end-to-end deployment configuration.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** Evaluation is limited to 3,000 synthetic, single-issue bilateral negotiations; multi-issue, multi-party, and real-world validation remain open (Sections 4.7 and 7.5).
- **Author-stated:** The passive adversary cannot influence offers. Active probing and privacy loss accumulated across repeated negotiations are not addressed (Sections 3.4, 7.3, and 7.5).
- **Author-stated:** The total privacy budget may be large, and tighter Rényi-DP or zero-concentrated-DP accounting is left for future work (Sections 6.1, 6.4, and 7.5).
- **Author-stated:** Experiments clip at the private constraint, creating boundary leakage; the formal DP result instead assumes a public proxy ceiling (Sections 4.4, 6.1, and 7.3).
- **Author-stated:** Results are point estimates without confidence intervals, and fixed-noise and Laplace baselines are absent (Section 7.5).
- **Analyst inference:** Training the standard attackers only on deterministic traces introduces a distribution shift favorable to the defense. The adaptive experiment partially addresses this, but its meta-learning procedure and data allocation are not detailed enough to assess attack strength.
- **Analyst inference:** The convergence proof is difficult to reconcile with the presented finite-horizon schedule: it invokes \(t\to T\), geometric convergence, and summable variances, while the baseline is defined over finite \(T\) and experiments impose a nonzero noise floor. The supplied text does not resolve this mismatch.
- **Analyst inference:** The Gaussian-accounting presentation is incomplete: \(\varepsilon_t=\Delta/\sigma_t\) is described as an approximation, and no concrete certified total \((\varepsilon,\delta)\) value is reported for the selected experimental configuration.
- **Analyst inference:** The utility gain depends on asymmetric clipping, which may make the comparison less informative about noise alone; no critic-free or symmetric-clipping ablation is supplied.

## Open research questions

1. **Can the deployed policy obtain a tight end-to-end privacy certificate?** Private-\(\theta\) clipping is outside Theorem 1. Compare private clipping, public-proxy clipping, and a randomized feasibility projection using explicit Rényi-DP accounting and matched utility.

2. **Does the defense withstand active strategic probing?** The current adversary only observes traces. Evaluate an adaptive counterparty trained to choose offers that maximize constraint information, including repeated negotiations with composed privacy budgets.

3. **Do the findings transfer to realistic negotiations?** All data are synthetic and single-issue. Test on confidential or carefully anonymized real traces, and on multi-issue simulations with cross-attribute leakage metrics.

4. **Which mechanism component produces each effect?** The reported utility gain may arise from asymmetric clipping rather than privacy randomization. Run factorial ablations over adaptive versus fixed noise, private versus public clipping, clipping versus projection, and Gaussian versus Laplace noise with confidence intervals.

## Confidence and missing information

**Confidence: medium.** The supplied text describes the intended method and headline outcomes clearly, but omits the numerical cells of Tables 1–5, detailed simulation and counterparty-generation parameters, values of \(\beta\) and \(\delta\), concrete total privacy budgets, exact train/test sample reconciliation, random-seed counts, uncertainty estimates, adaptive-adversary training details, and any code or data-release statement. These omissions limit independent assessment of reproducibility and the strength of the formal and empirical claims.
