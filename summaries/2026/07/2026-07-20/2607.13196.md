# From Human-Centric to Agentic Code Review: The Impact of Different Generations of Generative AI Technology on Review Quality

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-20.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.13196) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13196)


## One-sentence takeaway

Across 1.02 million pull requests, agent-initiated and multi-agent reviews were associated with faster decisions in some adoption settings, but AI involvement did not consistently improve process quality and usually coincided with more review smells than human-only review.

## Problem and motivation

AI-generated code may increase review workload, while LLM and agent reviewers are changing who participates in code review and when. Prior evidence largely concerns human review or individual AI tools; the authors identify a gap in longitudinal, project-scale evidence about AI adoption trajectories, human–AI interaction patterns, and their associations with review efficiency and quality across human-centric, LLM-assisted, and agentic eras (Introduction).

## Approach and contributions

- **Longitudinal study:** The authors analyze 1.02 million reviewed pull requests from 207 active open-source GitHub projects, each containing more than 400 reviewed pull requests in each project-specific era (Sections II-A–II-B).
- **Adoption taxonomy:** Monthly AI-review participation series are normalized and clustered with soft-DTW, yielding three adoption practices (Section III-A; Figure 2).
- **Collaboration modeling:** Ordered reviewer-type comment sequences are clustered using a mixture of Markov chains fitted by expectation-maximization, producing 10 collaboration patterns (Section III-B).
- **Outcome analysis:** Review efficiency is measured in days/KLOC; six rule-based review smells represent process-quality risks. Statistical comparisons and 36 logistic-regression models relate outcomes to collaboration patterns, PR characteristics, activity, and participant experience (Sections II-D and III-C; Tables II–III).
- **Reusable asset:** The authors report releasing a replication package containing the longitudinal dataset and detailed results (Introduction).

The evidence establishes observational associations within a large, selected population of active GitHub projects. It does not show that AI participation causes faster reviews or more smells, that review smells correspond to actual escaped defects, or that agent feedback is technically more accurate. The efficiency ranks in Table V are relative within each practice and era, not universal performance measurements.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** The “agent era” is chronological: not every review in that era involves an agent, and LLM reviewers may remain active. Patterns with less than 5% support are excluded from Table V comparisons (Section V).
- **Author-stated:** Logistic-regression results are explanatory associations, not causal effects (Section V).
- **Author-stated:** Results may change as AI-review tools evolve; observations cover May 2022 through February 2026 (Section V).
- **Analyst inference:** Review smells are rule-based process-risk proxies, not direct measurements of defect detection, maintainability, or post-release quality (Section II-D; Table II).
- **Analyst inference:** Requiring continuous activity, at least 100 stars, and more than 400 reviewed PRs per era favors established, highly active public projects and limits generalization to smaller, private, or intermittently maintained repositories (Sections II-A–II-B).
- **Analyst inference:** Era boundaries begin with first observed AI participation, so contemporaneous project changes could confound before/after comparisons; no matched control projects or causal identification design is reported.
- **Analyst inference:** The supplied text reports held-out AUC as the model evaluation method but omits actual AUC results, preventing assessment of model discrimination (Section III-C).
- **Analyst inference:** The clustering silhouette score of 0.40 indicates only fair separation, and no reported robustness analysis tests alternative interpolation, clustering, or era definitions (Section III-A).

## Open research questions

1. **Do agent reviewers improve defect detection rather than only process speed?** The paper measures smells and duration, not escaped defects or finding accuracy. A next experiment could compare post-merge defects and validated review findings across matched human-only, LLM-assisted, and agent-assisted PRs.

2. **Are observed differences caused by reviewer type or assignment selection?** Agent patterns are associated with particular PR types and larger changesets, while the models remain non-causal. A randomized or carefully matched rollout could assign eligible PRs to human-only, agent-init, and agent-assist workflows.

3. **Can reviewer diversity reduce the Review Buddies signal without sacrificing efficiency?** Repeated AI identities drive much of the smell gap, but the study does not test assignment policies. A controlled deployment could rotate independent agents or combine agent and human reviewers while measuring time, feedback overlap, and validated defects.

4. **How stable are the adoption and collaboration taxonomies?** The reported adoption clusters have fair separation and come from a fixed time window. Replication on later periods, private projects, and alternative clustering specifications could test whether the three practices and 10 interaction patterns persist.

## Confidence and missing information

**Confidence: high** for the reported design and headline associations because the supplied text includes the complete methods, tables, results, and validity discussion.

Material missing information includes actual held-out AUC values for the 36 regression models; per-project and per-era sample sizes; confidence intervals and exact corrected p-values; full cluster-robustness or sensitivity analyses; detailed reviewer-labeling agreement/error rates; the complete low-frequency collaboration-pattern results; tool-specific breakdowns; and direct product-quality outcomes such as validated findings or post-merge defects.
