# LATO.2: Factorized 3D Mesh Generation with Vertex and Topology Flow

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-14.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.10623) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.10623)


## One-sentence takeaway

LATO.2 separates explicit 3D mesh synthesis into controllable vertex generation and vertex-conditioned topology generation, improving reported geometric fidelity while enabling part-wise high-resolution synthesis and connectivity regeneration after vertex edits.

## Problem and motivation

Production meshes require both accurate vertex placement and coherent, editable connectivity. Existing neural-field pipelines recover surfaces through iso-surfacing, often producing dense, irregular tessellations, while autoregressive mesh generators incur long sequences that limit speed and mesh resolution. Prior flow-based methods commonly encode continuous geometry and discrete connectivity together; the authors argue that this entanglement makes flow learning harder and contributes to drifting vertices and broken surfaces (Introduction, pp. 2–3).

## Approach and contributions

- **Factorized generation:** Separate structured latent spaces and rectified-flow models generate vertices first and topology second. Both stages share a coarse voxel scaffold, while T-Flow conditions on the realized vertex set (Figure 2; §§3–3.2).
- **Vertex representation:** V-VAE encodes a vertex displacement field and decodes vertices through coarse-to-fine sparse subdivision and pruning. A learned sub-voxel offset refines each final vertex beyond its voxel center (§3.1, Equations 2–3).
- **Topology representation:** T-VAE uses adjacency-masked attention to encode connectivity into per-vertex features, then predicts symmetric pairwise edge probabilities; faces are recovered through loop detection (§3.1, Equations 4–7).
- **Controllability and editing:** V-Flow accepts a target vertex-count condition. Re-running T-Flow after vertex manipulation supports mesh stitching, transformed-part repair, and part-wise generation without topology re-optimization (§§3.2–3.3; Figures 3–5, 9).

These results support improved reconstruction and conditional-generation metrics within the paper’s test protocol, plus the usefulness of the offset head and coarse topology condition. They do not establish perceptual quality through a user study, production readiness, generalization outside the collected datasets, or scalability beyond the reported mesh sizes. Editing and part-wise benefits are primarily qualitative, not benchmarked against alternatives.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** Vertex-stage geometric errors cannot be corrected by the downstream topology stage (p. 14).
- **Author-stated:** Pairwise topology decoding is quadratic in vertex count and may require sparse candidate selection at larger resolutions (p. 14).
- **Author-stated:** UV coordinates, textures, and materials are not generated, so outputs are not complete production assets (p. 14).
- **Analyst inference:** The principal baseline comparison lacks test-set size, category composition, sampling details, uncertainty estimates, and repeated-run variance in the supplied text, limiting assessment of whether small metric differences are robust.
- **Analyst inference:** Baselines use default inference resolutions and are evaluated only when checkpoints or generated outputs are available (§4.1), so differences in output complexity may remain a confound despite identical inputs.
- **Analyst inference:** The claimed benefit of factorizing vertex and topology flows is not directly ablated against an otherwise matched joint-latent model. Tables 4–5 test components, but not factorized versus unified training under controlled capacity.
- **Analyst inference:** Section 4.4 says a deterministic topology predictor is compared, but the supplied Table 5 contains only “without geometric condition” and full T-Flow rows; the asserted comparison is therefore numerically unsupported here.
- **Analyst inference:** Mesh stitching, transformation repair, controllability, and part-wise refinement are demonstrated visually without quantitative topology-validity, seam-quality, target-count error, runtime, or memory measurements.

## Open research questions

1. **Does factorization itself cause the improvement?** No matched joint-latent ablation is reported. Train joint and factorized models with comparable parameter counts, data, and conditioning, then compare geometry, connectivity validity, convergence, and broken-surface frequency.

2. **Can topology decoding scale beyond current resolutions?** The authors identify \(O(N^2)\) pair scoring as a limit. Compare all-pairs decoding with spatial-neighbor, learned sparse-candidate, and hierarchical decoding across increasing vertex counts, measuring quality, recall, runtime, and memory.

3. **Can topology feedback repair vertex-stage errors?** The current one-way pipeline cannot do so. Test iterative vertex–topology refinement against the one-pass pipeline, particularly on thin structures and initially misplaced vertices.

4. **How reliable are editing and part-wise synthesis quantitatively?** Current evidence is mainly visual. Evaluate seam defects, non-manifold edges, self-intersections, connected components, target-count adherence, and geometric error against editing and stitching baselines.
