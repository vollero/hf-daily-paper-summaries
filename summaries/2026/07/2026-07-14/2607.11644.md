# Motion4Motion: Motion Transfer Across Subjects at Inference

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-14.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.11644) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11644)


## One-sentence takeaway

Motion4Motion transfers source-video motion to morphologically different target subjects without skeletal priors or training by mapping tracked pixel trajectories across subjects and injecting target appearance features along those trajectories through positional self-attention control.

## Problem and motivation

Existing video motion-transfer systems largely target humans or human-like characters and depend on predefined skeletons, pose detectors, topology-specific training, or per-video optimization. This makes transfer between subjects with different morphologies difficult, while paired motion data across diverse species is scarce and semantic correspondence without a shared skeleton is ambiguous (Introduction, pp. 1–2).

The authors seek a training-free, video-based method that preserves target appearance while transferring fine-grained motion across species and other structural categories.

## Approach and contributions

- **Skeleton-free motion representation:** Sample points on the source subject, semantically match them to points on the target, and track the source points through time to obtain dense, topology-agnostic motion flows (Sec. 3.2; Fig. 3).
- **TransPE attention manipulation:** Cache target appearance keys and values, repeat them temporally, encode their keys with the transferred trajectory coordinates through RoPE, and concatenate them into WAN’s self-attention while leaving queries unchanged (Sec. 3.3; Eqs. 2–3; Fig. 4).
- **Inference-only framework:** Apply the intervention to a pretrained WAN-14B video diffusion transformer without model training or subject-specific fine-tuning (Secs. 3.1, 4.1.1).
- **Cross-morphology applications:** Demonstrate transfers across animal species, real images and videos, and a manually constrained human-to-table example (Secs. 4.2.3, 5; Figs. 6 and 8).

These results support the authors’ claim that Motion4Motion performs better than the **listed and implemented baselines** on the reported benchmarks and metrics. They do not establish performance against unavailable methods, statistical significance, real-time efficiency, or reliable transfer across arbitrary morphologies. The real-world and table demonstrations are qualitative, and the latter uses manual semantic binding.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-evidenced boundary:** Extreme cross-morphology transfer is not fully automatic. The walking-table example requires manual binding of human and table leg masks to resolve semantic ambiguity (Sec. 5).
- **Author-evidenced dependency:** The method relies on segmentation, semantic point matching, and temporal tracking; the supplied text does not evaluate sensitivity to failures in these components (Sec. 3.2).
- **Analyst inference — limited ablation evidence:** Fig. 7 compares manipulation durations qualitatively, but no quantitative component ablation isolates matching, tracking, RoPE trajectory injection, or key/value concatenation.
- **Analyst inference — benchmark breadth:** Quantitative evaluation covers 123 human pairs, 33 animal pairs, and a new 50-pair animal set. This does not by itself validate unrestricted “general subject” transfer, especially for inanimate objects or severely mismatched topologies.
- **Analyst inference — comparison coverage:** Diffusion-As-Shader and WAN-Move are named as baselines, but Table 1 reports only MotionDirector, RoPECraft, MotionClone, and FlexiAct. MotionV2V and MotionShot were excluded because implementations were unavailable (Secs. 4.1.2, 4.2.1).
- **Analyst inference — reproducibility and cost:** The paper reports one H-800 GPU and key inference settings, but not runtime, memory use, number of anchor points, random-seed protocol, code availability, or full benchmark construction details (Sec. 4.1.1).
- **Analyst inference — statistical validity:** No confidence intervals, significance tests, or inter-rater agreement statistics are provided for either automatic metrics or the user study.

## Open research questions

1. **How robust is TransPE to correspondence and tracking errors?** The method depends directly on matched and tracked anchor points, but no corruption study is reported. A next experiment could perturb matches and trajectories at controlled rates and measure MF, PS, and AC degradation.

2. **Can extreme cross-morphology transfer become automatic?** The table demonstration requires manually bound leg masks. A plausible experiment would compare manual binding with learned or vision-language-based part correspondence across a benchmark of animals, furniture, and articulated objects.

3. **Which TransPE component produces each gain?** The paper does not quantitatively separate trajectory-aware RoPE, key injection, value injection, or manipulation ranges. A factorial ablation could evaluate each component on all three quantitative benchmark subsets.

4. **What is the quality–cost trade-off?** Runtime and memory are not reported despite use of WAN-14B on an H-800. Profiling different anchor counts, layer ranges, denoising steps, and backbone sizes could reveal whether similar quality is attainable with lower inference cost.
