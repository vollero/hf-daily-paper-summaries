# Evidence-Backed Video Question Answering

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-14.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.11862) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11862)


## One-sentence takeaway

The paper introduces video QA that requires answers to be accompanied by temporal segments and tracked pixel-level masks, and reports that targeted instruction tuning improves grounding far more than model scaling alone.

## Problem and motivation

Video LLMs typically return textual answers without verifiable visual support, making it difficult to determine whether they used genuine video evidence or language priors. Existing explanations—textual rationales, temporal windows, or sparse bounding boxes—may not capture occlusion, deformation, state changes, or object identity over time.

The authors identify a gap between semantic reasoning and dense visual perception: prior grounded QA often localizes the answer entity or uses grounding as an intermediate rationale, rather than requiring the final answer to include the visual cues that logically support it (Sections 1–2, pp. 1–4).

## Approach and contributions

1. **E-VQA task:** Given a video and question, predict a triplet consisting of the semantic answer, one or more supporting temporal segments, and dense tracked segmentation masklets for supporting objects or regions (Section 3, p. 5).

2. **ST-Evidence benchmark:** A human-verified benchmark with:

   - **ST-Evidence-Gen:** generative answer, temporal localization, and masklet prediction.
   - **ST-Evidence-MCQ:** separate multiple-choice answer, temporal-evidence, and spatial-evidence selection tasks.

   Annotations use a sample–annotate–verify pipeline, with masks annotated at 6 FPS (Sections 3.1, pp. 5–6; Table 1, p. 7).

3. **ST-Evidence-Instruct:** A 160k-triplet instruction dataset built through two automated pathways: generating QA from videos with existing masks, and generating evidence for existing QA pairs. The latter decomposes grounding into evidence-object identification, bounding-box generation, and dense mask propagation (Section 4.1, pp. 7–9; Figure 2).

4. **Grounded baseline:** The authors fine-tune 3B and 7B UniPixel variants using joint language and mask losses, reporting substantial temporal and spatial grounding gains while largely retaining performance on external segmentation and video-understanding benchmarks (Sections 5.3, pp. 12–13; Tables 2 and 4).

The results establish that the proposed training recipe improves the selected baseline substantially on this benchmark, especially for grounding. The corruption study also supports—but does not prove universally—that annotated masks contain answer-relevant content.

They do not establish that masklets are complete causal explanations, that gains transfer broadly beyond the listed benchmarks, or that general Video LLM spatial scores measure only the LLM: those scores depend on a frozen UniPixel-3B proxy. Comparisons also use different inference protocols for general and grounded models.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated technical constraint:** General Video LLMs cannot directly emit tracked masks under the evaluated setup, so their spatial evidence is generated through UniPixel-3B (Sections 5–5.1, pp. 9–10).

- **Author-stated data constraint:** Dense human annotation is exceptionally labor-intensive, motivating the reuse of ViCaS masks and automated construction (Sections 3.1–4, pp. 5–7).

- **Analyst inference — protocol comparability:** General models use a single inference pass, whereas grounded models use sequential multi-turn prompting. This weakens direct attribution of differences solely to model capability (Section 5.1, p. 10).

- **Analyst inference — synthetic supervision noise:** ST-Evidence-Instruct’s temporal annotations are model-generated and its masks are partly model-generated; answer matching, confidence filtering, and automated validation reduce but cannot eliminate incorrect or incomplete evidence (Section 4.1, pp. 7–9; Table 1).

- **Analyst inference — evidence completeness:** The corruption experiment covers only 200 samples and two models. It supports relevance of annotated regions but does not show that annotations include every valid source of evidence (Sections 5.4, pp. 13–14).

- **Analyst inference — limited ablation evidence:** The supplied text does not isolate the contributions of each data pathway, filtering stage, auxiliary training dataset, or individual loss.

- **Analyst inference — benchmark scope:** ST-Evidence-MCQ contains 1,298 questions from 348 NeXT-QA videos, while ST-Evidence-Gen contains 2,706 questions. This may limit conclusions about broad real-world video diversity (Table 1, p. 7).

## Open research questions

1. **Can a general Video LLM generate tracked masks natively?**  
   This remains open because general models were evaluated through a proxy segmenter. A next experiment could add the same video-mask decoder to several general LLM backbones and compare native versus proxy J&F under identical prompting.

2. **Which instruction-data pathway produces the grounding gains?**  
   The reported training combines grounding-to-semantics, semantics-to-grounding, and auxiliary datasets without component-wise results. A factorial ablation could train matched models on each pathway alone and in combination.

3. **Are annotated masks sufficient explanations rather than merely useful regions?**  
   Corruption shows that they affect answers, but not that they capture all necessary evidence. Human studies could annotate multiple independently valid evidence sets and score models against their union or equivalence classes.

4. **How robust is E-VQA under longer videos and harder tracking conditions?**  
   Benchmark sampling restricts videos to 5–200 seconds, and masks are annotated at 6 FPS. A controlled evaluation could vary duration, occlusion, frame rate, object crossover, and deformation while measuring accuracy, temporal grounding, and J&F separately.
