# AdvancedMathBench: A Benchmark Suite for Advanced Mathematical Proof Generation and Verification

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-14.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.11849) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11849)


## One-sentence takeaway

AdvancedMathBench evaluates advanced natural-language proof generation and verification with expert-aligned process judgments, finding that tested frontier models struggle particularly with doctoral-level proofs and detecting plausible but invalid arguments.

## Problem and motivation

The authors argue that answer-centric and competition-oriented benchmarks do not adequately measure advanced mathematical reasoning because a correct conclusion can conceal invalid intermediate reasoning. Existing evaluations also offer limited undergraduate and graduate disciplinary coverage, while general-purpose LLM judges can be inconsistent or biased. The paper therefore targets rigorous evaluation of both constructing and inspecting complete natural-language proofs (Introduction; Section 2).

## Approach and contributions

- **ProverBench:** 245 proof-generation problems—200 undergraduate and 45 doctoral qualifying-exam-level—covering multiple mathematical subjects (Sections 3.1–3.2; Table 4).
- **VerifierBench:** 888 model-generated problem–proof–ground-truth triples with expert full-chain annotations, including fatal errors, recoverable errors, rationales, and error locations (Section 3.3; Table 5).
- **Expert-aligned automatic verifier:** A verifier trained with approximately 2,000 annotated examples, approximately 1,200 repaired positive examples, GRPO using rationale-aware meta-verification rewards, and eight-pass pessimistic acceptance (Sections 4.1–4.4).
- **Rationale-sensitive evaluation:** Verification outputs are assessed not only for binary validity but also for agreement with expert error analysis and localization (Section 3.3; Appendix B.3).
- **Empirical diagnosis:** The evaluations identify doctoral-level proof construction and rejection of plausible invalid proofs as major weaknesses among the tested models (Sections 5.2–5.4).

These results establish performance on the paper’s curated problems, models, prompts, and verifier-based protocols. They support the authors’ diagnosis that QE proof generation and invalid-proof rejection are difficult in this setting. They do not establish performance on all advanced mathematics, equivalence to direct human grading of every generated proof, or the independent correctness of the meta-verifier.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-described risks:** General-purpose LLM judges may be biased or inconsistent, and uncertainty-based selection creates a negative-example skew that requires positive augmentation (Introduction; Sections 2 and 4.2).
- **Analyst inference — selection effects:** Filtering easy, trivially judged, ambiguous, or malformed cases and selecting high-uncertainty trajectories deliberately produces a challenging benchmark, but its scores may not represent naturally occurring problem or proof distributions (Sections 3.1 and 4.1).
- **Analyst inference — judge dependence:** ProverBench scores depend on a learned verifier and an all-eight-passes acceptance rule. The 94-example held-out validation is limited evidence for reliability across all 245 problems, subjects, and proof strategies (Sections 4.4 and 5.4).
- **Analyst inference — meta-verifier circularity risk:** The same meta-verification framework supplies both an RL reward and an evaluation signal, potentially favoring outputs aligned with its rubric. No independent human comparison of the final model rankings is reported in the supplied text (Sections 3.3, 4.3, and 5.4).
- **Analyst inference — source and domain imbalance:** The UG and QE splits differ in size, sources, and subject composition, so the UG-to-QE decline cannot be attributed solely to academic level (Section 3.2; Table 4).
- **Analyst inference — incomplete reproducibility details:** The supplied text gives prompts and high-level training components but not full training hyperparameters, annotation agreement statistics, dataset access terms, or contamination measurements.

## Open research questions

1. **How closely do ProverBench rankings match direct expert grading?** Only a 94-sample verifier evaluation is reported. A next experiment could independently grade stratified outputs from every subject and compare model rankings, calibration, and error categories.

2. **How much of the UG–QE gap reflects difficulty rather than subject distribution?** The splits differ substantially in both source and topic mix. A matched-subject, matched-format evaluation across academic levels could separate these effects.

3. **Does pessimistic verification generalize beyond the selected trajectories?** Eight-pass unanimity improves TNR here but may reject valid unconventional proofs. Testing varying pass counts on independently sampled proofs could measure the accuracy–cost and TPR–TNR trade-offs.

4. **Can verifier feedback improve proof generation itself?** The paper evaluates generation and verification as complementary tasks but does not report verifier-guided generation. A controlled experiment could compare one-shot proofs with iterative revision using verifier error localization.

## Confidence and missing information

**Confidence: high** for the benchmark design and reported results because the supplied text includes the main methods, tables, prompts, and annotation protocol.

Material missing information includes full training hyperparameters, annotator counts and inter-annotator agreement, dataset and code availability, per-subject model results, uncertainty thresholds, exact sampling procedures, contamination analysis, inference cost, repeated-run variability, and direct human validation of the complete ProverBench scoring pipeline.
