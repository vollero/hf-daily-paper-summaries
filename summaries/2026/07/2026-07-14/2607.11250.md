# Multi-Agent LLMs Fail to Explore Each Other

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-14.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.11250) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11250)


## One-sentence takeaway

Current LLM agents often commit prematurely to peers, while the authors’ contextual-bandit framework MACE improves peer exploration and task performance, with theoretical benefits that increase under capability diversity.

## Problem and motivation

Multi-agent LLM systems must discover which peers hold useful information or possess complementary capabilities. The authors argue that existing systems generally assume effective peer selection will emerge from prompting, despite agents observing only their own interaction histories and peers adapting over time.

A controlled two-peer delegation task shows the identified gap: Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, GPT-4, and GPT-5 frequently lock onto one peer after early noisy outcomes instead of gathering enough evidence to identify the more reliable peer (Section 2; Figures 1, 7, and 8).

## Approach and contributions

- **Problem formulation:** The paper defines Multi-Agent Exploration as a partially observable stochastic game in which agents probe peers with latent capabilities and minimize regret through decentralized interaction (Section 3; Figure 2).

- **MACE:** Multi-Agent Contextual Exploration decomposes the joint game into per-agent linear contextual bandits. LinUCB selects peers using predicted utility plus an uncertainty bonus (Section 4, Equations 2–5).

- **Relational representation:** Its nine-dimensional feature vector covers response diversity, network-level peer distinctiveness, historical reward, interaction round, and a bias term (Appendix B.1, Equation 10).

- **Empirical evaluation and transfer:** MACE is evaluated under contextual heterogeneity on HotpotQA and under model heterogeneity on Math500 and GPQA; frozen HotpotQA parameters are also transferred to 2WikiMultihopQA (Sections 5.1–5.3).

- **Theory:** Under contextual-linear-bandit and capability-gap assumptions, the authors derive sublinear regret for MACE and a linear-regret failure case for greedy peer selection (Section 6; Appendix E).

The evidence supports the authors’ claim that structured peer selection can outperform prompting, random selection, and fixed connectivity in the tested settings. It also shows transfer across two similarly structured multi-hop QA environments.

It does not establish universal failure across LLMs or real-world agent systems. No statistical significance tests or confidence intervals for the tabulated endpoint comparisons are supplied, and the theory depends on assumptions stronger than the empirical POSG formulation.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** Experiments cover only small-to-medium populations, not swarms with hundreds or thousands of agents. Large populations may introduce communication bottlenecks, delayed feedback, larger interaction spaces, and stronger non-stationarity (Appendix F).

- **Author-stated:** MACE is sensitive to its exploration coefficient; both weak and excessive exploration worsen regret, with \(\alpha\approx0.5\)–\(1.0\) performing best on HotpotQA (Appendix D.2, Figure 11).

- **Author-stated:** The temporal-difference extension is inconsistent: it has higher GPQA regret and lower exploration-phase HotpotQA F1 in some comparisons, and its apparent cooperative behavior is only preliminary evidence (Appendix D.5).

- **Analyst inference:** MACE’s online reward requires ground-truth answer quality after each interaction. This may limit deployment where labels or automatic correctness signals are unavailable (Appendix B.2, Equations 19–20).

- **Analyst inference:** The main experiments use only 3–5 interaction rounds, four agents for parametric diversity, and one ten-agent backbone for contextual diversity; broader communication horizons and agent compositions remain untested (Section 5.1).

- **Analyst inference:** MACE is not isolated against a scalar LinUCB/UCB method using only counts and historical reward, so the contribution of relational features versus algorithmic optimism is unclear. The reported feature-weight analysis is descriptive rather than a removal ablation (Section 4; Appendix D.6).

- **Analyst inference:** Theorem 2’s presentation is potentially stronger than its proof: the proof constructs a stochastic instance where persistent misranking occurs with constant probability, rather than establishing linear regret for every greedy policy execution (Appendix E.3).

- **Analyst inference:** Different stochastic initial responses appear across methods in several parametric tables, and no paired-test protocol is reported; this complicates precise attribution of small endpoint differences.

## Open research questions

1. **Can MACE work without ground-truth online rewards?** The current update uses reference-based F1 or exact match. A next experiment could replace this with delayed, noisy, self-evaluated, or preference-based feedback and measure regret and calibration.

2. **Which relational features actually cause the gains?** Learned weights do not establish necessity. A controlled ablation could remove diversity, distinctiveness, history, and round features individually while holding LinUCB and random seeds fixed.

3. **How does exploration scale to large and changing agent pools?** The authors explicitly leave large-\(N\) behavior open. A next experiment could test hierarchical or candidate-pruned MACE with hundreds of agents, measuring accuracy, regret, communication cost, and adaptation after agents enter or leave.

4. **Does guided peer coverage produce genuine cooperation?** MACE-TD increases coverage, but the paper does not establish cooperative intent or system-level benefit. An intervention could constrain or randomize coverage while measuring information propagation and collective performance.

## Confidence and missing information

**Confidence: high** for the reported setups, methods, and tabulated endpoint results because the supplied text includes the full paper and appendices.

Materially missing or unclear information includes statistical significance tests, confidence intervals for endpoint tables, repeated-run counts for the main benchmarks, exact numerical regret endpoints from plotted figures, a relational-feature removal ablation, detailed API/version reproducibility for proprietary models, and empirical results at large agent-population scale.
