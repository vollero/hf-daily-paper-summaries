# A Theory of Contrastive Learning with Natural Images

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-14.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.07470) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07470)


## One-sentence takeaway

Under stationary image statistics and simplified augmentations, the authors show that contrastive learning favors sinusoidal filters and partial whitening, with the selected frequencies determined from the dataset power spectrum by a waterfilling procedure.

## Problem and motivation

The paper asks why contrastive learning produces useful visual representations despite relying on low-level augmentations—and sometimes synthetic noise images—that seemingly lack object-level semantics. Existing spectral interpretations explain aspects of contrastive objectives but, according to the authors, do not specify what networks learn from natural-image statistics or prevent trivial finite-dataset solutions without further inductive biases (pp. 1–2).

The identified gap is therefore a concrete, analytically solvable account connecting image stationarity, augmentations, contrastive objectives, learned CNN filters, and downstream recognition utility.

## Approach and contributions

- **Analyzable contrastive objective:** The authors introduce Gaussian Uniformity Plus Alignment (LGUPA), which equals their distance-based InfoNCE formulation up to a constant when embeddings are Gaussian and batch size tends to infinity (Theorem 1.1, pp. 2–3; Appendix B).

- **Spectral solution for the projection layer:** For fixed penultimate features, the optimal linear projection consists of generalized eigenvectors of augmentation-difference covariance versus feature covariance. Their scaling is found by a convex waterfilling allocation (Theorem 1.3 and Algorithm 1, p. 3).

- **Global optimum for basic augmentations:** For stationary signals with circular crops, linear brightness/contrast jitter, and ideal blur, normalized squared Fourier magnitudes—or Fourier amplitudes with ReLU—give a globally optimal LGUPA representation (Theorem 3.1, p. 5; Theorem E.1, pp. 17–18).

- **Optimal simple-CNN structure:** With cyclic crop plus noise, linear jitter, and arbitrary blur, a one-hidden-layer CNN achieves its optimum using sinusoidal first-layer filters and a projection that partially whitens selected frequencies. Frequencies and weights follow from the expected power spectrum and waterfilling (Theorem 3.2, p. 6; Appendix G).

- **Empirical validation:** Across synthetic noise, dead-leaves, CIFAR-10/100, and ImageNet, trained shallow CNNs generally learn sinusoidal or frequency-localized filters and partial whitening; richer “SOTA” augmentations instead produce more localized spatial filters (Section 4, pp. 7–9; Figures 2, 7–10).

The evidence supports the proposed mechanism for the specified objectives, stationarity assumptions, architectures, and modeled augmentations. It also shows that partial whitening explains much of the shallow models’ recognition improvement.

It does not establish that partial whitening explains modern deep contrastive systems generally, that power-spectrum matching alone causes useful semantic representations, or that LGUPA and InfoNCE are equivalent for finite batches and non-Gaussian embeddings. The transfer comparison is observational across a small set of training distributions rather than a controlled causal test of spectral matching.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** The theory focuses on shallow CNNs and simple augmentations whose performance is far below state of the art (Section 5, p. 7).

- **Author-stated:** LGUPA equals InfoNCE only under Gaussian embeddings and an infinite-batch limit. Experimental similarity does not remove this theoretical restriction (Theorem 1.1; Section 5).

- **Author-stated:** The optimum is non-unique. Gradient-descent dynamics affect which frequencies and minimum are selected, and decreasing loss can coincide with worsening recognition (Section 5; Appendix M).

- **Author-stated:** The main theory assumes stationary grayscale signals. Color requires channel decorrelation, while the nonstationary extension is supported experimentally rather than proved generally (Section 5; Appendices K and N).

- **Analyst inference:** Standard random cropping is approximated by cyclic translation plus noise; Appendix F shows similar Fourier behavior but does not prove that their learning optima are equivalent.

- **Analyst inference:** The claim that synthetic-data utility improves with power-spectrum similarity is supported by Figure 10 but lacks a controlled ablation that varies spectral statistics while holding other distributional properties fixed.

- **Analyst inference:** The supplied text gives architecture dimensions and some hyperparameters, but not a complete training recipe for every experiment, limiting exact reproducibility from this text alone.

## Open research questions

1. **Does the partial-whitening account extend to deep modern encoders?**  
   The proofs cover a one-hidden-layer CNN, while the paper acknowledges a large gap from state-of-the-art architectures. A next experiment could measure layerwise frequency sensitivity and whitening in deeper encoders while comparing observed filters with power-spectrum predictions.

2. **Which parts of realistic augmentation pipelines cause localized rather than sinusoidal filters?**  
   Random crop size/aspect ratio and nonlinear color jitter appear together in the SOTA pipeline, so their individual effects remain unresolved. A factorial ablation could add each augmentation component separately and track filter localization, whitening, and KNN accuracy.

3. **Is power-spectrum matching causally sufficient for synthetic-to-real transfer gains?**  
   Figure 10 establishes an association across datasets, not sufficiency. A controlled experiment could generate distributions sharing the same spectrum but differing in phase or higher-order statistics, then compare downstream representations.

4. **Can optimization dynamics select optima with better downstream utility?**  
   Appendix M shows that lower contrastive loss can accompany lower recognition accuracy. A next experiment could compare early stopping, explicit frequency regularization, and alternative optimizers while holding the attainable objective minimum fixed.
