# Know Before Fix: QA-Driven Repository Knowledge Acquisition for Software Issue Resolution

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-15.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.11111) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11111)


## One-sentence takeaway

ACQUIRE improves repository-level bug fixing by having specialized agents formulate and answer targeted codebase questions before a repair agent edits the repository, reaching 70.8% Pass@1 on SWE-bench Verified with DeepSeek-V3.2.

## Problem and motivation

LLM coding agents often fail because issue descriptions omit repository-specific facts such as cross-module dependencies, data flows, and implicit API contracts. Existing pre-repair methods mainly return suspicious locations, code fragments, summaries, or repair plans without explicitly identifying the agent’s knowledge gaps, leaving the repair model to infer why the retrieved context matters (Sections I–II).

An oracle study illustrates the potential value of targeted knowledge: one QA pair generated using privileged gold-patch information recovered 26 of 116 SWE-bench Lite instances previously failed by Mini-SWE-Agent with DeepSeek-V3.2. This motivates a deployable method that generates questions and finds evidence without gold information (Section II).

## Approach and contributions

- **Two-stage framework:** ACQUIRE separates repository comprehension from patch generation. A Questioner produces targeted questions, independent Answerers explore the repository in read-only mode, and a Resolver receives the resulting QA pairs before beginning repair (Section III; Figure 1).

- **Knowledge-gap taxonomy:** Questions are guided by four categories—Mechanism & Behavior, Design & Usage, Locating & Structure, and Ecosystem & Standards—derived from manual analysis of 116 oracle questions (Sections II and III-B1).

- **Parallel, evidence-grounded acquisition:** Each question is handled independently by an Answerer instructed to cite repository artifacts, acknowledge missing evidence, and avoid modifying the repository. The parallel design limits acquisition latency to the slowest Answerer rather than their cumulative runtime (Section III-B2–B3).

- **Empirical and behavioral analysis:** The authors evaluate resolution accuracy, cost, time, factual reliability, trajectory changes, regressions, category guidance, QA decomposition, and the number of injected QA pairs (Sections IV–V).

- **Released assets:** The paper states that its code and data are publicly available (Data Availability).

The evidence establishes improved single-attempt, unit-test-measured performance for one repair scaffold, two backbone models, and Python tasks in SWE-bench Verified. Ablations support the importance of decomposition and category guidance, while audits show that high aggregate support does not imply exact answers: 56.9% of audited pairs contained minor deviations. The results do not establish generalization to other languages, repair backbones, benchmarks, or production maintenance settings, and the supplied rendering omits the numerical cells of the main comparison tables.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** The four-category taxonomy may not cover all useful knowledge gaps; hierarchical, adaptive, or learned schemas remain unexplored (Section VI-B).

- **Author-stated:** Knowledge is generated once and injected statically, so it cannot adapt to hypotheses arising during repair. Dynamic acquisition could improve freshness but increase cost (Section VI-B).

- **Author-stated:** Evaluation and prompt design are tailored to Python repositories; language-agnostic operation is asserted as a possibility but not demonstrated (Section VII).

- **Author-stated:** Pass@1 and developer tests may not fully represent repair quality. Prompt design, tools, and hyperparameters may also confound the measured effect (Section VII).

- **Author-stated:** Question-quality results depend on an LLM judge, despite repeated judgments, pairwise voting, and position-bias mitigation (Section VII).

- **Analyst inference:** Factual support is not equivalent to repair relevance: nine of ten QA pairs in the five misleading regression cases were judged supported, showing that correct contextual facts can still frame the repair incorrectly (Section V-B3; Supplement III-C).

- **Analyst inference:** The study does not report repeated end-to-end repair trials or uncertainty intervals, so run-to-run stability cannot be assessed from the supplied text.

- **Analyst inference:** Only two backbone models and one shared repair scaffold are evaluated, limiting claims about broader agent architectures (Sections IV-B and IV-D).

## Open research questions

1. **Can questions be generated adaptively during repair?** Static QA cannot react to new test evidence or discarded hypotheses. A next experiment could compare static injection with budget-matched, on-demand questioning triggered after failed tests.

2. **How can a Resolver distinguish relevant knowledge from merely correct knowledge?** Most QA pairs in misleading regressions were factually supported. A next experiment could add relevance scoring, counter-hypothesis generation, or mandatory repository verification and measure Pass→Fail transitions.

3. **Does ACQUIRE transfer beyond Python and Mini-SWE-Agent?** The current evaluation is tailored to Python and one repair scaffold. A next experiment could evaluate unchanged and language-adapted prompts across several languages and agent architectures.

4. **Can the number and categories of questions be selected per issue?** Two questions work best on average, while three reduce Pass@1 despite greater cost. A next experiment could train or validate a stopping policy that predicts when another question adds nonredundant information.
