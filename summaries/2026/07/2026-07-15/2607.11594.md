# MAGIC: Transition-Aware Generation of Navigable Multi-Scene Game Worlds with Large Language Models

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-15.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.11594) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11594)


## One-sentence takeaway

MAGIC converts an English prompt into a runnable, connected multi-scene Unity project by jointly planning transitions, validating portal reachability, generating transition scripts, and evaluating transitions through gameplay execution.

## Problem and motivation

Existing LLM/MLLM systems primarily generate isolated 3D interiors; repeating them independently does not ensure a functional world. The authors identify three unresolved requirements: consistent portal endpoints and effects across scenes, physically reachable portals after furnishing, and evaluation that actually executes transitions rather than measuring only single-scene visual fidelity or prompt alignment (Introduction).

This matters because designers otherwise must manually maintain scene graphs, paired portals, transition scripts, and doorway clearance across multiple files.

## Approach and contributions

1. **Transition-aware generation pipeline.** MAGIC uses four stages: planning a shared scene/transition representation, specifying layouts with portal validation, generating Unity scenes and scripts, and combining them into one project (Section III; Figure 1).

2. **Shared transition representation.** A connected, undirected, effect-labeled automaton records scenes, portals, transition effects, and paired scene relationships. An LLM verification loop checks that required portals appear in scene descriptions (Section III-A).

3. **Navigability validation.** Scene objects are projected onto a 2D occupancy grid with 0.05-unit cells. Flood-fill reachability drives placement regeneration, although the best available layout is returned if the retry limit is reached (Section III-B).

4. **Transition benchmark and evaluator.** The authors introduce 100 procedurally constructed multi-scene cases and an evaluation agent that identifies portal candidates, activates transitions in Unity, tests approachability from spawn, and uses an MLLM to judge portal appearance (Sections IV-C and IV-E; Figure 2).

## Limitations and validity risks

**Author-stated limitations:**

- MAGIC supports only indoor scenes, Unity, English prompts, and two effects: FadeInOut and IrisWipe (Section V-C).
- Mesh-dataset coverage limits portal appearance fidelity (Sections V-B and V-C).
- Placement is best-effort; blocked portals may remain when the retry budget is exhausted (Section V-C).
- The benchmark contains 100 synthetic cases with only one to five scenes, drawn from two datasets and evaluated with one engine and one model family (Section V-D).
- Results come from one stochastic run per case, with no variance estimates, significance tests, or repeated-run analysis (Section V-D).
- Approach Rate and Portal Match Rate are proxies rather than comprehensive measures of player experience (Section V-D).
- The evaluator study covers only 20 cases and two human annotators (Section V-D).

**Analyst inferences:**

- Baseline fairness is difficult to interpret: MAGIC uses project templates, whereas the direct LLM baseline does not, which the authors identify as the main reason for its 0% executability (Section V-A.3).
- Stage-wise comparative strength cannot be independently assessed from this text because Tables II–VI are referenced without their numeric entries.
- The evaluator partly depends on an MLLM judge whose strictness can be changed through its prompt, making portal-match results sensitive to evaluator configuration (Section V-B).

## Open research questions

1. **Does performance persist in substantially larger scene graphs?** The paper tests at most five scenes. A next experiment could stratify repeated trials across 10, 25, and 50 scenes while measuring transition F1, disconnected components, runtime, and failure modes.

2. **How robust is MAGIC across engines and asset libraries?** Only Unity and one mesh collection are evaluated. Porting identical cases to another engine and multiple asset libraries would separate engine integration failures from semantic asset-coverage failures.

3. **Can navigability be evaluated more realistically than with a 2D flood fill?** The current validator projects obstacles onto a grid and requires reachability from every walkable cell. A plausible experiment would compare it with collider-aware 3D navigation or player-controller path planning, using human traversal success as reference.

4. **How stable are results across models and stochastic runs?** The paper reports single runs and no variance. Repeating every case across seeds and multiple LLM families would quantify reliability and test whether the validation loops transfer across models.

## Confidence and missing information

**Confidence: medium.** The supplied text fully describes the claimed pipeline, evaluation design, headline results, and stated validity threats, but the numeric contents of Tables I–VI are missing. Consequently, exact baseline scores, stage-level planning and portal metrics, connectivity and occupancy values, LevelLoader counts, evaluator-ablation agreement and cost, Approach Rate, and the exact Portal Match Rate are unavailable. Prompt templates beyond the Stage 1 evaluator excerpt, retry limits, full benchmark serialization details, human-annotation protocol details, statistical uncertainty, and complete reproducibility settings are also not established.
