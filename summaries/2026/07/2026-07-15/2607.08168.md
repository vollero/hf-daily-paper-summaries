# MuScriptor: An Open Model for Multi-Instrument Music Transcription

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-15.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.08168) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08168)


## One-sentence takeaway

MuScriptor combines synthetic pre-training, large-scale real-audio fine-tuning, reinforcement-learning post-training, and optional instrument conditioning to improve multi-instrument transcription of real music, with real data providing the largest reported gains.

## Problem and motivation

Automatic music transcription converts audio into symbolic notes, typically MIDI. Existing systems work better for isolated instruments or synthetic mixtures than for real multi-instrument productions, which contain overlapping frequencies, varied timbres, effects, and diverse genres. The authors identify scarce real audio with aligned note annotations—and the resulting synthetic-to-real domain shift—as the central bottleneck (§1).

Accurate general-purpose transcription could support musicians, musicologists, generative music systems, and music-information-retrieval tasks, but the authors argue that existing outputs are often too error-prone for these uses (§1).

## Approach and contributions

- **Open model:** MuScriptor is a decoder-only Transformer released with weights and inference code. It autoregressively maps five-second mel-spectrogram segments to MIDI-like tokens (§3.2; §1, contribution 1).
- **Large-scale mixed-data training:** The model is synthetically pre-trained using approximately 1.45 million MIDI files and then fine-tuned on an internal collection of 170,000 aligned real recordings totaling more than 11,000 hours (§3.1).
- **Synthetic-data analysis:** Experiments vary the amount of real fine-tuning data and compare training from scratch against synthetic pre-training (§4.1.1).
- **RL post-training:** A REINFORCE estimator with group-relative reward normalization post-trains the model on 300 manually verified tracks, using onset, frame, and offset F1 as the reward components (§3.4).
- **Instrument conditioning:** Users may specify target instrument classes, intended both to customize output and stabilize assignments across adjacent segments (§3.2; §4.1.3).
- **Scale and representation studies:** The paper evaluates models from 60M to 1.3B parameters and compares mel spectrograms with CQT, Encodec, and MERT inputs (§4.2.1–§4.2.2).

The evidence supports the authors’ claims that real recordings are crucial, synthetic pre-training improves data efficiency, and the combined training pipeline performs better than the named YourMT3+ baseline in at least some settings. It does not establish universal superiority: most internal results use a newly collected test set, many table cells are missing from the supplied rendering, and only one complete external baseline comparison is numerically recoverable from the text.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Representational limitation stated by the authors:** The tokenization cannot encode concurrent notes with the same pitch and instrument. Filtering such notes materially inflates reported scores relative to an unfiltered test (§§3.3, 4.2.3).
- **Scope limitations stated by the authors:** Future work is needed to expand the instrument vocabulary, support concurrent note activity, and lengthen segments for faster inference (§5).
- **Conditioning assumption:** Instrument-conditioned evaluation supplies the ground-truth set of instruments for each track. Benefits may be smaller when metadata is absent or incorrect (§4.1.3).
- **Internal evaluation asset:** The main test set consists of 372 manually selected tracks drawn from the same broader internal collection as the training data, although those tracks and similarly titled tracks were removed from training (§3.1).
- **Analyst inference:** Manual verification of the RL and test subsets is mentioned, but annotator procedures, agreement, and detailed genre composition are not supplied, limiting assessment of annotation reliability and coverage.
- **Analyst inference:** The text reports that RL improves results, but the supplied rendering omits Table 1’s cells; the exact gain attributable to RL cannot be independently summarized.
- **Analyst inference:** Training-data licensing, public availability, and overlap checks beyond similar titles are not established, which limits reproducibility and contamination assessment.

## Open research questions

1. **Can the model represent overlapping same-pitch notes without evaluation filtering?** This remains open because the current tokenizer cannot do so and scores fall sharply on unfiltered annotations (§4.2.3). A next experiment could compare a voice-aware or note-identifier tokenizer on both filtered and unfiltered test sets.

2. **How robust is instrument conditioning to imperfect metadata?** The reported conditioned setting uses ground-truth instrument lists (§4.1.3). An experiment could corrupt, omit, or automatically estimate those lists and measure multi F1 and cross-segment consistency.

3. **Does RL generalize beyond the 300 curated tracks and chosen reward?** The paper uses a small verified subset and the sum of three F-scores (§3.4). A next study could vary subset size and reward composition, then evaluate per genre and instrument on held-out data.

4. **How well does MuScriptor transfer across production styles and regions?** The paper claims broad genre coverage, but detailed test-set composition and subgroup results are unavailable. A stratified benchmark could report metrics by genre, recording quality, effects, and instrument rarity.

## Confidence and missing information

**Confidence: medium.** The supplied text gives substantial methodological detail and several numerical results, but the rendered contents of Tables 1–5 are missing. Consequently, the complete metric suite, exact RL and conditioning gains, most external-dataset results, and detailed representation ablations are unavailable.
