# SynthDocBench: Controlled Benchmark for Long-Context Visual Document Understanding

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-15.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.10400) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.10400)


## One-sentence takeaway
SynthDocBench is a synthetic, controlled benchmark showing that current VLMs still struggle with long-document chart reading, cross-modal evidence integration, and position-sensitive retrieval even when questions have deterministic ground truth.

## Problem and motivation
The paper addresses long-context visual document understanding: answering questions over multi-page documents containing text, tables, charts, and complex layouts. The authors argue that existing benchmarks either isolate charts, use shorter documents, or rely on real documents where difficulty factors are confounded. The identified gap is diagnostic: when a model fails on a long visually rich document, current benchmarks often cannot tell whether the cause is document length, evidence depth, modality mismatch, layout, chart perception, or reasoning complexity (§1, Fig. 1).

## Approach and contributions
1. **Controlled synthetic benchmark:** SynthDocBench contains 200 synthetic reports and 1,788 questions across chart-reading, complex multi-hop, and cross-modal subsets, with independently varied document length, depth, modality, and question type (§3.3, Table 1).

2. **Dual-layer document generation:** Each chart is rendered visually with D3.js and paired with hidden structured metadata used for deterministic answer generation, while models see only rendered page images (§3.1, Fig. 2).

3. **Difficulty- and category-controlled QA:** Questions are generated from structured evidence traces, labeled by family and L1-L5 difficulty, and filtered through numeric recomputation, automated checks, and a 100-question manual review (§3.2, Appendix F, Table 13).

4. **Empirical evaluation of frontier VLMs:** The authors evaluate eight VLMs under a vision-only protocol and analyze performance by subset, difficulty, chart position, rendering settings, and judge robustness (§4, §5).

The evidence supports the authors’ claim that SynthDocBench exposes separable weaknesses in visual long-context document QA, especially cross-modal grounding, exact chart-value reading, and sensitivity to where evidence appears in the document. It does not establish real-world performance directly, because the documents are synthetic and generated with specific rendering/layout choices; the authors explicitly note possible rendering-familiarity confounds for Gemini (§5).

## Limitations and validity risks
The authors state that SynthDocBench trades ecological validity for diagnostic control: documents are synthetic, not real-world documents (§1, Ethics Statement). They also warn that Gemini’s large margin may partly reflect familiarity with web-rendered HTML/D3.js chart styles, and that alternative rendering backends are needed to isolate this confound (§5).

Reproducibility is partly supported by released code/data claims, hyperparameters, and appendices, but the supplied text also says code and data “will be publicly released upon acceptance” in the conclusion while the reproducibility statement says they are publicly available. This inconsistency is unresolved in the supplied text.

Analyst inference: model comparisons depend heavily on the GPT-5 judge and threshold ACC definition, although Appendix C reduces this concern for Gemini-as-judge. Claude-as-judge was substantially more lenient, especially on complex questions, so judge choice remains a validity risk for absolute scores.

Analyst inference: some reporting is internally inconsistent about the number of models in certain analyses: the main evaluation lists eight models, while some captions/text refer to six models or “seven frontier VLMs.” This does not invalidate the tables, but it makes summary claims about counts harder to interpret.

## Open research questions
1. **Would the same rankings hold with non-D3 or scanned-document renderings?**  
   This remains open because the authors identify rendering familiarity as a possible confound. A next experiment would regenerate the same metadata and QA pairs using alternative chart/rendering engines and compare model deltas.

2. **How much of cross-modal failure is retrieval versus chart perception?**  
   The paper shows cross-modal questions are hardest, but the supplied text does not fully decompose retrieval, localization, and visual decoding. A next experiment would provide oracle page or chart localization and measure recovery.

3. **Can training on controlled synthetic documents improve real-document performance?**  
   SynthDocBench is positioned as diagnostic, and no fine-tuning is reported. A next experiment would train or adapt models on synthetic subsets and evaluate transfer to MMLongBench-Doc or similar real-document tasks.

4. **What presentation format is fairest across model APIs?**  
   Pages-per-strip and DPI strongly change performance, with different optima across models. A next experiment would sweep model-specific input formats under equal information budgets and report both default and optimized scores.
