# SynthDocBench: Controlled Benchmark for Long-Context Visual Document Understanding

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-15.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.10400) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.10400)


## One-sentence takeaway

SynthDocBench uses controlled synthetic reports to show that frontier VLMs still struggle with precise chart reading, distant cross-modal evidence integration, and evidence-position sensitivity in long visual documents.

## Problem and motivation

Existing document and chart QA benchmarks either isolate individual visuals or use real multi-page documents whose length, layout, modality, and reasoning demands co-vary. Consequently, a model failure cannot be attributed cleanly to visual decoding, retrieval, cross-modal grounding, evidence depth, or reasoning complexity. The authors propose a synthetic diagnostic benchmark that varies these factors independently, trading some ecological validity for interpretable failure analysis (§1–§2).

## Approach and contributions

- **Controlled benchmark:** 200 synthetic reports and 1,788 questions spanning chart reading, cross-modal reasoning, and complex multi-hop reasoning. Documents average 51.1 pages and cover 24 chart types and six layout archetypes (§3.2–§3.3; Table 1).
- **Dual-layer generation:** Each chart has a visible D3.js rendering and hidden structured metadata from which reference answers and evidence traces are derived deterministically (§3.1; Figures 2–3).
- **Diagnostic axes:** Document properties, evidence position, modality composition, question family, and L1–L5 difficulty labels support stratified failure analysis (§3; Tables 3–4, 13).
- **Vision-only evaluation:** Models receive rasterized page images rather than HTML or metadata; responses are scored by an LLM judge using a disclosed 0–10 rubric and threshold accuracy (§4; Appendix A).
- **Empirical characterization:** Eight listed VLMs are compared, with rendering, prompting, judge, OCR/text-only, positional, and difficulty analyses (§4.1–§5).

These results establish diagnostic performance differences under this benchmark’s synthetic, rasterized, LLM-judged protocol. They do not establish equivalent performance on arbitrary real-world documents, nor do they isolate model architecture or training data as causal explanations. The OCR comparison directly supports modality asymmetry for GPT-4o, but the claim that visual perception is the primary general bottleneck is broader than that single-model comparison alone proves.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** Synthetic control reduces ecological validity; scores should not be generalized uncritically to real documents (Introduction; Ethics Statement).
- **Author-stated:** D3.js/web-rendered charts may favor models familiar with that rendering distribution, potentially confounding Gemini’s advantage (§5).
- **Author-stated:** Future benchmark overfitting could target the fixed chart types, layouts, and templates (Ethics Statement).
- **Author-stated:** Image grouping, resolution, compression limits, and prompting materially affect scores (Table 5; Appendix N).
- **Analyst inference:** LLM judging remains a measurement dependency. Gemini broadly agreed with GPT-5, but validation covered only two candidate models; Claude produced substantially more lenient scores (Tables 8–9).
- **Analyst inference:** The retrieved text contains internal reporting conflicts: seven versus eight evaluated models; “six” versus eight models in failure and positional summaries; differing counts for how many models show middle-position difficulty; and inconsistent ablation numbers between Table 5 and Appendix N. These weaken confidence in some narrative summaries.
- **Analyst inference:** Quality review covered only 100 of 1,788 questions, while automated checks cannot fully establish semantic clarity or visual answerability (Appendix F).
- **Analyst inference:** Parse failures are excluded from aggregates, and the OCR baseline lost 263 of 1,788 cases. This may bias comparisons if parsing failures are capability-related (§4 and §4.1).
- **Analyst inference:** No alternative chart-rendering backend or matched real-document evaluation is reported, leaving rendering familiarity and synthetic-to-real transfer unresolved.

## Open research questions

1. **Do the diagnosed failures transfer to real documents?** The benchmark is entirely synthetic. A next experiment could construct matched synthetic and real report pairs with equivalent evidence chains and compare model error profiles.
2. **How much of the ranking depends on D3.js styling?** The authors acknowledge rendering familiarity as a confound. Re-rendering identical metadata with several independent libraries and visual styles would test ranking stability.
3. **Can retrieval and visual decoding be separated more cleanly?** The OCR baseline separates some modality effects but only for GPT-4o and with many parse failures. A factorial experiment could provide oracle page retrieval, oracle chart crops, OCR text, or full documents to each model.
4. **Can models become robust to context packaging?** Pages-per-strip and resolution changed accuracy substantially. Future evaluation could optimize packaging per model under a fixed pixel/byte budget and report both standardized and model-optimal results.
