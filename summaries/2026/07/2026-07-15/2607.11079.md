# Are LLMs Ready for Scientific Discovery? A Capability-Oriented Benchmark for AI Scientists

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-15.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.11079) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11079)


## One-sentence takeaway

SDABench shows that evaluated LLMs are strong at descriptive scientific analysis but remain unreliable when valid answers require choosing assumptions, modeling relationships or latent processes, and constructing mechanistic explanations.

## Problem and motivation

Existing scientific-data-analysis benchmarks emphasize code execution or workflow completion, which may conflate computational success with scientific validity and conceal where reasoning fails. The authors argue that evaluation should instead distinguish the scientific claims being supported—description, exploration, inference, prediction, causality, or mechanism—because each has different assumptions and validity criteria (Introduction, pp. 1–2).

## Approach and contributions

- **Capability-oriented benchmark:** SDABench covers six capabilities—descriptive, exploratory, inferential, predictive, causal, and mechanistic—across Biology, Chemistry, Environment, Geography, and Physics. Its evaluation set contains 527 real-data and 6,000 controlled synthetic instances, each represented as MCQ and OEQ (Sections 3.1 and 3.4).
- **Automated construction:** A three-stage pipeline creates typed templates with four LLM agents, derives answers programmatically, and generates perturbation-based MCQ distractors (Section 3.3; Figure 2).
- **Controlled synthetic data:** Fifty validated semantic-graph datasets—ten per domain—support every task type and retain hidden graphs, equations, causal subgraphs, and random seeds for answer derivation (Section 3.2; Figure 1).
- **Diagnostic evaluation:** Fifteen listed model variants are evaluated, including two fine-tuned variants, alongside a five-stage taxonomy that locates the first failure at Scope, Variable, Function, Relationship, or Conclusion (Sections 4.1 and 4.3).

These results establish a consistent performance hierarchy within SDABench and show that aggregate accuracy masks distinct failure stages. They do not establish that the benchmark measures end-to-end scientific discovery, that its tasks predict real research success, or that the authors’ explanations for domain and format differences are causal.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** Synthetic semantic graphs may omit real experimental confounders and measurement artifacts; MCQs can induce superficial pattern matching; recording only the first error may undercount cascading failures (Section 5).
- **Author-stated:** Real data can align with pretrained terminology and inflate mechanistic accuracy, while heterogeneous schemas and noise make exploration harder (Section 5).
- **Analyst inference:** OEQ scoring partly relies on GPT-4o when deterministic matching fails, but the supplied text gives no evaluator validation, agreement rate, or fraction of answers routed to it (Section 4.1).
- **Analyst inference:** Human review is reported for all 307 templates and 2,000 sampled instances, but reviewer count, expertise criteria, agreement, and detailed audit protocol are absent (Section 3.4).
- **Analyst inference:** Domain explanations—such as Biology being difficult because of nonlinear interactions—are plausible author interpretations, but no ablation isolates data structure from terminology, schema, or task composition (Section 4.2).
- **Analyst inference:** The reported “15 representative LLMs” include two fine-tuned variants of one base model; conclusions about model diversity should account for that composition (Sections 4.1–4.2).

## Open research questions

1. **Do SDABench scores predict performance in genuine scientific investigations?** The paper evaluates bounded questions rather than complete discovery projects. A next experiment could correlate benchmark scores with blinded expert ratings on end-to-end analyses of previously unseen studies.
2. **Which synthetic properties drive the synthetic-to-real gap?** The paper identifies possible effects from canonical names, noisy schemas, and omitted artifacts without isolating them. Controlled ablations could vary noise, confounding, variable names, missingness, and graph complexity independently.
3. **Can process-aware training reduce late-stage errors without overfitting benchmark templates?** Fine-tuning improves accuracy, but cross-benchmark transfer is not reported. A next experiment could train on selected error stages and test on held-out templates, domains, and external real datasets.
4. **How reliable is the first-error taxonomy?** Cascading errors are intentionally suppressed, and annotation reliability is not reported. Multi-label annotation by independent experts could measure agreement and compare first-error versus causal-chain diagnoses.
