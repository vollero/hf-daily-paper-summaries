# Read It Back: Pretrained MLLMs Are Zero-Shot Reward Models for Text-to-Image Generation

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-15.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.11886) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11886)


## One-sentence takeaway

SpectraReward uses a frozen multimodal language model’s image-conditioned prompt log-likelihood as a training-free reward for text-to-image RL, while Self-SpectraReward uses a unified model’s own understanding branch and often matches or exceeds much larger external reward models.

## Problem and motivation

Text-to-image reinforcement learning depends heavily on reward quality, but existing reward models often require costly preference annotations and fine-tuning. Zero-shot MLLM alternatives avoid training but may rely on noisy scalar judgments or complex prompt decomposition. The authors seek an efficient, deterministic, preference-label-free reward that directly reuses pretrained image–text alignment and works across open-source generators (Section 1).

## Approach and contributions

- **SpectraReward:** Given a generated image and its original prompt, a frozen MLLM performs one image-conditioned, teacher-forced pass over the prompt. The mean token log-likelihood becomes the scalar reward; token likelihoods form a “semantic spectrum” (Equation 1; Sections 3.1–3.3).
- **Self-SpectraReward:** For unified multimodal models, the policy’s understanding branch rewards its generation branch. The authors argue that shared components and pretraining distribution improve reward–policy alignment while eliminating an external reward model (Section 3.2).
- **Broad empirical study:** The evaluation covers two generator backbones, three RL algorithms, nine reward backbones from four MLLM families, and five out-of-distribution benchmarks (Sections 4.1–4.4).
- **Empirical findings:** Reward-backbone scaling is non-monotonic; prompt likelihood is more effective than tested scalar and VQA rewards; and an internally aligned reward can outperform substantially larger external MLLMs (Tables 2–4).

The results establish consistent benchmark improvements under the reported training recipe, reward backbones, and evaluation protocols. Ablations support the usefulness of prompt likelihood relative to two tested MLLM reward formulations and show non-monotonic reward-model scaling.

They do not establish that self-reward is universally superior: SpectraReward leads Self-SpectraReward on some metrics, including 512-resolution DPG-Bench overall (88.08 versus 87.73) and 512 TIIF overall short/long by small margins. Nor do the experiments isolate reward–policy alignment causally from every architectural difference between BAGEL and external MLLMs.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** Reward quality is bounded by the selected MLLM’s visual understanding and reasoning ability (Appendix A).
- **Author-stated:** Scoring only the original prompt emphasizes explicit semantics and may miss implicit physical or commonsense requirements such as steam implied by “hot coffee” (Appendix A).
- **Author-stated:** SpectraReward does not directly optimize aesthetics or safety and may inherit biases or visual errors from its reward backbone (Appendices A and F).
- **Author-stated:** Self-reward may align generation with the model’s own understanding rather than human preferences or safety requirements (Appendix F).
- **Analyst inference:** The alignment explanation is plausible but not fully isolated experimentally; Table 2 compares different model families and architectures, not otherwise identical reward models with alignment as the sole manipulated variable.
- **Analyst inference:** TIIF results for SpectraReward and BAGEL use GPT-4.1 evaluation, but the supplied text does not report evaluator variance or human validation (Table 9).
- **Analyst inference:** The main training recipe is resource-intensive—32 A100 GPUs for 380 steps—and the text does not provide wall-clock time or total compute, limiting cost reproducibility (Section 4.1).
- **Analyst inference:** Figure 3’s semantic-sensitivity analysis uses only four pairs and two error types, so it provides illustrative rather than broad validation of token-level interpretability.

## Open research questions

1. **Does reward–policy alignment itself cause the self-reward advantage?**  
   This remains open because external and self-reward models differ in more than alignment. A controlled experiment could vary shared encoders, tokenizers, or pretraining data independently while holding reward-model capacity fixed.

2. **Can the reward capture implicit requirements without losing its single-pass simplicity?**  
   Appendix A acknowledges weak coverage of unstated commonsense implications. A next experiment could compare original-prompt likelihood with likelihood over automatically expanded prompts on a benchmark annotated for implicit physical consequences.

3. **How should semantic alignment be combined with aesthetics and safety?**  
   These criteria are outside the current reward. Multi-objective RL could combine SpectraReward with specialized aesthetic and safety rewards, then measure trade-offs and reward hacking.

4. **How robust is SpectraReward to prompt length, phrasing, and language prior outside group-relative training?**  
   Section 3.3 argues that text-only likelihood cancels within fixed-prompt groups, leaving cross-prompt calibration unresolved. Experiments could compare raw likelihood and PMI-normalized variants across prompt lengths, paraphrases, and non-group-relative algorithms.
