# Blind-Spots-Bench: Evaluating Blind Spots in Multimodal Models

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-15.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.08317) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08317)


## One-sentence takeaway
Blind-Spots-Bench is a 235-sample diagnostic benchmark showing that modern LLMs, VLMs, and image generators still fail on human-simple tasks involving exact visual, spatial, counting, constraint, and character-level reasoning.

## Problem and motivation
The paper addresses a mismatch between strong benchmark performance and persistent failures on tasks humans find easy, such as exact string manipulation, visual counting, spatial constraints, and constrained image generation. The authors argue that many established benchmarks are too broad, saturated, or aggregate-oriented to reveal these residual “blind spots.” Their gap is a need for a benchmark built from naturally observed model failures, with fine-grained task labels and reference solutions that support model-level and task-level diagnosis.

## Approach and contributions
The authors collect candidate questions from students in a graduate AI course, asking for problems that frontier chatbots failed around October 2025, then clean and annotate them into a 235-sample benchmark.

Contributions:
1. A curated benchmark, Blind-Spots-Bench, covering text-only, image-generation, and multimodal-to-text formats, with structured reference solutions and failure-mode annotations.
2. A task taxonomy with 3 high-level categories and 12 subcategories: object-centric, abstract reasoning, and language/knowledge tasks.
3. An automated evaluation pipeline using solver models plus an AI grader, with manual validation of grader agreement.
4. A comparative evaluation of 32 LLM/VLM models and 6 specialized image-generation models.

The evidence supports the authors’ claim that the benchmark exposes concrete residual weaknesses not captured by aggregate benchmark success, especially in visual perception, counting, spatial reasoning, and strict constraints. It does not establish a general reliability ranking for all AI systems: the dataset is small, intentionally challenge-focused, imbalanced across subtasks, and lacks a measured human baseline.

## Limitations and validity risks
Authors’ stated limitations:
- The dataset is modest in size and imbalanced across subtasks, weakening statistical reliability for some fine-grained analyses.
- Questions were primarily created by students to challenge two frontier models, so the dataset may overrepresent those models’ weaknesses.
- The paper does not include a human baseline, despite describing the tasks as easy for humans.
- The benchmark should be treated as a stress test, not a complete safety or robustness evaluation.
- Public release may allow future models to overfit to the benchmark.

Analyst inferences based on the supplied text:
- Some reported tables are only partially visible in the supplied extraction, especially image-generation Table 2 and tool-use Table 3, limiting verification of detailed claims.
- AI grading is a validity risk despite high reported agreement, especially for image-generation tasks where agreement is lower at 90.9%.
- Some subtask results rely on very small counts, such as 6 attribute/pattern-recognition questions, so fine-grained percentages may be noisy.

## Open research questions
1. How large is the actual human-model gap on Blind-Spots-Bench?  
It remains open because the paper states the tasks are human-easy but does not report a human baseline. A next experiment would measure accuracy and time-to-answer for a diverse human sample on the same benchmark.

2. Do models fail because of task type, prompt wording, or dataset construction bias?  
It remains open because questions were collected from students targeting failures of specific frontier models. A next experiment would create controlled paraphrases and independently sourced tasks for each subcategory.

3. Can tool use reliably improve exact constraint and character-level tasks?  
It remains open because tool use had mixed effects and the paper reports qualitative input-handling failures. A next experiment would compare no-tool, code-tool, and structured-verifier settings with logged tool traces.

4. Are the subtask rankings stable with more examples?  
It remains open because several subtasks have small or imbalanced sample counts. A next experiment would expand each subtask to a balanced minimum sample size and recompute confidence intervals.
