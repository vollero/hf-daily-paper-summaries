# Blind-Spots-Bench: Evaluating Blind Spots in Multimodal Models

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-15.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.08317) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08317)


## One-sentence takeaway

Blind-Spots-Bench uses 235 curated, human-easy problems to reveal persistent and model-specific weaknesses across language, vision-language, and image-generation systems that aggregate benchmarks can obscure.

## Problem and motivation

Modern models score highly on established benchmarks but still fail at seemingly simple tasks such as exact string manipulation, visual counting, spatial reasoning, and constrained image generation. The authors argue that broad aggregate scores under-measure these residual weaknesses and provide too little diagnostic detail about specific capabilities.

The identified gap is a unified, fine-grained benchmark built from naturally observed failures rather than perturbations of existing benchmark questions (Sections 1–2).

## Approach and contributions

- **Diagnostic dataset:** 235 open-ended problems, reduced from approximately 287 student submissions through filtering, normalization, and quality review. Each includes a structured reference solution, correctness conditions, common failure modes, format labels, and task annotations (Section 3.1).
- **Taxonomy:** Three high-level categories—object-centric, abstract reasoning, and language and knowledge—divided into 12 subtasks such as spatial reasoning, counting, logical reasoning, and character-level manipulation (Table 4).
- **Evaluation framework:** An Inspect AI pipeline generates solver responses and uses Gemini-3-Flash with code execution for binary grading against reference solutions. The authors evaluated 32 LLMs/VLMs and six image-generation models (Sections 4.1 and 1).
- **Diagnostic analysis:** Results are broken down by modality, cost, model family, tool access, model size, and task taxonomy rather than only aggregate accuracy (Sections 4.2–4.3).

These results establish performance differences on this particular curated stress test under the stated prompting, grading, tool, and repetition settings. They do not establish general human-level reliability, causal explanations for model failures, or broad superiority of one model family across other task distributions. The claimed association with the external Intelligence Index is described as positive, but no correlation coefficient or uncertainty estimate is supplied.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** The dataset is small and imbalanced across subtasks, limiting statistical reliability, especially for categories containing only a few examples (Section 5).
- **Author-stated:** Questions were primarily designed by graduate AI students to challenge two frontier models, potentially emphasizing those systems’ weaknesses and omitting other failures (Section 5).
- **Author-stated:** No human baseline verifies or quantifies the premise that the tasks are easy for humans (Section 5).
- **Author-stated:** Limited formulations and answer distributions, along with future benchmark overfitting, prevent use as a comprehensive robustness or safety evaluation (Appendix G).
- **Author-stated:** Open-weight image generators were excluded for technical reasons, narrowing comparisons in that modality (Section 4.1).
- **Analyst inference:** Image-generation estimates are particularly uncertain because each model receives only one run, whereas textual tasks receive four.
- **Analyst inference:** AI-grader errors could affect close comparisons, especially for images, where reported human agreement is 90.9%.
- **Analyst inference:** Tool-use conclusions are difficult to quantify from the supplied text because Table 3’s numerical contents are absent.
- **Analyst inference:** Cost comparisons mix provider prices and an institutional serving platform’s prices, so they may not reflect identical infrastructure or market conditions (Section 4.1; Table 7).

## Open research questions

1. **Do the tasks reliably remain easy for a broad human population?** No human baseline is reported. A next experiment could measure accuracy, completion time, and inter-participant variation across participants with different educational backgrounds.

2. **Do the diagnosed blind spots generalize beyond the student-authored sample?** Collection targeted failures observed in specific October 2025 systems. A next experiment could construct a held-out set from independent contributors and compare category-level model rankings.

3. **Which tool-use policies improve performance without introducing input-handling errors?** Tool access produces mixed effects, but the supplied text lacks the numerical Table 3 results. A controlled experiment could compare no tools, model-selected tools, and enforced verifier calls while separately labeling tool-selection, transcription, and execution failures.

4. **How stable are rankings under alternative graders and repeated image generation?** Image grading has lower human agreement and image tasks receive one attempt. A next experiment could use multiple independent graders, blinded human adjudication, and repeated image samples with confidence intervals.
