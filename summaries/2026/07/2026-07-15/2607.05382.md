# Search Beyond What Can Be Taught: Evolving the Knowledge Boundary in Agentic Visual Generation

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-15.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.05382) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05382)


## One-sentence takeaway
The paper argues that text-to-image generators need generator-specific, selectively triggered search because indiscriminate retrieval can damage outputs, while co-training a generator and search reasoner can shift and recalibrate the boundary between internalized and externally supplied knowledge.

## Problem and motivation
The authors address a “world-knowledge bottleneck” in visual generation: generators can render plausible images but fail on requests involving recent events, long-tail entities, culturally specific artifacts, factual diagrams, niche typography, or other knowledge not reliably present in training data.

They argue this matters because real user prompts are open-ended and evolving, while generators are trained on fixed corpora. Existing benchmarks, in the authors’ view, under-measure these failures because they focus more on known concepts and compositional ability than on missing world knowledge.

The key gap they identify is not simply “add search.” They claim naive search introduces noise, corrupts concepts the generator already knows, and can cause copying or stylistic leakage. The paper frames the central problem as learning a generator-specific and evolving “knowledge boundary”: what should be learned into the generator versus retrieved at inference time.

## Approach and contributions
1. **Benchmark and corpus.** The authors construct SearchGen-20K / SearchGen-Bench from production-style prompts, with 20,839 prompts spanning 12 failure categories and 22 domains, plus SearchGen-Corpus-1M for offline replay of search-augmented workflows.

2. **Noise-resistant search reasoner.** They propose a three-stage gate-filter-integrate protocol: decide whether and how to search, select relevant search results, then integrate retrieved visual/textual evidence into generation inputs while reducing noise.

3. **Knowledge-boundary framing.** The paper introduces the idea that the optimal search policy depends on the specific generator and changes after training.

4. **Teach-then-search co-training.** The method first uses search-augmented supervision and online Diffusion-DPO to help the generator internalize stable knowledge, then uses rejection-sampling finetuning to recalibrate the reasoner to the improved generator’s new boundary.

The evidence supports the authors’ claim that world-knowledge-heavy prompts expose failures not captured by ordinary rendering benchmarks, and that selective retrieval is preferable to always-on retrieval in their setting. It also supports, within the reported experiments, that co-training improves two open generators and that the search policy depends on the paired generator.

It does **not** establish that the proposed recipe closes the gap to frontier commercial systems: the authors themselves report a large remaining gap to GPT-Image-2. It also does not fully isolate whether gains come from better search decisions, better prompt rewriting, generator DPO, judge-specific optimization, or interactions among these components, because the supplied text gives only partial ablation detail.

## Limitations and validity risks
The authors state several limitations. Their co-training recipe uses only one DPO pass and one RFT pass, so multi-round convergence, instability, and diminishing returns remain untested. They also note that experiments use 4B and 7B generators, leaving the scaling behavior of the knowledge boundary unclear. The reward/evaluation signal relies on an automated VLM judge, which they acknowledge can introduce noisy rewards despite reported human correlation.

Analyst inference: the comparison to commercial systems is hard to interpret causally because Appendix G says public documentation does not specify whether those systems use retrieval, tools, or proprietary knowledge bases internally. Their scores are therefore end-to-end product comparisons, not controlled model-only comparisons.

Analyst inference: the supplied text references many table results whose full numerical contents are not included in the retrieved markdown, especially Tables 4, 5, 6, 7, 9, 10, and 11. That limits independent verification of several aggregate claims.

Analyst inference: because the same general evaluation protocol supplies training rewards and reported metrics, there is a risk of optimizing toward the judge’s preferences rather than human-perceived fidelity, although the paper partly addresses this with the ρ=0.87 human correlation result.

## Open research questions
1. **Does multi-round co-training continue to improve the boundary?**  
   This remains open because the paper reports a deliberately minimal single DPO plus single RFT cycle. A plausible next experiment is to run several alternating DPO/RFT rounds and track per-stratum gains, regressions, and convergence.

2. **How does generator scale change what should be searched?**  
   The paper tests 4B and 7B generators and states that larger models may shift the internalizable/contextual split. A next experiment would compare the same calibrated protocol across substantially larger generators while keeping the benchmark and search corpus fixed.

3. **Which failure categories resist internalization?**  
   The paper suggests some categories, such as cultural specificity or temporal recency, may scale differently, but the supplied text does not give category-level co-training outcomes. A next experiment would report boundary shifts and search benefit by each of the 12 failure categories.

4. **Can the boundary be predicted without full co-training?**  
   The authors raise this as a future direction but do not test it. A plausible experiment would train probes or uncertainty estimators to predict when search improves output, then compare them against RFT-calibrated reasoners.
