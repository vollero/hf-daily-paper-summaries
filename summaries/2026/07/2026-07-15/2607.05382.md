# Search Beyond What Can Be Taught: Evolving the Knowledge Boundary in Agentic Visual Generation

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-15.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.05382) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05382)


## One-sentence takeaway

The authors propose co-training an image generator and search reasoner so the system selectively retrieves external knowledge only when useful, improving knowledge-intensive text-to-image generation while reducing corruption from unnecessary search.

## Problem and motivation

Modern image generators can render plausible images while fabricating unfamiliar, recent, culturally specific, or long-tail content. The authors call this mismatch between fixed training corpora and open-ended user requests the “world-knowledge bottleneck” (§§1–2).

Existing benchmarks emphasize rendering and composition within familiar concepts, obscuring this failure mode. Search is a natural remedy, but indiscriminate retrieval can override correct internal knowledge or leak unwanted style, layout, and background information into the output (§2.5; Figure 7). The identified gap is therefore not simply better retrieval, but coordination between each generator’s evolving internal knowledge and its search policy.

## Approach and contributions

1. **Evaluation and training resources.** SearchGen-20K and SearchGen-Bench cover twelve failure categories and twenty-two domains, with prompt-specific knowledge annotations and automated evaluation criteria. SearchGen-Corpus-1M freezes multimodal search sessions for offline replay (§2.2; Tables 2–3).

2. **Selective multimodal search.** An 8B vision-language reasoner follows a gate–filter–integrate protocol: decide whether and how to search, select minimally distracting evidence, and translate retrieved visual/textual evidence into a grounded generation specification (§3.1; Figure 8).

3. **Teach-then-search co-training.** Online iterative Diffusion-DPO first trains the generator from search-augmented preference pairs, then rejection-sampling finetuning retains positive-advantage reasoner trajectories to recalibrate search to the strengthened generator (§3.3; Algorithm 1).

4. **Generator-specific knowledge boundary.** The authors operationalize the division between knowledge that can be internalized and knowledge that remains context-dependent, arguing that this boundary changes with generator training (§3.2; Figure 9).

| Finding | Evidence / setting | Location in paper |

| The degradation is concentrated in knowledge-sensitive dimensions. | On the 651-prompt Search-Intensive subset, Flux.2-Klein-9B scores 24.2 on Checklist and 16.9 on Visual Reference Fidelity, versus 36.8 on Image Quality and 48.6 on Physical Plausibility. | §2.4; Table 4 |

| Co-training improves Klein-4B across successive phases. | On the 751-prompt test evaluation, generator DPO adds 2.8 points over Phase 0 and reasoner RFT adds another 2.6; Phase 2 reaches 31.8 versus 31.2 for the frontier-reasoner oracle on the same generator. | §4.1; Table 6 |

| Calibration is generator-specific. | The calibrated reasoner scores 31.8 with its intended Klein-4B-DPO generator but 26.8 when paired with base Klein-4B. | §4.1; Table 6 |

The evidence supports the authors’ claims that missing knowledge is distinct from rendering quality, indiscriminate search can be harmful, and one co-training iteration improves the tested generator–reasoner pairs. It does not establish convergence under repeated co-training, generalization to larger open generators or other modalities, or causal proof that all commercial-system advantages arise from integrated search; the paper explicitly says their internal retrieval behavior is undocumented (Appendix G.1).

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** Only one DPO pass and one RFT pass are evaluated, leaving multi-round convergence, diminishing returns, and instability unknown (Appendix F).

- **Author-stated:** Co-training is demonstrated with 4B and 7B generators; the best result remains far below frontier commercial systems, so scaling and architectural generality remain unresolved (Appendix F).

- **Author-stated:** Training and evaluation rely on automated VLM rewards. Although human correlation is ρ=0.87, agreement is weaker for typography- and layout-heavy TextualSearch prompts (Appendix B.2; Appendix F).

- **Author-stated:** Cached web-search results may inherit search-engine bias, while commercial APIs are not controlled under identical conditioning assumptions (Appendix G).

- **Analyst inference:** Generator-aware calibration is tested on only two architectures and one benchmark family, limiting evidence that the learned boundary transfers across datasets or production distributions.

- **Analyst inference:** The supplied text reports the gate–filter–integrate pipeline jointly but does not provide a clear component-wise ablation isolating each stage’s contribution.

- **Analyst inference:** Dataset accounting is internally inconsistent: the abstract reports 20,839 prompts, while §2.2 reports 20,188 training rows plus 751 test prompts; Appendix A.1 gives another split description. The exact corpus cardinality and overlap cannot be resolved from the supplied text.

- **Analyst inference:** The benchmark uses LLM-assisted prompt synthesis and an answer-first construction process, so performance on fully organic, unseen production prompts is not directly established.

## Open research questions

1. **Does repeated co-training converge or destabilize?** Only one DPO–RFT cycle is tested. A next experiment could run several alternating cycles while tracking per-stratum gains, search frequency, and regressions on NoSearch prompts.

2. **How does generator scale change the knowledge boundary?** Results cover 4B and 7B generators. A controlled scaling study could hold the reasoner, retrieval corpus, and evaluation fixed while varying generator size and architecture.

3. **Can search necessity be predicted without expensive rollouts?** The current boundary emerges from scored generations. A next experiment could compare uncertainty probes or internal classifiers against rollout-derived search-benefit labels.

4. **Which protocol stages actually prevent search noise?** The supplied results do not isolate gate, filter, and integrate. A factorial ablation could remove or replace each stage and measure concept corruption, reference copying, and overall scores separately.
