# What LLM Forecasters Know but Don&#x27;t Say: Probing Internal Representations for Calibration and Faithfulness

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-15.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.08046) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08046)


## One-sentence takeaway
The authors claim that lightweight probes on LLM internal activations can recover better-calibrated confidence and detect evidence-driven forecast shifts that the model’s verbalized confidence and chain-of-thought often fail to reveal.

## Problem and motivation
The paper addresses two trust problems in LLM forecasting: models can be accurate but poorly calibrated, and their chain-of-thought reasoning may omit or misrepresent the evidence that actually drives a forecast. This matters because users need both reliable uncertainty estimates and auditable reasoning when using forecasters for decision support. The identified gap is that verbalized probabilities and written rationales may be unreliable, while internal representations may contain more faithful confidence and evidence-use signals.

## Approach and contributions
The authors train small probes on frozen intermediate activations to predict whether a model forecast will be correct. They test mean-pooling, attention-pooling, and covariance-pooling probes across layers and reasoning positions.

Contributions:
1. They show probe-derived confidence is better calibrated than verbalized confidence for EF-8B on OpenForesight.
2. They extend probe-only calibration to frozen GLM-4.7-Flash and GLM-4.5-Air models.
3. They test chain-of-thought faithfulness with evidence ablation and misleading-evidence injection.
4. They use probe shifts as an audit signal for “silent” behavioral changes.
5. They study forced answering in EF-32B and propose routing questions by pre-reasoning answer spread to save tokens.

## Evidence and main results

| Finding | Evidence / setting | Location in paper |
|---|---|---|
| EF-8B is overconfident across temperatures | On OpenForesight-test*, accuracy stays around `37±1%` and verbalized confidence near `50%` for `T≲1.6`; ECE is `0.110–0.150`. | Section 4.1.1; Appendix B; Figures 12–13 |
| Forecast accuracy depends strongly on whether the gold answer appears in retrieved evidence | EF-8B reaches `86%–94%` accuracy when the prompt contains the gold answer versus `0.26–0.33` when absent; reported deltas are `+57%` to `+65%`. | Section 4.1.2 |
| EF-8B covariance probe is better calibrated than verbalized confidence | Layer-21 covariance probe on OpenForesight-test: ECE `0.044` vs verbalized ECE `0.093`, with similar AUROC `0.756` vs `0.758`, `N=3,020` rollouts. | Section 4.2.2; Figure 1 |
| Probe calibration advantage persists on OOD forecasting splits | On `skysports`, probe/verbalized ECE `0.064` vs `0.089`; on `aljazeera`, `0.140` vs `0.178`; verbalized AUROC on `aljazeera` is `0.587`. | Section 4.2.2 |
| Frozen GLM probes improve raw calibration | GLM-4.7-Flash: probe ECE `0.054` vs verbal ECE `0.287`; GLM-4.5-Air: probe ECE `0.102` vs verbal ECE `0.255`, each on `2,960` test rollouts. | Section 4.3; Table 1; Figures 2–3 |
| GLM ranking gains are stronger for GLM-4.5-Air than GLM-4.7-Flash | GLM-4.5-Air clean ΔAUROC `+0.110 [0.069, 0.149]`; GLM-4.7-Flash clean ΔAUROC `+0.055 [-0.006, 0.111]`, so the latter interval includes zero. | Section 4.3; Table 1; Appendix D |
| Math probes improve OOD correctness discrimination | On pooled AIME24/25 + AMC23/24, `n=580`: untrained Qwen3-8B AUROC `0.894` vs best non-probe `0.752`; DCPO-recipe Qwen3-8B AUROC `0.929` vs `0.865`. | Section 4.4; Table 2; Figure 5 |
| Math calibration result is mixed | On OOD math, untrained model probe ECE `0.083` vs best non-probe `0.270`; DCPO-recipe model probe ECE `0.143` vs best non-probe `0.125`, so recalibrated verbal confidence is better by ECE there. | Section 4.4; Table 2 |
| Chain-of-thought weakly tracks evidence ablation impact | Across `1,303` ablation pairs from `354` questions, behavioral change and reasoning change have Spearman `ρ=0.215`; among `460` high-impact pairs, `107` show no reasoning change, a `23%` stealth-influence rate. | Section 4.5.1; Figure 6a |
| Misleading injected evidence is usually disclosed when used | In `489` questions, `81.2%` are honest-susceptible, `2.5%` are stealth at question level, and `46/1,227` susceptible completions are stealth. | Section 4.5.2; Figure 6b |
| Probe shifts track behavioral shifts better than CoT text | Across `1,792` perturbation cases, probe change tracks probability change with Spearman `ρ=0.565`, signed shifts `ρ=0.641`, and predicts direction in `83.6%` of cases. | Section 4.5.3; Figure 7 |
| Probe detects some silent ablation effects | In `107` stealth ablation cases, mean `|∆probe|=0.065` and direction agreement is `78.5%`; matched faithful cases have mean `|∆probe|=0.088`. | Section 4.5.3; Figure 7b |
| EF-32B answers and confidence are often fixed before reasoning | Forced and free confidence correlations are Spearman `0.90` on test, `0.87` on Late2025, `0.78` on 2026Q1; forced answer matches free modal answer on `67%`, `64%`, and `56%` of questions respectively. | Section 4.6; Figure 8 |
| Chain-of-thought gives small accuracy gains | Free reasoning beats forced answering by `+1.9pp` in distribution, with `95% CI [+1.0, +2.9]`; OOD gains attenuate, including non-significant `+0.9pp` on Late2025. | Section 4.6; Figure 8c |
| Pre-reasoning answer spread can save tokens | A forced-pass routing gate saves `30–47%` of generated tokens with no measurable accuracy loss; per-split changes are within about `1pp` of full reasoning with CIs including zero. | Section 4.7; Figure 9 |

The evidence supports the authors’ core claim that internal activations contain useful correctness and evidence-sensitivity signals not fully reflected in verbalized confidence or CoT. It does not establish that probes reveal the model’s true causal reasoning mechanism, nor that the results generalize beyond the tested models, datasets, prompting schemes, and probe-training setups.

## Limitations and validity risks
Authors’ stated or implied limitations include possible dataset issues: the OpenForesight audit finds shared qids across splits, though the authors judge them to be collisions rather than duplicate questions; semantic near-duplicates are not ruled out by the exact-match audit (Appendix A, Figure 10).

The GLM experiments have leakage caveats. GLM-4.7-Flash often has drafted answers in the chain-of-thought before the final answer block, reducing the leakage-controlled comparison to `423` usable rollouts; its clean AUROC interval includes zero (Appendix D).

The math experiment is framed as an OOD monitoring result, not proof of training-invariant correctness representations. The authors state that the DCPO probe-train pool overlaps the RLVR training distribution through DeepScaleR (Section 3.2; Appendix E).

Analyst inference: the faithfulness tests measure response to controlled perturbations, not all forms of CoT faithfulness. Text similarity and LLM-judge scoring may miss subtler reasoning changes, and the paper does not show that probe shifts are causally involved in forecast formation.

Analyst inference: reproducibility is limited by non-public or model-generated components mentioned in the text, including the improved compact-context dataset and injected articles generated by Claude Sonnet.

## Open research questions
1. Can models be trained to verbalize the calibrated internal signal?
   This remains open because the paper shows the signal exists but does not train the base model to report it. A next experiment would distill probe probabilities into verbalized confidence and evaluate ECE/AUROC before and after training.

2. Do probe signals generalize across forecasting datasets and retrieval systems?
   The paper uses OpenForesight variants and selected OOD splits, but retrieval quality strongly affects accuracy. A next experiment would train probes on one forecasting corpus and test on another with a different retrieval pipeline.

3. Are probe shifts causally related to forecast changes?
   The paper shows correlation between probe shifts and behavioral shifts, not causal mediation. A next experiment would intervene on activation directions associated with probe predictions and measure whether forecasts change.

4. Can high-entropy forced-answer cases be improved by retrieval routing?
   The authors propose retrieval for high-spread cases but explicitly do not test it. A next experiment would route high-entropy questions to additional retrieval and compare accuracy, calibration, and token cost.

## Confidence and missing information
Confidence: `high` for summarizing the supplied text; `medium` for broader interpretation of validity risks.

Material missing information: full code, exact probe implementation details beyond the paper description, access to the improved non-public dataset, injected-article generation prompts, LLM-judge reliability details, full per-split confidence intervals for routing, and independent replication on additional forecasting benchmarks.
