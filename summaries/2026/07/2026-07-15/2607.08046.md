# What LLM Forecasters Know but Don&#x27;t Say: Probing Internal Representations for Calibration and Faithfulness

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-15.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.08046) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08046)


## One-sentence takeaway

The authors find that lightweight probes over frozen LLM activations provide better-calibrated correctness estimates than verbalized confidence, reveal forecast changes omitted from chain-of-thought, and enable pre-reasoning routing that reduces generation by 30–47% without measurable accuracy loss.

## Problem and motivation

Accurate LLM forecasters may still be unreliable because their stated probabilities are miscalibrated and their chain-of-thought (CoT) may omit evidence that actually drives their predictions. This matters for decision support: users cannot meaningfully assess uncertainty or audit a forecast if confidence and explanations are unreliable.

The identified gap is whether intermediate representations expose correctness and evidence sensitivity more directly than model-generated confidence or reasoning. The paper also asks how much of a forecast is already determined before CoT begins (Sections 1 and 4.6).

## Approach and contributions

- **Activation-based confidence estimation:** Train mean-, attention-, and covariance-pooling classifiers on frozen residual-stream activations to predict forecast correctness, primarily for Eternis-Forecaster 8B (EF-8B), with probe-only tests on two frozen GLM models (Sections 3.1–3.3, 4.2–4.3).
- **Cross-domain stress test:** Compare correctness probes with token-logprob and verbalized-confidence baselines on pooled OOD AIME/AMC math rollouts from base and DCPO-recipe Qwen3-8B models (Section 4.4; Table 2).
- **Causal CoT auditing:** Remove genuine articles or inject fabricated ones, then compare changes in stated forecast probability, probe output, and written reasoning (Section 4.5).
- **Pre-reasoning forecast analysis:** Force EF-32B to answer with an empty think block, using its candidate-answer distribution to study pre-commitment and route questions between immediate answering and full reasoning (Sections 4.6–4.7).
- **Main claimed contribution:** A unified use of internal probes for calibration, detection of undisclosed evidence influence, and inference-time triage without updating the underlying model.

The results support the authors’ claim that model internals contain useful correctness and evidence-sensitivity signals beyond explicit self-report. They do not establish that probes reveal the model’s true causal computation, that their probabilities transfer without retraining to unseen model families, or that the routing policy improves accuracy through retrieval—the proposed retrieval branch was not tested.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** The compact-context forecasting dataset is non-public (Section 3.2), limiting exact reproduction of parts of the study.
- **Author-stated:** GLM-4.7-Flash often drafts its answer inside CoT; after leakage filtering only 423 usable rollouts remain, and its clean probe-ranking interval includes zero (Appendix D).
- **Author-stated:** The math probe-training pool overlaps the RLVR training distribution through DeepScaleR, so similar probe behavior does not demonstrate training-invariant representations (Sections 3.2 and Appendix E).
- **Author-stated:** The evidence-ablation and injection stealth rates have different denominators and are not directly comparable (Section 4.5.2).
- **Author-stated:** Retrieval routing is proposed but not evaluated (Section 4.7).
- **Author-stated:** The data audit checks exact identifiers and text but does not rule out semantic near-duplicates (Appendix A).
- **Analyst inference:** Most forecasting evidence centers on one model family, while GLM experiments train model-specific probes; broad cross-model transfer remains unsupported.
- **Analyst inference:** Answer correctness and CoT citation/stance depend on LLM judges, but the supplied text reports no human-agreement study for these judgments.
- **Analyst inference:** A probe’s correlation with changed output behavior is evidence of a useful audit signal, not proof that the decoded feature caused the forecast.
- **Analyst inference:** The paper sweeps layers, pooling families, and read sites with validation selection, but the supplied text does not provide a fully independent replication across seeds for the headline forecasting probes.

## Open research questions

1. **Can models directly verbalize the calibrated signal decoded by probes?** The paper shows a gap between internal and stated confidence but does not train the model to close it. A next experiment could distill probe probabilities into self-reports and evaluate held-out/OOD ECE, AUROC, and any change in the original internal signal.

2. **Do correctness probes transfer across models or domains without retraining?** Each reported probe is trained for its target model, and math is only a separate stress test. Train on one model or forecasting corpus, then evaluate the frozen probe or a mapped representation on another model and domain.

3. **Does probe-guided retrieval improve accuracy and cost?** The high-entropy branch is hypothesized to need more evidence, but retrieval was not run. Compare full reasoning, entropy-gated retrieval, random retrieval, and always-retrieve policies on accuracy, calibration, latency, and tokens.

4. **How robust is the “lie detector” beyond article perturbations?** The study uses single-article removal and fabricated-news injection. Future tests could vary evidence quality, contradiction strength, ordering, and multi-source interactions while preregistering behavioral and textual faithfulness criteria.
