# Let RGB Be the Language of Vision

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-15.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.12450) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12450)


## One-sentence takeaway
RINO reframes many vision understanding and conditioned-generation tasks as RGB-to-RGB image editing, showing that frozen generic image editors can perform a broad set of tasks zero-shot, though often below specialist models and with important evaluation caveats.

## Problem and motivation
The paper addresses the fragmentation of vision systems: different tasks use different representations, encoders, decoders, heads, and adapters, unlike language models that operate through a shared text interface. The authors argue this limits free-form transfer across vision tasks. Their proposed gap is whether RGB itself can serve as a common “visual language” for both structured perception outputs and structured generation inputs.

## Approach and contributions
RINO, or RGB In and RGB Out, represents natural images, masks, depth maps, normal maps, pose skeletons, edge maps, and other structured signals as RGB images.

Key contributions:

1. A unified RGB/text-to-RGB/text formulation for both visual understanding and conditioned image generation.
2. Use of frozen pretrained image editors, including Qwen-Image-Edit, LongCat-Image-Edit, and FireRed-Image-Edit, without task-specific fine-tuning, heads, adapters, or auxiliary parameters.
3. Parameter-free conversion modules before or after inference, such as color decoding for masks or luminance decoding for depth.
4. A broad zero-shot evaluation across dense understanding, 3D/geometry-related tasks, grounding, segmentation, pose, and conditioned generation.

The evidence supports the authors’ central claim that RGB can be used as a practical shared interface across many perception and generation tasks. It does not establish that RINO matches specialist systems in general: several results are qualitative, many exact table values are absent from the supplied text, and some comparisons use oracle information, alignment protocols, paper-reported anchors, or different resolutions.

## Limitations and validity risks
The authors state that RINO-Zero depends on strong generic image-editing backbones whose web-scale pretraining may already contain visual signals such as masks and depth maps. They also acknowledge gaps to specialist models on some applications and propose future instruction tuning with RGB-formatted non-natural visual data.

Other validity risks supported by the text:

- Some evaluations use oracle information, including oracle classes and counts for COCO detection/instance segmentation and oracle present classes for segmentation/panoptic settings.
- Some comparisons are not directly comparable, such as Vision Banana raw metric depth vs. affine-invariant editor depth in Table 2.
- Generation comparisons sometimes use paper-reported external anchors rather than locally re-scored baselines.
- The depth-conditioned generation setup has a resolution caveat: editor outputs are generated at native 1024-pixel resolution from 512-pixel control maps, while public depth-control baselines are reported at 512 pixels.
- Several tables are referenced but their numeric contents are not included in the supplied text, limiting independent assessment.

Analyst inference: the reliance on parameter-free RGB decoding may make results sensitive to color jitter, palette mismatch, thresholding, connected-component choices, and post-processing, because the paper itself describes such refinement and decoding steps for multiple tasks.

## Open research questions
1. Can RGB-interface instruction tuning close the gap to specialist models?
   The paper proposes this as future work but reports only fully zero-shot RINO-Zero. A plausible next experiment is to fine-tune one editor on mixed natural editing data plus RGB masks, depth, normals, and pose maps, then compare against the same zero-shot protocol.

2. How much performance comes from pretraining exposure to structured visual signals?
   The authors note that web-scale editor pretraining may contain masks and depth maps. A plausible experiment is to compare editors with known filtered pretraining distributions or run contamination-style probes for structured visual formats.

3. How robust is RINO without oracle task information?
   Several tasks use ground-truth-present categories, counts, or crops. A plausible next experiment is a fully open-set version of detection, panoptic segmentation, and semantic segmentation without oracle class lists or counts.

4. Which RGB encoding choices are most reliable?
   The paper uses grayscale depth, RGB normal maps, color masks, skeletons, and binary edges, but does not fully isolate encoding design. A plausible experiment is an ablation over palettes, quantization, resolution, and decoding rules.
