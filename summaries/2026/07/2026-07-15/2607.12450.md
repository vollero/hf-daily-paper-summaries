# Let RGB Be the Language of Vision

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-15.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.12450) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12450)


## One-sentence takeaway

RINO reformulates 25 perception and conditioned-generation tasks as prompted RGB-to-RGB editing, allowing frozen general-purpose image editors to perform them zero-shot with parameter-free format conversion, though performance and evaluation comparability vary substantially by task.

## Problem and motivation

Vision tasks conventionally use incompatible representations—natural RGB images, categorical masks, continuous depth maps, pose skeletons—and therefore often require separate encoders, decoders, heads, or conditioning adapters. The authors argue that this fragmentation obstructs cross-task transfer and general-purpose interaction. They investigate whether RGB can instead serve as a common interface for both reading structured information from images and using structured information to generate images (Introduction; Figure 1).

## Approach and contributions

- **Unified formulation:** RINO represents natural images and structured signals as RGB and casts perception and generation as dual RGB/text-to-RGB/text transformations (Section 2, Equation 1).
- **Frozen-editor implementation:** RINO-Zero uses released Qwen-Image-Edit, LongCat-Image-Edit, and FireRed-Image-Edit weights without fine-tuning, architectural changes, or task-specific parameters (Section 2, “Base models”).
- **Parameter-free translation:** Pre/post-processing converts masks, depth, normals, poses, and other structures between RGB and evaluation formats; some tasks also use morphological or filtering heuristics (Sections 2 and 3.1).
- **Broad evaluation:** The framework is tested across 25 tasks covering dense estimation, language grounding, segmentation, and several forms of conditioned generation (Related Work; Conclusion).
- **Image-versus-text ablation:** On Canny-conditioned generation, the RGB edge map carries substantially more spatial control than either the original caption or a textual description of the edge map (Section 3.2; Table 15).

The evidence supports the authors’ central feasibility claim: one frozen editor can consume and emit multiple RGB-encoded signal types without task-specific learned modules. It does not establish parity with specialists across all tasks, nor that RGB is universally sufficient. Several protocols simplify the task through oracle labels/counts, ground-truth alignment, recaptioning, or task-specific decoding. Some comparison rows are imported from other papers, and resolution or evaluation differences limit direct comparability.

## Limitations and validity risks

- **Author-stated:** Performance depends on visual priors learned by generic editors from web-scale data that may contain masks, depth maps, or related signals; the content and prevalence of such data are unknown (Introduction).
- **Author-stated:** Accuracy is limited by the backbone and parameter-free RGB read-out, leaving gaps to specialists on some tasks (Conclusion).
- **Author-stated:** The current study covers images; 3D vision, video, and world models remain proposed extensions (Introduction; Conclusion).
- **Analyst inference:** “Zero-shot” does not isolate acquisition of task knowledge because relevant structured imagery may already occur in editor pretraining.
- **Analyst inference:** Oracle classes/counts in detection, oracle-present classes in some segmentation protocols, ground-truth boxes for pose crops, and per-image depth or pose alignment make several results easier than unrestricted deployment (Section 3.1).
- **Analyst inference:** Hierarchical segmentation, filtering, connected-component processing, palette resolution, and coordinate-convention selection introduce task-specific procedural design despite the absence of learned modules.
- **Analyst inference:** External baseline scores are sometimes not locally re-evaluated; Table 11 also notes a 1024-versus-512 resolution caveat. These factors weaken strict cross-method comparisons.
- **Missing evidence:** The Introduction claims strong human-preference results and a new zero-shot state of the art, but the supplied text contains neither the human-study protocol nor numerical preference results. The reported generation evidence is mixed and explicitly trails trained systems on depth and Canny control.

## Open research questions

1. **Does explicit RGB-signal training improve transfer without harming ordinary editing?** The paper proposes instruction-tuning but evaluates only frozen editors. A next experiment could tune on a controlled mixture of natural editing and structured RGB tasks, then measure seen-task gains, unseen-task transfer, and editing regression.

2. **How much performance comes from pretraining exposure rather than interface generality?** The authors cannot characterize web-scale exposure to masks or depth maps. A plausible experiment would compare editors trained on audited datasets with and without structured visual signals.

3. **Can unrestricted recognition replace oracle-assisted protocols?** Several evaluations provide present classes, counts, boxes, or alignment to ground truth. A next experiment could remove each oracle input separately and report the resulting recognition and localization changes.

4. **Which RGB encodings are robust to generation artifacts?** Color jitter, palette instability, and decoding ambiguity affect fine categories and instances. An experiment could compare alternative palettes, redundancy/error-correcting encodings, resolutions, and decoders under identical backbones.
