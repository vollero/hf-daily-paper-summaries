# Tracing Agentic Failure from the Flow of Success

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-16.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.12747) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12747)


## One-sentence takeaway
The paper proposes Oat, a one-class Neural CDE method that trains only on successful LLM-agent trajectories and localizes failure-contributing steps by scoring deviations from learned successful trajectory dynamics.

## Problem and motivation
The authors address failure attribution for LLM-based agentic systems: identifying which step or steps in a failed trajectory caused the task failure. They argue this matters because long-horizon agent trajectories can contain many actions across tools or agents, making manual debugging slow, costly, and inconsistent. The identified gap is that prior approaches either depend on expensive prompting with frontier LLMs or require step-level annotations on failure trajectories, which the authors describe as costly, ambiguous, and hard to scale (Introduction; Problem Statement).

## Approach and contributions
1. **New formulation:** The paper defines unsupervised failure attribution, where the model is trained only on successful trajectories and predicts failure-contributing steps at inference time for failed trajectories (Section 3, Definition 3.1).

2. **Method:** Oat models successful agent trajectories as continuous latent paths over LLM-extracted step representations using Neural Controlled Differential Equations, then assigns per-step anomaly scores using reconstruction error (Sections 4.1 and 4.2).

3. **Robustness design:** The method adds a gated control path intended to suppress out-of-distribution control signals when test trajectories differ from the successful training distribution (Section 4.1, Eq. 8-9).

4. **Empirical evidence:** The authors evaluate Oat on MCP-Atlas in-domain failures and Who&When OOD failures, comparing against random, first-step, GPT-4o, and GPT-5 prompting baselines (Section 5).

The evidence supports the authors’ claim that Oat is competitive with or better than the listed prompting baselines on the reported datasets and metrics, while being much cheaper at inference after representations are available. It does not establish broad generality beyond MCP-Atlas and Who&When, nor does it prove that anomaly scores always identify causal root causes rather than correlated deviations.

## Limitations and validity risks
The authors state that Oat is an initial exploration of NDE-based successful-trajectory modeling and leave alternatives such as continuous normalizing flows, attention-based control paths, and Neural SDEs for future work (Appendix A).

Author-stated and evidence-backed risks include threshold sensitivity: one case study shows conformal prediction missed a root reasoning error because it fell below the hard threshold (Appendix H.2, Figure 23). The case studies also show Oat may miss early latent reasoning errors and instead flag downstream explicit failure verbalizations or suboptimal plans (Appendix H.2).

Analyst inference: evaluation is narrow relative to the claimed deployment setting because experiments use successful training trajectories from MCP-Atlas and two evaluation sources only. The method also depends on access to hidden representations from an LLM or proxy LLM; the paper evaluates proxy extraction but does not establish this for all deployed closed systems (Appendix G.2).

Analyst inference: reproducibility is partly supported by reported code/data availability and hyperparameters, but the retrieved text does not include exact run commands, optimizer name, or full numerical ablation tables.

## Open research questions
1. Can trajectory-level or adaptive thresholds improve localization of subtle root causes?
This remains open because conformal prediction missed a root step in a case study. A next experiment would compare global CP thresholds against per-trajectory adaptive thresholds on MCP-Atlas failure annotations.

2. Does likelihood-based scoring outperform reconstruction-error anomaly scores?
Appendix A suggests continuous normalizing flows could provide negative log-likelihood scores, but the paper does not run this comparison. A next experiment would replace reconstruction error with likelihood scores under the same train/test splits.

3. How well does Oat generalize when successful training trajectories come from the target deployment domain?
The OOD experiment trains on MCP-Atlas and tests on Who&When, but the paper does not test incremental target-domain successful data. A next experiment would add varying numbers of successful Who&When-like trajectories, if available, and measure F1/AUROC.

4. How reliable are the author-created step labels under independent annotation?
Appendix E says authors reviewed and discussed ambiguous cases, but no inter-annotator agreement is reported. A next experiment would use independent annotators and report agreement before consensus.
