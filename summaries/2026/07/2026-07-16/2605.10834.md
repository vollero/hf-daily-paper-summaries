# From Controlled to the Wild: Evaluation of Pentesting Agents for the Real-World

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-16.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2605.10834) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.10834)


## One-sentence takeaway
The paper proposes and illustrates a finding-level evaluation protocol for AI pentesting agents that emphasizes validated vulnerability discovery, ambiguity-aware matching, repeated/cumulative runs, and operational efficiency over fixed-goal benchmark success.

## Problem and motivation
The authors address how to evaluate AI pentesting agents for real-world usefulness rather than controlled benchmark completion. They argue that existing evaluations often use predefined goals such as CTF flags, remote code execution, exploit reproduction, or trajectory similarity, which can miss open-ended exploration, planning, prioritization, multiple attack surfaces, stochastic behavior, incomplete ground truth, runtime, and cost. The gap is a practical evaluation methodology that scores whether agents discover valid vulnerabilities in realistic targets rather than whether they complete a single prescribed task.

## Approach and contributions
The core method is a protocol centered on validated vulnerability discovery. Agent-reported findings are compared to structured ground-truth vulnerability entries using LLM-based semantic matching, then resolved with maximum bipartite matching so each ground-truth issue is credited at most once.

Contributions:
1. A finding-to-ground-truth scoring pipeline using structured ground truth, LLM-as-judge candidate matching, and bipartite resolution.
2. Continuous ground-truth maintenance through periodic expert review of unmatched or ambiguous findings.
3. Repeated and cumulative evaluation for stochastic agents, including per-run and multi-run aggregate scoring.
4. Efficiency tracking, including runtime, monetary cost, and discovery over time.
5. Reduced-suite selection using historical full-suite data and correlation with full-benchmark results.
6. Released expert-annotated ground truth and code, according to the abstract.

The evidence supports the authors’ claim that finding-level evaluation reveals trade-offs hidden by binary success metrics: recall, precision, duplicates, cost, runtime, and cumulative behavior can diverge across systems. It does not establish a definitive ranking of engines or models, because the authors explicitly frame the evaluated systems as representative configurations and note that these systems are highly configurable.

## Limitations and validity risks
Authors’ stated limitations: the paper proposes a protocol rather than a new benchmark suite; it does not contribute new targets; the protocol depends on sufficiently realistic targets; it does not experimentally study cross-run memory or evolving/patched targets; and it does not yet cover safety behavior such as avoiding destructive actions (§5).

Authors also identify validity risks: incomplete ground truth can distort precision/recall (§3.2); public targets may suffer training-data contamination (Appendix B.2); hosted model behavior can vary over time (Appendix B.3); and low replication limits statistical power (§3.3, Appendix F.3).

Analyst inference: the quantitative results are illustrative rather than conclusive because the experiments use three targets, three runs per condition, and configuration-specific agent setups. This inference is based on the authors’ own cautions in §4 and §5.

## Open research questions
1. How would cross-run memory change cumulative vulnerability discovery?
It remains open because the paper proposes this setting but does not experimentally study it. A next experiment would compare repeated runs with and without retained findings, tool traces, or learned target notes.

2. How robust is the protocol on larger and less public target suites?
The authors use three open-source targets and note training-data contamination risk. A next experiment would evaluate private or newly constructed multi-vulnerability targets with controlled disclosure.

3. Can safety behavior be integrated without weakening discovery metrics?
The current protocol does not evaluate destructive or unsafe actions. A next experiment would add safety event logging and score vulnerability discovery jointly with policy violations.

4. How stable is LLM-as-judge matching across judge models and ground-truth quality?
The paper runs a 50-finding sanity check, but broader robustness is not established. A next experiment would vary judge models, entry specificity, and expert adjudication sets.
