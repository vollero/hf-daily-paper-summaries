# Self in Space: Benchmarking Self-Awareness and Spatial Cognition in UAV Embodied Intelligence

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-16.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.12477) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12477)


## One-sentence takeaway
SIS-Bench tests whether video MLLMs jointly understand UAV surroundings and the UAV’s own motion state, finding strong gaps in self-awareness and reasoning, while a controlled optical-flow-based motion extension improves mostly perception and memory.

## Problem and motivation
The paper addresses UAV embodied intelligence for MLLMs, where a UAV must understand both the external environment and its own changing state. The authors argue that prior UAV benchmarks are mostly environment-centered or task-centered, leaving the UAV’s “self” state, motion history, and agent-environment interaction implicit. This matters because UAV actions change the viewpoint and therefore the visual evidence used for navigation, planning, and decision-making. The stated gap is a lack of unified evaluation for “space” and “self” across perception, memory, and reasoning levels (Sections 1, 3.1).

## Approach and contributions
The authors introduce **SIS-Bench**, a multiple-choice QA benchmark with 4,856 QA pairs from 1,646 real-world UAV videos, organized into 13 tasks across spatial cognition and self-awareness, with perception, memory, and reasoning levels (Sections 1, 3.3, 8.5).

They evaluate **26 video-capable MLLMs** plus a human baseline, reporting accuracy by task and overall, and diagnose two main patterns: models perform better on spatial cognition than self-awareness, and accuracy declines from perception to memory to reasoning (Sections 4.1, 4.2).

They also build **SIS-Motion**, a controlled motion-aware extension using optical flow, a ViT-based motion encoder, and feature fusion with visual tokens. It is trained with SIS-Motion-54K and compared against a visual-only SFT baseline (Sections 5.1, 5.2).

The evidence supports the authors’ main claim that current video MLLMs struggle with UAV self-state and higher-level temporal/spatial reasoning. It also supports a narrower claim that explicit motion cues help perception and memory. It does not establish that SIS-Motion solves high-level planning or closed-loop UAV autonomy; the authors explicitly frame it as a controlled probe rather than a flagship architecture.

## Limitations and validity risks
The authors state that SIS-Bench standardizes and reorganizes existing data and tasks rather than introducing entirely new atomic tasks. Its scope is limited to selected source datasets, multiple-choice QA, and open-loop UAV video understanding (Section 13.1).

SIS-Motion is described as a controlled motion-aware probe, not an optimized architecture. On Qwen2.5-VL-3B it improves overall accuracy from 66.4% to 69.1%, but reasoning gains are modest and inconsistent (Section 13.1).

The evaluation samples at most 32 frames per video, which is a practical cost control but may constrain long-video temporal evidence (Section 9). The authors run controls suggesting the degradation trend is not merely due to sampling, but this remains an evaluation design boundary (Section 10.1).

Analyst inference: because benchmark QA generation uses LLM assistance and model-assisted metadata for some tasks, residual annotation bias is possible despite manual correction and dual-expert review. This risk is supported by the reported 47.2% modification rate and 21.3% rejection rate during review (Section 8.4).

## Open research questions
1. Can motion-aware modeling improve reasoning, not just perception and memory?  
It remains open because SIS-Motion gains are limited and inconsistent on reasoning tasks. A plausible next experiment is to train with planning and action-prediction supervision, then compare reasoning-only performance against visual-only and motion-only ablations.

2. How well does SIS-Bench predict closed-loop UAV behavior?  
The benchmark is open-loop QA, and the authors state autonomous flying agents remain future work. A next experiment would evaluate models in a simulator or real UAV loop where predicted actions affect subsequent observations.

3. Which sensing cues best support self-in-space modeling?  
The paper tests optical-flow variants, but the authors identify depth, geometry, and temporal correspondence as future integrated sensing directions. A next experiment would compare optical flow, depth, pose/IMU-like signals, and fused geometry under the same backbone and training data.

4. Are the benchmark findings robust beyond the selected UAV sources and scenes?  
The benchmark uses AirScape, UrbanVideo-Bench, and VisDrone, with authors noting source-scope limits. A next experiment would evaluate the same task taxonomy on new real-world UAV collections with different regions, altitudes, weather, and lighting.
