# Self-Improvements in Modern Agentic Systems: A Survey

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-16.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.13104) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13104)


## One-sentence takeaway
The survey frames self-improving foundation-model agents as systems that durably update either model parameters or agent scaffolds from execution-derived signals, and organizes the fragmented literature around those update targets and signal sources.

## Problem and motivation
The authors address the fragmented terminology and scope around “self-improving” FM-based agents, where related mechanisms appear as self-correction, meta-prompting, self-play, scaffold evolution, or model fine-tuning. They argue this matters because deployed autonomous agents need controllable adaptation from experience with minimal human input. The identified gap is that prior surveys often treat foundation-model updates and agent scaffolding as separate topics and provide limited historical grounding. Evidence: Abstract; Section 1; Table 1.

## Approach and contributions
1. Historical synthesis: the paper traces self-improvement from early optimization, cybernetics, symbolic self-modification, meta-learning, Gödel machines, NAS, self-play, and modern FM agents. Evidence: Sections 2.1-2.5.

2. Unified formalization: the authors model an agent as `A_t = (theta_t, Sigma_t)`, where `theta_t` is the foundation model and `Sigma_t` is the scaffold of prompts, memory, tools, and control logic. Evidence: Section 3.1.

3. Taxonomy: the survey separates foundation-model improvement from scaffolding improvement, then subdivides by learning signal and scaffold component. Evidence: Sections 4-6; Figures 1, 3.

4. Evaluation and application coverage are claimed, but the supplied source is truncated before the full later sections; only partial evidence is available from the introduction and references. Evidence: Section 1 states planned Sections 7-9; source truncation limits verification.

## Limitations and validity risks
The supplied paper text is truncated, so this analysis cannot verify the full application, evaluation, safety, open-problem, or conclusion sections.

Author-stated risks include model collapse and forgetting from recursive synthetic-data training, diversity loss, evaluator bias, self-consistency failures when models are confidently wrong, reward hacking, capability regression under narrow RL rewards, hallucinated world-model dynamics, context-window pressure for long trajectories, memory poisoning/drift, and brittle prompt or scaffold updates. Evidence: Sections 5.1-5.3; Sections 6.1-6.2.

Analyst inference: as a survey, the paper’s claims depend heavily on the completeness and categorization of cited literature, but the supplied text does not provide enough of the later evaluation section to assess how systematically benchmarks or safety criteria are compared.

## Open research questions
1. How can self-generated training signals be validated without reinforcing model blind spots?  
Why open: the authors repeatedly note evaluator unreliability, self-consistent errors, and model collapse.  
Plausible next experiment: compare self-generated feedback loops with and without external validators across matched tasks.

2. When should an agent update parameters versus scaffolding?  
Why open: the taxonomy distinguishes the two pathways but does not provide a decision rule in the supplied text.  
Plausible next experiment: evaluate matched agents that improve via LoRA/fine-tuning, prompt/memory updates, or hybrid updates under equal compute budgets.

3. How can memory systems remain useful over long horizons without drift or contamination?  
Why open: memory sections identify retrieval noise, stale heuristics, privacy leakage, and silent corruption.  
Plausible next experiment: run long-horizon agent tasks with controlled memory poisoning and measure recovery under different CRUD policies.

4. How should self-improving agents be evaluated beyond single endpoint performance?  
Why open: the paper says evaluation trajectories and protocols matter, but the full evaluation section is truncated.  
Plausible next experiment: benchmark improvement curves, rollback frequency, regression, and safety violations over repeated update cycles.
