# Ring-Zero: Scaling Zero RL to a Trillion Parameters for Emergent Reasoning

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-16.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.12395) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12395)


## One-sentence takeaway
Ring-Zero claims that a minimally modified zero-RL pipeline can stably train a 1T-parameter base LLM into a strong mathematical reasoner, with scaling improving sample efficiency, performance ceilings, and emergent reasoning behaviors.

## Problem and motivation
The paper addresses how reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards, without human-annotated CoT data, behaves at trillion-parameter scale. The authors argue that prior zero-RL studies mostly use smaller models because of compute constraints, leaving large-scale training dynamics and emergent capabilities underexplored. They also identify practical quality problems in naive zero RL: unreadable traces, redundant token growth, and fixed-depth reasoning that does not adapt to problem difficulty (Introduction).

## Approach and contributions
The core approach is a four-phase self-iteration pipeline for zero RL from pretrained base models: first-stage RL with clipped importance-sampling policy gradient, KL regularization, and token-level loss; self-distillation to shorten and stabilize traces; second-stage RL with sample-level loss normalization; and third-stage tier-based RL for adaptive reasoning depth (Section 3.1, Figure 1).

Contributions:
1. A stable 1T-scale zero-RL training recipe using clipped importance sampling, training-inference ratio correction, mixed-precision control, and context-parallelism optimizations (Sections 3.1.1, 3.2).
2. A CoT quality evaluation framework covering comprehensibility, reproducibility, and efficiency, beyond final-answer accuracy (Section 2).
3. Empirical evidence that the 1T model outperforms a 104B model in sample efficiency and benchmark performance, and that advanced behaviors emerge without explicit supervision (Sections 4.2, 5.1, 6).

The evidence supports the authors’ claim that their 1T zero-RL pipeline can reach strong math benchmark performance and that specific stabilization choices matter. It does not fully establish generality beyond mathematical reasoning, because the reported task evaluation is math-centered. It also does not independently verify whether the emergent behaviors are beneficial in non-math settings.

## Limitations and validity risks
Authors state several limits: the High tier is limited by a lack of high-quality ultra-long reasoning data, multi-tier training introduces negative transfer, training is restricted to a 64k context window because of hardware constraints, and zero RL is bounded by pretrained knowledge rather than inventing missing concepts (Sections 4.2, 5.6).

Analyst inference: reproducibility is constrained because the paper reports large-scale infrastructure requirements, including 320 H200 GPUs, and does not provide enough supplied text to verify released model weights, datasets, or code. Another risk is that most detailed ablations are on the 104B flash model, not the 1T model, due to compute constraints (Section 4.4). The CoT comprehensibility evaluation depends on LLM-as-judge comparisons, so its validity depends on judge reliability and prompt design; Appendix B describes randomization but the supplied text does not give numerical win rates.

## Open research questions
1. Can the same zero-RL scaling behavior transfer beyond mathematical benchmarks? This remains open because evaluation is centered on AIME, HMMT, and IMOAnswerBench. A next experiment would apply the pipeline to code, tool-use, or scientific reasoning tasks with comparable pass@k and trace-quality metrics.

2. Can reasoning quality and token efficiency be optimized in one RL objective? The authors call their self-distillation plus sample-level loss a heuristic workaround. A next experiment would compare unified reward objectives that jointly score correctness, brevity, and trace quality against the current staged pipeline.

3. How much do the emergent behaviors improve actual task success? The paper documents anthropomorphism, structured formatting, self-verification, parallel reasoning, and context anxiety, but causal contribution is unclear. A next experiment would ablate or reward-suppress individual behaviors and measure benchmark and CoT-quality changes.

4. Does extending context beyond 64k improve capability as claimed? The authors state this belief but report a 64k training constraint. A next experiment would train matched models at larger context windows and compare pass@1, pass@1024, and token efficiency.
