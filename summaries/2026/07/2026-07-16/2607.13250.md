# AffectFlow-DINO: Uncertainty-Aware Multi-Task Affect Estimation via Conditional Rectified Flow

> [← Daily summary](../2026-07-16.md) · [Relationship graph](relationships.md) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/papers/2607.13250) · [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13250)


## One-sentence takeaway
AffectFlow-DINO adds a conditional rectified-flow label-space head to a DINOv3 facial affect model, improving uncertainty-aware multi-task affect estimation on s-Aff-Wild2 validation, with the strongest reported gains coming from backbone fine-tuning and post-hoc calibration.

## Problem and motivation
The paper addresses frame-level facial affect estimation in the ABAW 2026 multi-task setting: continuous valence-arousal regression, eight-class expression classification, and twelve Action Unit detection. The authors argue this matters because in-the-wild facial behavior is ambiguous and partially annotated, while s-Aff-Wild2 has severe class imbalance and incomplete joint supervision. They identify a gap in deterministic point-estimate models: such models collapse multiple plausible affect interpretations into one output and do not represent uncertainty across the joint VA/EXPR/AU space.

## Approach and contributions
The method uses a DINOv3 ViT-S/16 visual backbone, deterministic task heads, and a conditional rectified-flow head over a 22-dimensional joint affect vector. The flow head learns a conditional distribution \(p(y \mid x)\) by transporting Gaussian noise to affect labels and averages Monte Carlo samples at inference.

Main contributions claimed by the authors:

1. A masked rectified-flow multi-task objective that handles partially available VA, expression, and AU labels in a shared 22-dimensional target space.
2. A systematic ablation study over flow weight, decoding, fine-tuning, imbalance remedies, inference efficiency, and calibration strategies.
3. Post-hoc per-AU and per-expression-class calibration that improves rare-class F1 and raises the best validation composite score to \(P_{MTL}=1.177\).

The evidence supports the authors’ claim that rectified-flow training can add useful distributional structure, especially before backbone fine-tuning and for VA estimation. It also supports the practical value of calibration under severe imbalance. It does not establish hidden-test performance, superiority to the cited 7th ABAW leaderboard, or that uncertainty estimates are calibrated in a probabilistic sense beyond the task-score improvements reported.

## Limitations and validity risks
Author-stated limitations and caveats include validation-only reporting on the local s-Aff-Wild2 split, non-comparability with hidden-test SOTA scores from earlier ABAW settings, and the absence of temporal aggregation. The authors also state that the masked-flow objective relies on a missingness-independence assumption that may not fully hold.

Analyst inference: post-hoc calibration is tuned on the validation set, so its reported gains may overestimate generalization without a separate test set. The supplied text also does not provide the full numerical contents of several referenced main tables, limiting independent verification of some summarized claims. Another risk is that after backbone fine-tuning, deterministic decoding continues to outperform flow decoding, so the strongest final result depends more on deterministic heads plus calibration than on using flow sampling at inference.

## Open research questions
1. Can the calibrated gains generalize to a held-out hidden test set? This remains open because all reported results are on the local validation split. A next experiment would evaluate the calibrated and uncalibrated checkpoints on an untouched test set.

2. Can flow decoding be made competitive after backbone fine-tuning? The paper reports that deterministic decoding still wins in fine-tuned variants. A next experiment would jointly tune flow weight, sampling temperature, and schedule during end-to-end training.

3. Would temporal context close the expression gap? The authors leave temporal aggregation for future work, and expression prediction remains difficult for rare classes. A next experiment would add video-level temporal modeling while keeping the same VA/EXPR/AU metrics.

4. Are the uncertainty estimates themselves well calibrated? The paper uses flow sampling and threshold calibration but does not report uncertainty calibration metrics. A next experiment would measure uncertainty quality against ambiguity, error rates, or calibration curves.
